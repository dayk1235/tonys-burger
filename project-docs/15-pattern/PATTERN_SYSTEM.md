## CONSTITUTIONAL STATEMENT

Patterns are the second cognitive layer of Restaurant OS.

Observation captures reality. Pattern discovers structure inside reality. Knowledge validates that structure. Reasoning uses validated structure.

A Pattern is the bridge between Observation and Knowledge.

Without Patterns, Observations remain isolated facts — a collection of events with no connection, no meaning, no predictive power. Without Patterns, Knowledge has no foundation — it would be built on isolated events rather than recurring structures.

Patterns are discovered. They are never created, never invented, never assumed. The system does not decide what Patterns exist — it discovers the Patterns that are already present in the Observations.

Nothing in Restaurant OS reaches Knowledge without first passing through Pattern.

## Part 1 — Philosophy of Pattern

### 1.1 Why Pattern Exists

Pattern exists because the business world is not random. Events recur. Behaviors repeat. Relationships persist. Causes produce effects. If every day were entirely novel — unrelated to every other day — Pattern would not exist, and cognition would be impossible.

But the business world is not random. Tuesday lunch traffic follows a different rhythm than Friday dinner. Food cost responds to supplier prices. Customer satisfaction correlates with service speed. These are Patterns — recurring structures in the stream of Observations.

Pattern exists to capture these structures. Without Pattern, the system would experience every Observation as if it were the first of its kind. It would never learn, never predict, never improve.

Pattern makes intelligence possible by transforming a sequence of isolated facts into an understanding of how the business works.

### 1.2 Pattern as Recurring Structure

A Pattern is a recurring structure in the Observation stream. It is:

- **Recurring** — The same relationship appears across multiple Observations, not just once
- **Structural** — It describes a relationship between Observations, not just the Observations themselves
- **Observable** — It is present in the data, waiting to be discovered, not imposed by the system

A Pattern answers: "What repeats?" Not "What happened?" (that is Observation). Not "Why does it happen?" (that is Knowledge). Not "What should I do?" (that is Recommendation).

"What repeats" is the fundamental Pattern question.

Examples:
- "Tuesday lunch covers are consistently 15-20% lower than Friday lunch covers" — Temporal Pattern
- "When the weather is hot, frozen dessert sales increase 30-40%" — Environmental Pattern
- "Customers who order a second drink are 3x more likely to order dessert" — Behavioral Pattern

### 1.3 Pattern vs. Observation

| Dimension | Observation | Pattern |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Definition** | A single verified fact | A recurring structure across multiple facts |
| **Granularity** | Atomic | Composite |
| **Temporality** | Point in time | Across time |
| **Discovery** | Captured (from stimulus) | Discovered (from Observations) |
| **Certainty** | Verified (fact did occur) | Probabilistic (structure may change) |
| **Example** | "Tuesday covers: 142" | "Tuesday covers are 15-20% lower than Friday" |
| **Lifecycle** | Permanent once captured | Evolves as new Observations arrive |

Observation answers "what happened." Pattern answers "what tends to happen."

Patterns depend on Observations. Observations do not depend on Patterns. An Observation is true regardless of whether it fits a Pattern. A Pattern is true only to the extent that its supporting Observations continue to support it.

### 1.4 Pattern vs. Knowledge

| Dimension | Pattern | Knowledge |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Definition** | A recurring structure in Observations | Validated understanding of why the structure exists |
| **Validation** | Supported by Observations | Validated through learning and outcome testing |
| **Confidence** | May be tentative or strong | High (passed validation gates) |
| **Understanding** | "What repeats" | "Why it repeats and what it means" |
| **Permanence** | May evolve or be deprecated | Stable once validated |
| **Dependency** | Depends on Observations | Depends on validated Patterns |

A Pattern says "Tuesday lunch is consistently slower." Knowledge says "Tuesday lunch is slower because our business customer base has a consistent meeting schedule, as confirmed by 12 months of data and validated by the Owner's observation."

Knowledge is Pattern plus understanding, validation, and integration. A Pattern becomes Knowledge when the system understands why it exists and has confirmed that understanding through the Learning Layer.

### 1.5 Pattern vs. Signal

| Dimension | Signal | Pattern |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Definition** | An Observation carrying predictive information | A recurring structure across Observations |
| **Scope** | Single Observation | Multiple Observations across time |
| **Maturity** | Raw material for pattern detection | The detected structure itself |
| **Purpose** | Flag potential significance | Describe established recurrence |
| **Relationship** | Signals are inputs to Pattern detection | Patterns are the output of Signal analysis |

A Signal is an Observation that has caught the system's attention as potentially meaningful. A Pattern is the confirmed structure that the Signal hinted at. A Signal says "this Tuesday is different." A Pattern says "Tuesdays follow a different rhythm, and this Tuesday fits within expected variance."

### 1.6 Pattern vs. Correlation

| Dimension | Correlation | Pattern |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Definition** | A statistical relationship between variables | A recurring structure in the business |
| **Scope** | Purely numeric or categorical | Includes temporal, behavioral, operational context |
| **Depth** | Surface-level relationship | May include causal understanding |
| **Certainty** | Mathematical (may be spurious) | Contextual (understood within business reality) |
| **Communication** | "X correlates with Y (r=0.7)" | "When X happens, Y tends to follow, and here is why it matters" |

Correlation is a type of Pattern, but not all Patterns are correlations. A Pattern may be temporal ("Tuesdays are slower"), behavioral ("regulars order the special"), or operational ("food cost rises in December"). These are not statistical correlations — they are business structures.

A Pattern carries business meaning. A correlation carries mathematical meaning. A Pattern always includes the question "Why does this matter for the business?" Correlation alone does not.

### 1.7 Pattern vs. Rule

| Dimension | Pattern | Rule |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Definition** | A discovered recurring structure | An imposed or derived principle |
| **Origin** | Emerges from Observations | Defined by the system or Owner |
| **Flexibility** | Probabilistic (may have exceptions) | Deterministic (applies always) |
| **Evolution** | Changes as new Observations arrive | Changed only through explicit revision |
| **Certainty** | Always carries confidence | Treated as true until contradicted |
| **Example** | "Tuesday lunch tends to be slower" | "The restaurant opens at 11:00 AM every day" |

Rules exist in the Business Knowledge Graph as established facts about how the business operates. Patterns are discovered structures in the Observation stream. A Rule may be confirmed by Pattern evidence, and a Pattern may reveal a Rule that was not previously known.

### 1.8 Pattern vs. Trend

| Dimension | Pattern | Trend |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| **Definition** | A recurring structure | A directional movement over time |
| **Temporality** | Cyclic or periodic | Monotonic (up, down, flat) |
| **Examples** | "Seasonal cycles," "Day-of-week rhythms" | "Revenue growing 3% month-over-month" |
| **Relationship** | Trend may be a component of a larger Pattern | Pattern may contain multiple trends |

A Trend is a value object — it describes direction and magnitude. A Pattern may contain Trends (e.g., "Tuesday lunch is trending downward within the larger pattern of day-of-week rhythms"). A Trend without a Pattern is an isolated observation. A Pattern with Trends is a rich structure that describes both cycles and directional changes.

### 1.9 Pattern vs. Insight

| Dimension | Pattern | Insight |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Definition** | A recurring structure in Observations | A meaningful understanding derived from Patterns |
| **Audience** | Internal cognitive artifact | Designed for Owner understanding |
| **Depth** | Structural description | Contextual interpretation |
| **Example** | "Average check increases 12% on Saturday nights" | "Saturday night customers are celebrating — they order more drinks, more desserts, and are less price-sensitive" |

Patterns are internal to the system. Insights are communications to the Owner. An Insight is a Pattern placed in business context with narrative significance. Multiple Patterns may combine into a single Insight.

### 1.10 Pattern vs. Prediction

| Dimension | Pattern | Prediction |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| **Definition** | A recurring structure in Observations | A forecast of a future Observation |
| **Direction** | Grounded in past Observations | Projects into the future |
| **Dependency** | Self-contained (describes past) | Depends on Pattern validity |
| **Role** | Input to Reasoning | Output of Reasoning |

Prediction depends on Pattern. Without Pattern, the system has no basis for predicting the future. "Tuesday lunch will be slower than Friday lunch" is a Prediction derived from the Pattern "Tuesday lunch is consistently slower."

A Pattern says "this is what has happened." A Prediction says "based on this Pattern, this is what will happen." Every Prediction must trace to at least one Pattern.

### 1.11 Why Restaurant OS Never Invents Patterns

Patterns are discovered, not invented. The system does not decide what Patterns exist — the business's behavior reveals them.

An invented Pattern is a false structure that the system imposes on random variation. It maps noise and calls it meaning. This violates Constitution Article 3 (Never Pretends To Know) and Article 5 (Knowledge Is Earned).

The system's role is to listen to the business and detect the structures that are genuinely present. If no Pattern is found, the system must say "I do not see a Pattern yet" — not create one from nothing.

**Discovery rules:**
1. A Pattern requires supporting Observations. No Observations → No Pattern.
2. A Pattern requires recurrence. A single occurrence is an event, not a Pattern.
3. A Pattern requires consistency. The relationship must hold across multiple instances.
4. A Pattern must be explainable in business terms. Statistical artifacts are not Patterns.
5. A Pattern's confidence reflects its evidentiary foundation. Low evidence → low confidence.

## Part 2 — Pattern Lifecycle

### 2.1 Lifecycle Model

A Pattern begins as a hint in the Observation stream and evolves through stages of validation, confirmation, and eventual deprecation. This lifecycle is the journey of a recurring structure from potential signal to established knowledge evidence.

```
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  POTENTIAL            │
                  │  PATTERN              │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Multiple Observations
                             │ suggest recurrence
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  CANDIDATE            │
                  │  PATTERN              │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Sufficient evidence
                             │ for formal investigation
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  EMERGING             │
                  │  PATTERN              │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Consistency threshold
                             │ reached
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  SUPPORTED            │
                  │  PATTERN              │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Validation gates
                             │ passed
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  VALIDATED            │
                  │  PATTERN              │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Learning Layer
                             │ confirms understanding
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  KNOWLEDGE            │
                  │  CANDIDATE            │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Promoted to
                             │ Knowledge
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  KNOWLEDGE            │
                  │  EVIDENCE             │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Pattern validity
                             │ degrades
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  DEPRECATED           │
                  │  PATTERN              │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Archived with
                             │ deprecation record
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  HISTORICAL           │
                  │  PATTERN              │
                  └──────────────────────┘
```

### 2.2 Stage Definitions

#### Stage 1 — POTENTIAL PATTERN

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | A hint of recurring structure has been detected in the Observation stream. Not yet a Pattern — an area of interest. |
| **Entry** | The Pattern Detection Service identifies a possible recurring relationship between two or more Observations. |
| **Exit** | Multiple supporting Observations accumulate → Candidate. Insufficient evidence or contradictory evidence → dismissed. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Candidate (sufficient Observations suggest genuine recurrence); Dismissed (insufficient or contradictory evidence, recorded as dismissed) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Supported (cannot skip investigation); → Validated; → Knowledge |
| **Failure Modes** | Premature detection (noise interpreted as potential Pattern); detection blindness (genuine Pattern not identified); excessive Potential Patterns (too many areas of interest, system overwhelmed) |

A Potential Pattern is not a Pattern. It is the system's suspicion that a Pattern may exist. It lives in the intersection of Pattern Memory and Working Memory — held as long as it remains viable, dismissed if evidence does not accumulate.

#### Stage 2 — CANDIDATE PATTERN

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The system has identified a specific recurring relationship supported by multiple Observations. The Pattern hypothesis is formalized with a description, scope, and initial confidence. |
| **Entry** | Sufficient Observations have accumulated to suggest a genuine recurrence, not random variation. |
| **Exit** | Further evidence confirms the relationship → Emerging. Contradictory evidence refutes it → Deprecated (short cycle). |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Emerging (evidence continues to support); → Deprecated (contradictory evidence) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Validated (cannot skip emerging stage); → Knowledge |
| **Failure Modes** | False candidate (noise that appears patterned); premature candidate (insufficient Observations promoted too early); candidate stall (neither confirming nor disconfirming evidence arrives) |

A Candidate Pattern has a formal identity, description, and set of supporting Observations. It is stored in Pattern Memory and actively monitored. This is the first stage at which the system can reference the Pattern as a discrete cognitive artifact.

#### Stage 3 — EMERGING PATTERN

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Pattern is showing consistent evidence but has not yet reached full validation thresholds. The system is actively gathering evidence, monitoring stability, and assessing strength. |
| **Entry** | Pattern consistency exceeds the baseline detection threshold. |
| **Exit** | Reaches Supported threshold → Supported. Loses consistency → falls back to Candidate or Deprecated. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Supported (consistency threshold reached); → Candidate (evidence weakens); → Deprecated (contradictory evidence outweighs supporting) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Validated (cannot skip supported stage); → Knowledge Evidence |
| **Failure Modes** | Emerging stagnation (Pattern neither strengthens nor weakens over extended period); false emergence (consistency appears but is coincidental); emergence reversal (supporting evidence that appeared consistent suddenly diverges) |

An Emerging Pattern is the system's "watching closely" state. The Pattern is interesting enough to track but not reliable enough to use in high-confidence reasoning. Emerging Patterns may inform low-confidence predictions and tentative recommendations.

#### Stage 4 — SUPPORTED PATTERN

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Pattern has sufficient supporting evidence to be considered a reliable structure. It can be used in reasoning, predictions, and recommendations with moderate to high confidence. |
| **Entry** | Evidence consistency and volume exceed the supported threshold. |
| **Exit** | Passes validation gates → Validated. New evidence weakens or contradicts → back to Emerging or Deprecated. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Validated (validation gates passed); → Emerging (evidence weakens but does not fully contradict); → Deprecated (evidence fully contradicts) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Candidate (cannot regress two stages); → Knowledge Evidence (must pass through Validated and Knowledge Candidate first) |
| **Failure Modes** | False confidence (Pattern appears strong but is based on biased sampling); support illusion (supporting Observations are not independent); validation gate failure (Pattern fails validation unexpectedly) |

A Supported Pattern is the system's workhorse. Most Patterns used in day-to-day reasoning are at this stage. They are reliable enough to use but not yet validated enough to become Knowledge.

#### Stage 5 — VALIDATED PATTERN

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Pattern has passed the system's validation gates: statistical significance, cross-validation, temporal stability, causal plausibility, and business meaning. |
| **Entry** | All validation gates passed. The Pattern is confirmed as genuine. |
| **Exit** | Learning Layer evaluates whether to promote to Knowledge → Knowledge Candidate. Pattern remains Validated even if not promoted. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Knowledge Candidate (selected for Knowledge promotion); → Supported (if re-evaluation weakens confidence but does not invalidate); → Deprecated (if re-evaluation finds fundamental flaw) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Emerging (validated Pattern cannot regress to early stages); → Candidate |
| **Failure Modes** | Validation false positive (Pattern passes gates but is still spurious); validation false negative (genuine Pattern fails gates due to unusual structure); over-validation (Pattern requires more validation than its importance justifies) |

A Validated Pattern is the system's most reliable understanding of business structure. It has survived rigorous scrutiny. The system can use Validated Patterns for high-confidence predictions, recommendations, and narratives.

#### Stage 6 — KNOWLEDGE CANDIDATE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Pattern is being evaluated for promotion to Knowledge. The Learning Layer assesses whether the Pattern's structure is understood well enough to become permanent Knowledge. |
| **Entry** | The Learning Layer selects a Validated Pattern as potentially worth promoting to Knowledge. |
| **Exit** | Learning Layer confirms understanding → Knowledge Evidence. Learning Layer determines understanding is insufficient → remains Validated. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Knowledge Evidence (confirmed and promoted); → Validated (returned to validated state) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Supported (cannot regress while under Knowledge consideration); → Deprecated |
| **Failure Modes** | Premature candidacy (Pattern promoted to Knowledge Candidate before understanding is sufficient); missed candidacy (Pattern should be Knowledge but is never evaluated); candidacy stall (Pattern remains in Knowledge Candidate state indefinitely) |

A Knowledge Candidate is a Pattern that has earned the right to be considered as permanent Knowledge. Not all Patterns should become Knowledge — only those that are stable, understood, and valuable.

#### Stage 7 — KNOWLEDGE EVIDENCE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Pattern has been promoted to Knowledge. It now serves as supporting evidence for a Knowledge statement. The Pattern itself remains in Pattern Memory, but it has an additional role as Knowledge Evidence. |
| **Entry** | Learning Layer confirms understanding and promotes the Pattern and its supporting Observations to Knowledge. |
| **Exit** | Permanent. The Pattern serves as Knowledge Evidence for the life of the Knowledge. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Deprecated (if the Knowledge it supports is deprecated and the Pattern no longer holds independently); → Historical (if the Pattern transitions to archival) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Any earlier lifecycle stage |
| **Failure Modes** | False Knowledge (Knowledge is incorrect, contaminating the Pattern's role as evidence); evidence disconnect (Knowledge references a Pattern that has drifted); Knowledge dependency trap (Pattern cannot be deprecated because Knowledge depends on it) |

Knowledge Evidence is the highest cognitive role a Pattern can achieve. A Pattern that reaches this stage has contributed to the system's permanent, validated understanding of the business.

#### Stage 8 — DEPRECATED PATTERN

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Pattern is no longer valid. New Observations contradict it, the business has changed, or the Pattern was found to be spurious. |
| **Entry** | Evidence no longer supports the Pattern, or the system determines the Pattern was false. |
| **Exit** | Archived to Historical Pattern Memory → Historical. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Historical (archived with full deprecation record) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Any active stage. A deprecated Pattern cannot be reactivated without a new discovery cycle. Exception: Historical Patterns may be re-evaluated (see Stage 9). |
| **Failure Modes** | Premature deprecation (Pattern deprecated while still valid due to anomalous evidence); delayed deprecation (Pattern persists after it has ceased to be valid, contaminating reasoning); deprecation without learning (Pattern deprecated but no knowledge extracted from its failure) |

Deprecation is not failure — it is learning. A deprecated Pattern teaches the system that its understanding was incomplete or that the business has changed. Every deprecation should produce a Learning Observation.

#### Stage 9 — HISTORICAL PATTERN

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Pattern has been archived. It remains in Historical Pattern Memory permanently with its full lifecycle record, supporting Observations, and deprecation reason. |
| **Entry** | Deprecated Pattern is archived. |
| **Exit** | Permanent. May be re-evaluated if new Observations suggest the Pattern is re-emerging. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Potential (rare — new Observations suggest the same structure is re-emerging, starting a new discovery cycle) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Any active lifecycle stage without a new discovery cycle |
| **Failure Modes** | Historical loss (Pattern archived without sufficient indexing, unreachable for re-evaluation); re-emergence blindness (Pattern re-emerges but system does not recognize it as historical) |

Historical Patterns are the system's memory of what it used to know. They enable the system to recognize when old Patterns re-emerge and to avoid repeating past errors.

### 2.3 Lifecycle Principles

1. **Forward motion is the default.** Patterns evolve toward validation and Knowledge. Regression requires explicit contradictory evidence.
2. **Stages are not skipped.** A Pattern cannot go from Candidate to Validated without passing through Emerging and Supported.
3. **Deprecation preserves history.** Every deprecated Pattern retains its full lifecycle record.
4. **Re-emergence is possible.** A Historical Pattern may be re-discovered if the same structure re-emerges in the Observation stream.
5. **Not every Pattern becomes Knowledge.** Most Patterns remain Supported — useful for reasoning but not important enough for permanent Knowledge status.

## Part 3 — Pattern Anatomy

### 3.1 Conceptual Fields

Every Pattern has the following conceptual fields. Each field exists for a specific cognitive reason.

#### Identity

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The unique identity of the Pattern. Distinguishes this Pattern from all others. |
| **Why it exists** | Without identity, Patterns cannot be referenced, related, or traced. Identity enables every cognitive operation involving the Pattern. |
| **Cognitive role** | Provides a fixed reference point for all relationships, reasoning, and memory. |

#### Description

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | A natural-language description of the recurring structure the Pattern captures. |
| **Why it exists** | The description is what makes the Pattern meaningful to cognition. It answers "what repeats and why does it matter?" |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables narrative composition, explanation generation, and human-readable reasoning. |

#### Pattern Type

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The primary category of the Pattern (see Part 4 — Pattern Classification). |
| **Why it exists** | Type determines how the Pattern is processed, what it contributes to, and how it relates to other Patterns. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables type-specific processing, appropriate reasoning strategy selection, and relationship inference. |

#### Business Context

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The Business and specific Restaurant to which this Pattern belongs. |
| **Why it exists** | Patterns are business-specific. A Pattern discovered in one business may not apply to another. |
| **Cognitive role** | Scopes all Pattern-based reasoning to the relevant business entity. |

#### Temporal Scope

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The temporal range across which the Pattern has been observed: start time, end time (or ongoing), duration, periodicity. |
| **Why it exists** | Patterns exist within time. A Pattern that held for 12 months may change. Temporal scope tells the system how long the Pattern has been valid. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables confidence assessment (longer duration → higher confidence), temporal reasoning, and staleness detection. |

#### Behavior Scope

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The behavioral dimension the Pattern captures: customer behavior, employee behavior, Owner behavior, system behavior. |
| **Why it exists** | Behavior Patterns reveal intent and preference. They are the most predictive class of Patterns. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables behavioral prediction, preference inference, and behavioral recommendation. |

#### Operational Scope

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The operational conditions under which the Pattern holds: service period, menu state, staffing level, promotion status. |
| **Why it exists** | Patterns are often conditional on operational context. A Pattern that holds during normal operations may not hold during a promotion. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables context-constrained reasoning, exception detection, and conditional prediction. |

#### Environmental Scope

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The environmental conditions under which the Pattern holds: weather, season, holidays, local events, economic conditions. |
| **Why it exists** | External conditions drive business Patterns. A Pattern that holds in summer may not hold in winter. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables environmental correlation, seasonal adjustment, and context-aware reasoning. |

#### Supporting Observations

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | References to the Observations that support this Pattern. Every Pattern must trace to at least one Observation. |
| **Why it exists** | Observations are the evidence that the Pattern is real. Without supporting Observations, a Pattern is speculation. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables evidence tracing, confidence assessment, validation, and contradiction resolution. |

#### Supporting Signals

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | References to the Signals that contributed to the Pattern's discovery. |
| **Why it exists** | Signals are the trail that led to the Pattern. Retaining Signal references enables the system to understand how Patterns were discovered. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables discovery audit, signal effectiveness evaluation, and attention calibration learning. |

#### Supporting Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | A composite view of all evidence supporting the Pattern: Observations that directly confirm it, related Patterns that are consistent with it, and external validation. |
| **Why it exists** | Evidence is stronger than any single Observation. The cumulative weight of multiple evidence types determines Pattern strength. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables evidence-weighted reasoning, confidence calculation, and evidence-based recommendation. |

#### Confidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The system's assessment of how likely it is that the Pattern reflects a genuine recurring structure (not random variation). |
| **Why it exists** | Confidence enables the system to calibrate how heavily to weight the Pattern in reasoning. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables uncertainty-aware reasoning, appropriate recommendation certainty, and communication tone calibration. |

#### Strength

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | A measure of how consistently the Pattern holds. A strong Pattern has high consistency across Observations. A weak Pattern has high variance. |
| **Why it exists** | Strength is distinct from confidence. A Pattern may have high confidence (genuine structure) but low strength (high variance). Both matter. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables prediction range estimation (strong Pattern → tight prediction range), risk assessment, and exception frequency estimation. |

#### Novelty

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | How new or unexpected the Pattern is relative to the system's existing knowledge. |
| **Why it exists** | Novel Patterns deserve more attention. They may signal a change in the business or a gap in the system's understanding. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables attention prioritization, learning opportunity identification, and discovery tracking. |

#### Persistence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | How long the Pattern has been observed. A Pattern observed for 12 months is more persistent than one observed for 2 weeks. |
| **Why it exists** | Persistence is a trust signal. Patterns that have persisted through multiple business cycles are more reliable. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables confidence calibration, stability assessment, and deprecation resistance. |

#### Recurrence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The frequency with which the Pattern manifests. Daily, weekly, seasonal, annual, or irregular. |
| **Why it exists** | Recurrence determines how often the Pattern can be observed and thus how quickly its confidence can be updated. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables temporal reasoning, prediction scheduling, and monitoring frequency determination. |

#### Importance

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The Pattern's significance to business outcomes. How much does this Pattern matter? |
| **Why it exists** | Not all Patterns are equally important. Importance determines cognitive resource allocation. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables attention prioritization, narrative selection, recommendation urgency, and resource allocation. |

#### Predictive Value

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | How well the Pattern enables prediction of future Observations. |
| **Why it exists** | The primary value of a Pattern is its ability to reduce uncertainty about the future. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables prediction generation, prediction confidence estimation, and prediction-based recommendation. |

#### Relationships

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Links from this Pattern to Observations, other Patterns, Knowledge, Recommendations, Narratives, and Decisions. |
| **Why it exists** | A Pattern's cognitive power comes from its position in the relationship network. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables relational reasoning, influence tracing, evidence chains, and knowledge graph construction. |

#### Learning Relevance

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether this Pattern has contributed to Learning, and how. |
| **Why it exists** | Patterns that generate learning drive the system's evolution. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables learning tracing, knowledge evolution tracking, and meta-cognitive assessment. |

#### Decision Relevance

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether this Pattern has informed a Recommendation or Decision, and how directly. |
| **Why it exists** | Patterns that influence decisions are operationally significant. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables decision tracing, evidence-to-decision mapping, and Pattern impact assessment. |

#### Narrative Relevance

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether this Pattern has contributed to a Narrative, and how central it was. |
| **Why it exists** | Patterns that inform Narratives communicate understanding to the Owner. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables narrative tracing, Pattern-to-story mapping, and communication impact assessment. |

#### Evolution History

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The complete lifecycle record of the Pattern: when discovered, each stage transition, confidence changes, strength changes, re-evaluations. |
| **Why it exists** | Evolution history enables the system to understand how its understanding has changed. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables learning audit, confidence trajectory analysis, pattern stability assessment, and meta-cognition. |

#### Quality Profile

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The Pattern's quality assessment across all quality dimensions (see Part 5 — Pattern Quality). |
| **Why it exists** | Quality determines how much the system can trust the Pattern in reasoning. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables quality-weighted reasoning, appropriate confidence assignment, and quality-based processing decisions. |

### 3.2 Field Principles

1. **Every field has a cognitive purpose.** No field exists for technical convenience.
2. **Fields are not implementation columns.** They are conceptual dimensions of Pattern understanding.
3. **Core fields are present at creation** (Identity, Description, Pattern Type, Business Context, Supporting Observations, Confidence). Context and quality fields fill as the Pattern evolves.
4. **Fields reflect the Pattern's lifecycle stage.** A Candidate Pattern has minimal fields. A Validated Pattern has the full set.
5. **Fields do not change after the Pattern is deprecated.** The final field state at deprecation is frozen.

## Part 4 — Pattern Classification

### 4.1 Classification Model

Every Pattern belongs to exactly one primary category and may belong to zero or more secondary categories. Classification determines processing priority, reasoning strategy, and relationship to other cognitive artifacts.

### 4.2 Categories

#### Operational Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Patterns in business operations: service speed, table turns, order accuracy, kitchen throughput, opening/closing routines. |
| **Examples** | "Lunch service takes 12 minutes longer on average than dinner service." "Table turns per server decrease after the 4th table." |
| **Purpose** | Enable operational optimization, bottleneck detection, efficiency improvement. |
| **Processing priority** | Continuous (every service period produces data that reinforces or challenges operational Patterns). |

#### Behavioral Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Patterns in human behavior: customer ordering habits, employee performance cycles, Owner decision timing. |
| **Examples** | "Regular customers order the special 60% of the time." "Server accuracy declines 15% after the 6th hour of a shift." |
| **Purpose** | Enable behavioral prediction, preference inference, staffing optimization, personalized recommendations. |
| **Processing priority** | High (behavioral Patterns are highly predictive). |

#### Financial Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Patterns in financial performance and structure: revenue cycles, cost relationships, margin dynamics, pricing effects. |
| **Examples** | "Food cost peaks in December due to holiday supplier surcharges." "Average check increases 8% on weekends." |
| **Purpose** | Enable financial forecasting, cost control, margin optimization, pricing strategy. |
| **Processing priority** | Continuous (every financial period reinforces or challenges financial Patterns). |

#### Customer Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Patterns in customer behavior and characteristics: segment behavior, visit frequency, loyalty dynamics, satisfaction triggers. |
| **Examples** | "VIP customers visit 3x per week on average." "Customers who receive a comped dessert return within 14 days at a 40% higher rate." |
| **Purpose** | Enable customer understanding, retention strategies, personalization, segment management. |
| **Processing priority** | Per-customer (aggregated for segment Patterns, individual for per-customer Patterns). |

#### Temporal Patterns

| **Processing priority** | Event-driven (triggered by supply events). |

#### Risk Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|

| **Examples** | "Food cost over 33% for 3 consecutive weeks typically leads to a margin crisis within 2 months." "Employee turnover above 30% annually correlates with 15% lower customer satisfaction scores." |
| **Purpose** | Enable early warning, preventative action, risk mitigation, strategic planning. |

| Property | Definition |

| **Purpose** | Enable opportunity capture, revenue growth, value creation, competitive advantage. |

#### Growth Patterns

| Property | Definition |

| **Examples** | "New customer acquisition accelerates 3 months after a menu refresh." "Positive online reviews correlate with 12% cover growth the following week." |
| **Purpose** | Enable growth strategy, marketing optimization, expansion planning. |
| **Processing priority** | Medium (growth Patterns influence strategic recommendations). |

#### Failure Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Patterns that precede or characterize failure: error chains, breakdown sequences, failure precursors, quality degradation cycles. |
| **Examples** | "Equipment failures are 3x more likely in the 2 weeks following a missed maintenance cycle." "Order errors increase 25% when the kitchen is staffed with >30% new hires." |

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|

#### Anomaly Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Patterns in what is abnormal: deviation baselines, outlier structures, exception dynamics, breakpoint sequences. |
| **Examples** | "Revenue deviations greater than 2 standard deviations from the 8-week average require investigation within 24 hours." "A 3-day pattern of declining quality scores typically precedes a critical review." |
| **Purpose** | Enable anomaly detection, exception management, investigation triggers, early warning. |
| **Processing priority** | High (anomaly Patterns detect when things are wrong). |

#### Recovery Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Patterns in how the business recovers from disruptions: recovery time, recovery trajectory, resilience factors, normalization sequences. |

| **Purpose** | Enable recovery planning, resilience assessment, disruption response optimization. |
| **Processing priority** | Event-driven (triggered by disruption events). |

#### Learning Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Patterns in the system's own learning: what it learns from, how quickly it learns, where its understanding gaps persist. |
| **Examples** | "Patterns related to customer behavior learn faster than patterns related to supply chain (more data)." "The system learns most from failed predictions (3x learning value vs. confirmed predictions)." |
| **Purpose** | Enable meta-cognition, learning optimization, gap identification. |
| **Processing priority** | Periodic (meta-cognitive review cycles). |

#### Meta Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Patterns about Patterns: Pattern interaction dynamics, Pattern evolution trajectories, cross-Pattern relationships, Pattern lifecycle trends. |
| **Examples** | "Financial Patterns tend to shift 2-4 weeks after Operational Patterns." "Behavioral Patterns are more stable than Environmental Patterns." |
| **Purpose** | Enable higher-order reasoning, Pattern system optimization, cognitive architecture improvement. |
| **Processing priority** | Periodic (meta-cognitive review cycles). |

#### Composite Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Patterns composed of multiple sub-Patterns. A higher-order structure that emerges from the interaction of simpler Patterns. |
| **Examples** | "Tuesday lunch decline Pattern" may combine: Temporal Pattern (Tuesday is slower), Operational Pattern (lunch service runs leaner), Environmental Pattern (construction nearby), Customer Pattern (business customers have meetings). |
| **Purpose** | Enable multi-dimensional reasoning, holistic understanding, complex prediction. |
| **Processing priority** | By composite priority (highest-priority component Pattern determines composite priority). |

### 4.3 Classification Principles

1. **Every Pattern has exactly one primary category.** This determines its primary processing path.
2. **A Pattern may have multiple secondary categories.** A Pattern showing that "Tuesday lunch revenue declines when it rains" is Temporal (primary), Environmental (secondary), Financial (secondary).
3. **Category may evolve.** A Potential Pattern may start as Operational and later gain Financial significance.
4. **Composite Patterns are not a separate hierarchy.** They are Standard Patterns with explicit sub-Pattern relationships.

## Part 5 — Pattern Quality

### 5.1 Quality Dimensions

Every Pattern is assessed across ten quality dimensions. Quality determines how much the system trusts the Pattern and how heavily it is weighted in reasoning.

#### Validity

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Pattern reflects a genuine recurring structure (not random variation, not a sampling artifact, not a coincidence). |
| **Assessment** | Statistical significance testing. Cross-validation across independent Observation samples. Temporal stability check. |
| **Failure detection** | Pattern fails to predict new Observations. Pattern contradicts established Knowledge. Owner corrects or dismisses Pattern-based recommendations. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Flag Pattern for validity review. (2) Reduce confidence. (3) Run additional validation tests. (4) If confirmed invalid, deprecate with full documentation. (5) If confirmed valid with reduced scope, adjust scope and confidence. |

#### Consistency

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How consistently the Pattern holds across its scope. A consistent Pattern has low variance in its expression. |
| **Assessment** | Variance analysis. Deviation frequency. Outlier proportion within supporting Observations. |
| **Failure detection** | Increasing variance over time. Growing proportion of Observations that contradict the Pattern. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess whether the Pattern is genuinely degrading or experiencing a temporary perturbation. (2) If degrading, narrow scope or reduce strength. (3) If temporary, monitor for return to baseline. (4) If inconsistent beyond usefulness, deprecate. |

#### Coverage

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How much of the Pattern's claimed scope is supported by Observations. Full coverage → every scope element is observed. Partial coverage → some scope elements are inferred. |
| **Assessment** | Proportion of scope elements with direct Observation support vs. inference. Temporal coverage (how much of the Pattern's time range has been observed). |
| **Failure detection** | Gaps in Observation coverage that are larger than the Pattern's confidence justifies. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify coverage gaps. (2) Reduce confidence proportional to gap size. (3) Mark scope elements without Observation support as "inferred." (4) Prioritize gap-filling Observations. |

#### Stability

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Pattern's characteristics (direction, magnitude, scope) are stable over time or changing. |
| **Assessment** | Parameter tracking over time. Change point detection. Regime shift monitoring. |
| **Failure detection** | Significant parameter changes detected. Pattern predictions systematically diverging from actuals. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess whether the Pattern has genuinely changed or the divergence is temporary. (2) If changed, update Pattern parameters. (3) If temporary, maintain current parameters but monitor. (4) If unstable beyond usefulness, deprecate. |

#### Novelty

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How new or unexpected the Pattern is relative to the system's existing knowledge. |
| **Assessment** | Comparison with established Pattern library. Deviation from expected Pattern evolution. |
| **Failure detection** | Pattern is not actually novel — it duplicates an existing Pattern. Pattern is too novel — it conflicts with established Knowledge without justification. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess genuine novelty. (2) If duplicate, merge with existing Pattern. (3) If genuinely novel, prioritize for investigation. (4) If novelty is false, adjust novelty detection parameters. |

#### Strength

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | The magnitude of the Pattern's effect. A strong Pattern has a large effect size. |
| **Assessment** | Effect size measurement. Signal-to-noise ratio. |
| **Failure detection** | Effect size is statistically significant but business-insignificant. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess business significance. (2) If effect is too small to matter for decisions, mark as "weak" and deprioritize. (3) If effect is meaningful, maintain at appropriate strength level. |

#### Generality

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How broadly the Pattern applies. A general Pattern holds across many conditions. A specific Pattern holds only under narrow conditions. |
| **Assessment** | Cross-condition testing. Does the Pattern hold under different operational states, times, and contexts? |
| **Failure detection** | Pattern claimed as general but fails in specific well-understood conditions. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify the conditions under which the Pattern holds and does not hold. (2) Narrow scope to conditions where it holds. (3) If scope becomes too narrow to be useful, deprecate. |

#### Predictive Power

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How well the Pattern enables prediction of future Observations. |
| **Assessment** | Prediction accuracy tracking. Comparison of predicted vs. actual Observations over time. |
| **Failure detection** | Prediction accuracy declining. Prediction confidence intervals widening. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess why predictive power is declining. (2) If Pattern has changed, update. (3) If Pattern is fundamentally unpredictable, reduce confidence and narrow prediction horizon. (4) If predictive power is zero, deprecate. |

#### Explainability

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How well the Pattern can be explained in business terms. A Pattern that cannot be explained is less useful for reasoning and communication. |
| **Assessment** | Can the system produce a natural-language explanation of the Pattern? Does the explanation make business sense? |
| **Failure detection** | Pattern passes statistical tests but the system cannot explain why it exists in business terms. Owner does not understand Pattern-based recommendations. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Attempt to find causal or contextual explanation. (2) If explanation is found, document and maintain. (3) If no explanation exists, flag as "unexplained statistical pattern" with reduced confidence. (4) If Pattern cannot be explained and has low predictive power, deprecate. |

#### Robustness

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How resilient the Pattern is to noise, missing data, and minor context changes. |
| **Assessment** | Noise injection testing. Missing data tolerance assessment. Context variation testing. |
| **Failure detection** | Pattern collapses under mild noise. Pattern requires complete data to hold. Pattern fails when context varies slightly. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess robustness boundaries. (2) Document conditions under which the Pattern is fragile. (3) Reduce confidence when operating near fragility boundaries. (4) If Pattern is too fragile to be useful, deprecate. |

### 5.2 Quality Principles

1. **Every Pattern has a quality profile.** Quality is assessed at creation and continuously updated.
2. **Quality determines weight.** Higher-quality Patterns contribute more to reasoning.
3. **Quality can change.** A Pattern may improve (more evidence, higher consistency) or degrade (drift, contradictions).
4. **Quality failures are not deletions.** A low-quality Pattern is preserved with its quality annotation.
5. **Quality is dynamic.** Continuous re-assessment is mandatory. A Pattern's quality at discovery is not its final quality.

## Part 6 — Pattern Relationships

### 6.1 Relationship Network

Patterns exist in a dense relationship network. A Pattern's cognitive value is largely determined by its position in this network.

### 6.2 Relationship Definitions

#### Pattern ↔ Observation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | A Pattern is supported by Observations. Observations are the evidence that the Pattern exists. |
| **Direction** | Observation → Pattern (Observations support Pattern). |
| **Cardinality** | A Pattern requires at least one Observation. An Observation may support many Patterns. |
| **Cognitive significance** | This is the most fundamental Pattern relationship. A Pattern without Observation support is not a Pattern — it is speculation. |

#### Pattern ↔ Pattern

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Patterns may relate to other Patterns through: composition (Pattern A is a component of Pattern B), correlation (Pattern A and B tend to co-occur), causality (Pattern A influences Pattern B), exclusion (Pattern A and B cannot both hold), hierarchy (Pattern A is a sub-type of Pattern B). |
| **Direction** | May be directional (causal, compositional) or bidirectional (correlational). |
| **Cardinality** | Any Pattern may relate to any number of other Patterns. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Pattern-to-Pattern relationships enable composite understanding. The interaction of Patterns reveals more than any individual Pattern. |

#### Pattern → Knowledge

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | A Pattern may become the evidentiary basis for Knowledge when it is validated and understood. |
| **Direction** | Pattern → Knowledge (Pattern supports Knowledge). |
| **Cardinality** | A Pattern may support multiple Knowledge statements. Knowledge is typically supported by multiple Patterns. |
| **Cognitive significance** | This is the bridge from structure to understanding. Pattern is "what repeats." Knowledge is "why it repeats and what it means." |

#### Pattern → Recommendation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | A Pattern may inform a Recommendation by providing the structural understanding from which the recommendation is derived. |
| **Direction** | Pattern → Recommendation (via Reasoning → Decision). |
| **Cardinality** | A Pattern may inform many Recommendations. A Recommendation typically derives from multiple Patterns. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Patterns are the structural foundation of Recommendations. A Recommendation without Pattern lineage is an opinion, not an informed suggestion. |

#### Pattern → Narrative

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | A Pattern may be referenced within a Narrative as the structural evidence for the story being told. |
| **Direction** | Pattern → Narrative (Pattern informs Narrative). |
| **Cardinality** | A Pattern may appear in multiple Narratives. A Narrative typically references multiple Patterns. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Patterns give Narratives their authority. "Tuesday lunch is slower" is not a story — it is a Pattern. "Tuesday lunch is slower because..." is a Narrative built on the Pattern. |

#### Pattern → Learning

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | A Pattern may trigger Learning when it is validated (new Knowledge), contradicts existing Knowledge (belief update), or is deprecated (understanding evolution). |
| **Direction** | Pattern → Learning (Pattern creates learning opportunity). |
| **Cardinality** | A Pattern may generate multiple Learning events over its lifecycle. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Patterns are the primary source of Learning. The system learns when it discovers a Pattern, validates a Pattern, or deprecates a Pattern. |

#### Pattern → Business Pulse

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Patterns collectively inform Business Pulse. The health of the Pattern ecosystem — discovery rate, validation rate, deprecation rate, contradiction rate — reflects the system's cognitive health. |
| **Direction** | Pattern → Business Pulse (aggregate). |
| **Cardinality** | All active Patterns contribute to Pulse. |
| **Cognitive significance** | A system that is discovering many Patterns has high Pulse. A system whose Patterns are being deprecated faster than discovered has degrading Pulse. |

#### Pattern → Decision

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | A Pattern may be traced to a Decision that the Owner made, either because the Pattern informed the Recommendation or because the Decision's outcome became evidence for the Pattern. |
| **Direction** | Bidirectional: Pattern → Decision (Pattern informs Decision); Decision → Pattern (Decision outcome supports or challenges Pattern). |
| **Cardinality** | A Pattern may inform many Decisions. A Decision may reference many Patterns. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Decision-Pattern tracing enables the system to learn from which Patterns produce good decisions and which produce poor ones. |

#### Pattern → Memory

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Patterns are stored in Pattern Memory. They may also be referenced from Working Memory (during discovery), Short-Term Memory (recent Patterns), Long-Term Memory (mature Patterns), Decision Memory (Patterns that informed decisions), and Learning Memory (Patterns that generated learning). |
| **Direction** | Pattern → Memory (Pattern is stored in Memory). |
| **Cardinality** | Each Pattern exists in Pattern Memory. Active Patterns may simultaneously exist in multiple other memory systems. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Memory is where Patterns live and are organized for retrieval. The Pattern-Memory relationship determines how quickly Patterns can be accessed for reasoning. |

#### Pattern → Confidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Every Pattern carries a Confidence assessment. Additionally, Patterns collectively determine confidence in Knowledge (Pattern confidence propagates upward) and are influenced by the confidence of their supporting Observations (Observation confidence propagates upward). |
| **Direction** | Observation → Pattern (Observation confidence influences Pattern confidence). Pattern → Knowledge (Pattern confidence influences Knowledge confidence). |
| **Cardinality** | Each Pattern has exactly one Confidence assessment. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Confidence is the thread that connects Observations → Patterns → Knowledge → Recommendations. The confidence chain determines how much the system trusts its own reasoning. |

### 6.3 Relationship Principles

1. **Pattern lineage is mandatory.** Every Pattern must trace to at least one Observation. Every Knowledge must trace to at least one Pattern.
2. **Relationships do not modify the Pattern.** A Pattern's relationships are external. The Pattern itself remains unchanged.
3. **Relationships are additive.** New relationships do not replace existing ones.
4. **Relationship strength is proportional to Pattern and Observation quality.** Higher-quality artifacts create stronger relationships.
5. **Relationship decay is inherited.** When a Supporting Observation's confidence decays, the Pattern's relationship strength to that Observation decays correspondingly.

## Part 7 — Pattern Invariants

### 7.1 Identity Invariants (1–6)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | Every Pattern has a unique identity. No two Patterns share the same identity. | Identity collision. The system confuses two different structures. |
| 2 | Identity is assigned at Candidate stage and never changes. | Identity mutation. A Pattern becomes untraceable across its lifecycle. |
| 3 | Every Pattern has exactly one identity. | Identity multiplicity. The same structure appears as multiple distinct Patterns. |
| 4 | Identity is independent of description. Two Patterns with similar descriptions but different supporting Observations have different identities. | Identity conflation. Different structures collapse into one. |
| 5 | Identity is not meaningful. It is a unique reference, not a description. | Identity pollution. The identity carries semantic meaning that may conflict with the Pattern's content. |
| 6 | Identity survives deprecation. Even deprecated Patterns retain their identity. | Identity reclamation. A deprecated Pattern's identity could be reused for a new Pattern, causing confusion. |

### 7.2 Discovery Invariants (7–13)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 7 | A Pattern must be discovered from Observations. It is never invented, created, or assumed. | Invented Pattern. The system imposes false structure on random variation. |
| 8 | Every Pattern must be supported by at least one Observation. Zero-Observation Patterns do not exist. | Baseless Pattern. A claim of structure without evidence. |
| 9 | A Pattern requires recurrence. A single occurrence is not a Pattern. | Single-event Pattern. The system treats a unique event as recurring structure. |
| 10 | A Pattern requires consistency across multiple instances. Coincidental alignment is not a Pattern. | Coincidental Pattern. The system mistakes coincidence for structure. |
| 11 | Discovery is passive. The system discovers Patterns that exist. It does not select which Patterns to discover. | Selective discovery. The system discovers only Patterns that confirm its beliefs. |
| 12 | All Patterns are equally discoverable. The system does not privilege certain Pattern types. | Discovery bias. The system systematically misses certain Pattern types. |
| 13 | A Pattern that cannot be discovered from available Observations does not exist, even if it is real. | Assumed discovery. The system claims knowledge it cannot support. |

### 7.3 Validation Invariants (14–20)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 14 | Validation is mandatory before a Pattern can be promoted to Validated stage. Every validation gate must be passed. | Skipped validation. An unvalidated Pattern is treated as reliable. |
| 15 | Validation gates are: statistical significance, cross-validation, temporal stability, causal plausibility, business meaning. All five must pass. | Partial validation. A Pattern passes some gates but is treated as if it passed all. |
| 16 | Validation is repeatable. A Pattern must pass validation at any point in its lifecycle, not just at promotion. | One-time validation. A Pattern passes validation once and is never re-evaluated. |
| 17 | A Pattern that fails validation cannot be promoted. It must remain at its current stage or be deprecated. | Forced promotion. A Pattern is promoted despite failing validation. |
| 18 | Validation is independent of importance. A highly important Pattern must still pass validation. | Importance-based validation bypass. Important Patterns are exempted from validation. |
| 19 | Validation produces a validation record. Every validation event is recorded with its outcome. | Validation amnesia. The system cannot audit its own validation history. |
| 20 | Re-validation is required when new evidence contradicts a validated Pattern. | Validation expiry. A Pattern remains validated long after evidence has contradicted it. |

### 7.4 Evidence Invariants (21–27)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 21 | Supporting Observations are immutable references. The Observations themselves never change. | Evidence mutation. The Pattern's evidentiary foundation shifts. |
| 22 | Supporting Observations must be independent. Observations from the same source at the same time do not count as independent evidence. | False independence. The Pattern appears more supported than it is. |
| 23 | Evidence strength is proportional to Observation quality. Low-quality Observations contribute less support. | Evidence-quality disconnect. All Observations are treated as equal regardless of quality. |
| 24 | Contradictory evidence must be recorded alongside supporting evidence. A Pattern's evidence profile includes both. | Evidence suppression. The system ignores evidence that contradicts the Pattern. |
| 25 | Evidence is additive. New supporting evidence increases Pattern confidence. Evidence is never retroactive. | Retroactive evidence. New evidence is treated as if it existed at the Pattern's discovery. |
| 26 | Evidence decay occurs. As Observations age, their evidentiary weight decreases. | Static evidence. Old Observations carry the same weight as new ones. |
| 27 | A Pattern must always be aware of its evidence gaps. Gaps are explicitly tracked. | Evidence gap blindness. The system does not know what evidence is missing. |

### 7.5 Lifecycle Invariants (28–35)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 28 | Pattern lifecycle moves forward only. No stage regression beyond one level (Supported → Emerging is allowed; Supported → Candidate is not). | Lifecycle reversal. A Pattern returns to an early stage, invalidating all reasoning that depended on it. |
| 29 | Every Pattern must pass through Emerging before reaching Supported. | Stage skip. A Pattern is treated as reliable without emerging evidence. |
| 30 | A Pattern cannot reach Knowledge Evidence without passing through Validated and Knowledge Candidate. | Knowledge bypass. A Pattern becomes the basis for Knowledge without proper validation. |
| 31 | Deprecation is not deletion. Deprecated Patterns are preserved with full lifecycle records. | Pattern deletion. The system loses its learning history. |
| 32 | Deprecation must include a reason. Every deprecation is documented with why it occurred. | Reasonless deprecation. The system cannot learn from why the Pattern failed. |
| 33 | A Pattern cannot be deprecated while it is the sole support for active Knowledge. The Knowledge must be addressed first. | Knowledge orphan. Deprecating a Pattern orphans the Knowledge that depends on it. |
| 34 | Historical Patterns may be re-evaluated. Re-emergence starts a new discovery cycle. | Permanent archival. Historical Patterns are invisible to future discovery. |
| 35 | A Pattern's lifecycle stage is always known. The system always knows where every Pattern is in its lifecycle. | Lifecycle ambiguity. The system does not know what stage a Pattern is in. |

### 7.6 Confidence Invariants (36–42)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 36 | Every Pattern has a confidence assessment. | Unconfident Pattern. The system cannot calibrate trust. |
| 37 | Confidence is derived from: Observation quantity, Observation quality, Observation consistency, temporal persistence, validation status. Not manually assigned. | Gamed confidence. Confidence could be inflated or deflated arbitrarily. |
| 38 | Confidence is never zero. Even a Candidate Pattern has minimal positive confidence (indicates it was worth investigating). | Zero confidence. The system cannot distinguish between "not a Pattern" and "Pattern with no evidence." |
| 39 | Confidence is never complete (100%). Every Pattern carries some uncertainty. | False certainty. A Pattern is treated as infallible. |
| 40 | Confidence decays without reinforcement. Unreinforced Patterns lose confidence over time. | Static confidence. A Pattern retains its confidence even as supporting evidence ages. |
| 41 | Confidence updates are recorded. Every confidence change is logged with reason. | Confidence amnesia. The system cannot learn from its confidence calibration. |
| 42 | Pattern confidence is bounded by its weakest quality dimension. A Pattern with perfect validity but poor freshness has reduced confidence. | Dimension-independent confidence. One high dimension compensates for a failing dimension. |

### 7.7 Relationship Invariants (43–49)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 43 | A Pattern must always trace to at least one Observation. | Orphan Pattern. A Pattern exists without evidentiary foundation. |
| 44 | A Pattern may support many other Patterns. Relationship cardinality is unlimited. | Relationship cap. A Pattern's cognitive influence is artificially limited. |
| 45 | Knowledge never owns a Pattern. Patterns remain independent after Knowledge formation. | Knowledge ownership. Deprecating Knowledge would orphan the Pattern. |
| 46 | A Pattern-Pattern relationship is recorded as a relationship, not as a merge. The Patterns retain their independent identities. | Pattern merger. Two distinct Patterns are collapsed into one, losing their individual evidentiary chains. |
| 47 | Pattern-to-Pattern relationships must be explainable. "These Patterns are related because..." must be answerable. | Unexplained relationship. A relationship exists without cognitive justification. |
| 48 | Every Recommendation must trace through at least one Pattern to at least one Observation. | Pattern-less recommendation. A Recommendation exists without structural foundation. |
| 49 | Every Narrative must trace through at least one Pattern to at least one Observation. | Pattern-less narrative. A story is told without structural foundation. |

### 7.8 Evolution Invariants (50–55)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 50 | A Pattern's parameters (scope, strength, confidence) evolve as new evidence arrives. A static Pattern is a dead Pattern. | Pattern stagnation. The Pattern does not reflect current business reality. |
| 51 | Evolution is evidence-driven. Parameters change only in response to new Observations or contradictions. | Speculative evolution. Parameters change without evidentiary basis. |
| 52 | Evolution preserves history. Previous parameter values are recorded alongside current values. | Evolution amnesia. The system cannot track how its understanding has changed. |
| 53 | A Pattern may split if evidence reveals two distinct structures within it. The split produces two new Patterns with their own identity. | False unity. A single Pattern masks two genuinely different structures. |
| 54 | Two Patterns may merge if evidence reveals they are the same structure. The merge produces one Pattern with combined evidence. | False separation. Two Patterns represent the same structure, wasting cognitive resources. |
| 55 | Split and merge events are recorded and dated. The system can reconstruct the evolution of any Pattern. | Evolution amnesia. The system loses the history of structural reorganization. |

### 7.9 Integrity Invariants (56–63)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 56 | The system never uses a Pattern beyond its validated scope. A Pattern validated for lunch service is not applied to dinner. | Scope violation. The Pattern is applied where it has not been validated. |
| 57 | The system never relies on a Pattern that has been deprecated. Deprecated Patterns are excluded from active reasoning. | Deprecated Pattern use. The system continues to reason based on invalid structure. |
| 58 | The system never treats a Potential or Candidate Pattern as reliable enough for high-confidence recommendations. | Premature use. An unvalidated Pattern influences important decisions. |
| 59 | Pattern discovery is continuous. The system is always looking for Patterns, not just during designated discovery periods. | Periodic discovery. Patterns that emerge between discovery cycles are missed. |
| 60 | The system never stops monitoring existing Patterns. Every Pattern is continuously re-evaluated. | Monitoring cessation. A Pattern that has changed goes undetected. |
| 61 | A Pattern that has been stable for an extended period is not assumed to remain stable. Continuous monitoring is mandatory. | Stability complacency. The system assumes a stable Pattern will remain stable. |
| 62 | Pattern-to-Pattern conflicts are not errors. They signal incomplete understanding and are resolved through evidence, not suppression. | Conflict suppression. Conflicting Patterns are resolved by ignoring one, not by investigation. |
| 63 | Pattern relationships are acyclic. Pattern A supporting Pattern B and Pattern B supporting Pattern A is not permitted (circular reasoning). | Circular pattern support. Two Patterns support each other with no external evidentiary foundation. |

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |

## Part 8 — Pattern Failure Modes

### 8.1 False Pattern

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | The system identifies a Pattern that does not exist. Random variation, sampling error, or coincidence is interpreted as structure. |
| **Detection** | Pattern fails to predict new Observations. Pattern contradicts established Knowledge. Pattern does not replicate across independent samples. |
| **Mitigation** | Statistical significance requirements. Cross-validation across independent samples. Temporal stability requirements (Pattern must hold over time, not just at discovery). |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify the Pattern as false. (2) Deprecate with full documentation of why it was false. (3) Audit all downstream recommendations and narratives that depended on it. (4) Adjust discovery parameters to reduce false Pattern rate. (5) Communicate any affected Owner communications. |

### 8.2 Overfitting

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | The Pattern describes the supporting Observations too precisely, capturing noise as if it were signal. The Pattern works on existing data but fails on new data. |
| **Detection** | High performance on existing Observations, poor performance on new Observations. Pattern complexity is high relative to available evidence. |
| **Mitigation** | Complexity constraints proportional to evidence volume. Cross-validation. Simplicity preference (simpler Pattern with adequate fit is preferred over complex Pattern with perfect fit). |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify overfitted Pattern. (2) Simplify to essential structure. (3) Re-validate on held-back Observations. (4) If simplification reduces performance below usefulness threshold, deprecate. (5) Adjust discovery parameters to favor simpler Patterns. |

### 8.3 Underfitting

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | The Pattern is too simple to capture the genuine structure in the Observations. The system misses important nuance. |
| **Detection** | Pattern predictions are systematically biased or have high error rates. Pattern does not capture known sub-structures. |
| **Mitigation** | Complexity adequacy checks. Residual analysis. Sub-group validation (does the Pattern hold across all relevant sub-groups?). |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify underfitted Pattern. (2) Investigate whether additional structure exists that the Pattern has missed. (3) If yes, refine the Pattern or create sub-Patterns. (4) If no additional structure exists (Pattern is genuinely simple), maintain. |

### 8.4 Coincidental Pattern

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Two unrelated phenomena align temporarily, creating the appearance of a Pattern. When the alignment ends, the Pattern disappears. |
| **Detection** | Pattern appears strong but has no plausible business explanation. Pattern disappears when external conditions change. Pattern does not replicate in similar contexts. |
| **Mitigation** | Temporal stability requirements (Pattern must hold over multiple cycles, not just one). Causal plausibility assessment. Cross-context validation. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify as coincidental. (2) Deprecate with documentation of the coincidental alignment. (3) Extract learning: what aligned to create the illusion of Pattern? (4) Adjust discovery parameters to require temporal persistence. |

### 8.5 Weak Pattern

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | A genuine Pattern exists but its effect size is too small to be useful for business decisions. |
| **Detection** | Pattern is statistically significant but business-insignificant. Effect size is within normal business variance. |
| **Mitigation** | Business significance threshold. Minimum effect size requirements for promotion to Supported. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess business significance. (2) If genuinely too weak, mark as "weak" and deprioritize for reasoning. (3) Retain in Pattern Memory for potential future strengthening (if business conditions change). (4) Do not use in high-confidence recommendations. |

### 8.6 Pattern Drift

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | The business changes over time, causing a previously valid Pattern to gradually lose accuracy. The Pattern exists but no longer reflects current reality. |
| **Detection** | Gradual decline in prediction accuracy. Increasing variance in Pattern expression. Growing contradiction count with recent Observations. |
| **Mitigation** | Continuous monitoring of prediction accuracy. Temporal confidence decay. Automatic re-validation intervals. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Confirm drift (not temporary perturbation). (2) Assess whether the Pattern has changed or ceased to exist. (3) If changed, update parameters. (4) If ceased, deprecate. (5) Document the drift pattern for future recognition. |

### 8.7 Pattern Fragmentation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | A single genuine Pattern is captured as multiple disconnected sub-Patterns. The system sees pieces of a structure without recognizing the whole. |
| **Detection** | Multiple Patterns with overlapping scope, supporting Observations, and predictions. Composite reasoning produces fragmented output. |
| **Mitigation** | Pattern correlation analysis. Similarity detection during Candidate stage. Pattern relationship inference. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify fragmented Pattern cluster. (2) Assess whether a unified Pattern exists. (3) If yes, merge into one Pattern with combined evidence. (4) Retain sub-Patterns as components of the composite Pattern. (5) Adjust discovery parameters to recognize broader structures. |

### 8.8 Pattern Collapse

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | A Pattern that was valid suddenly stops being valid due to a regime change (new menu, new manager, new competitor, new regulation). The structural foundation shifts. |
| **Detection** | Sudden, dramatic decline in prediction accuracy. Systematic prediction failure across multiple instances. |
| **Mitigation** | Regime change detection. Abrupt shift monitoring. Automatic confidence reduction on detection of possible collapse. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Confirm collapse (not temporary anomaly). (2) Immediately reduce Pattern confidence to minimum. (3) Suspend Pattern-dependent reasoning. (4) Investigate cause of collapse. (5) If the regime change is permanent, deprecate. (6) If the regime change is temporary (e.g., seasonal), preserve Pattern but reduce priority until conditions return. |

### 8.9 Pattern Pollution

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Low-quality, false, or irrelevant Patterns accumulate in Pattern Memory, degrading the quality of pattern-based reasoning. |
| **Detection** | Increasing noise in composite reasoning. Decreasing prediction accuracy across multiple Pattern domains. Growing number of low-confidence Patterns. |
| **Mitigation** | Quality thresholds for active Pattern retention. Regular Pattern Memory audits. Importance-weighted retention. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify pollution sources. (2) Audit low-quality Patterns. (3) Deprecate or archive Patterns below quality thresholds. (4) Clean downstream reasoning that used polluted Patterns. (5) Address root cause: is a particular source, category, or discovery process producing low-quality Patterns? |

### 8.10 Pattern Saturation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | The Pattern detection system discovers more Patterns than the cognitive system can effectively process. Reasoning quality degrades because too many Patterns compete for attention. |
| **Detection** | Increasing latency in Pattern-based reasoning. Decreasing recommendation quality despite increasing Pattern count. Attention fragmentation across too many Patterns. |
| **Mitigation** | Importance-based processing priority. Quality thresholds for active Pattern use. Automatic deprecation of low-importance Patterns. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify saturation level. (2) Prioritize high-importance Patterns. (3) Deprecate or archive low-importance Patterns. (4) Increase discovery quality thresholds (fewer, better Patterns). (5) Monitor reasoning quality for improvement. |

## Part 9 — Future Evolution

### 9.1 Evolution Philosophy

The Pattern system is designed to evolve. The following extension points are reserved for future capabilities.

### 9.2 Future Pattern Capabilities

#### Hierarchical Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Patterns organized into multi-level hierarchies. A high-level Pattern (e.g., "Weekend revenue exceeds weekday revenue") decomposes into mid-level Patterns ("Saturday > Sunday > Friday") and low-level Patterns ("Friday dinner drives the weekend increase"). |
| **Architecture Fit** | Enhanced Pattern-to-Pattern relationship (compositional hierarchy). Scope inheritance (child Patterns inherit scope from parent). |
| **Constraints** | Hierarchy is discovered, not imposed. A child Pattern must have its own evidentiary support. |

#### Cross-Domain Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Patterns that span business domains — e.g., combining Financial, Operational, and Customer Patterns into a unified "Tuesday lunch dynamics" Pattern. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Composite Pattern category. Multi-domain scope. Cross-domain relationship inference. |
| **Constraints** | Each domain component must be independently validated. Cross-domain interactions are recorded as relationships, not merged. |

#### Collective Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Anonymous Pattern discovery across multiple businesses. "The structure we see in restaurant A is also present in restaurants B, C, and D — here is the common Pattern." |
| **Architecture Fit** | Cross-instance Pattern matching. Anonymous structure comparison. Private data never leaves the business; only Pattern structure is shared. |
| **Constraints** | Constitution Article 13 (private data never leaves the customer). Only anonymous Pattern metadata crosses boundaries. |

#### Strategic Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Patterns that inform long-term strategy: multi-year trends, market dynamics, competitive evolution, demographic shifts. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Extended temporal scope (years). Reduced confidence (longer horizons have higher uncertainty). Strategic decision relationship. |
| **Constraints** | Strategic Patterns are advisory, not operational. They do not trigger immediate action. Lower confidence is acceptable for strategic Patterns. |

#### Causal Pattern Networks

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Patterns organized into causal networks where Pattern A causes Pattern B, which moderates Pattern C. Full causal chain reasoning. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Typed Pattern-to-Pattern relationships (causal, moderating, mediating). Causal direction tracking. Network-level reasoning. |
| **Constraints** | Causal relationships require stronger evidence than correlational. Causal claims are always qualified with confidence. |

#### Self-Evolving Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Patterns that autonomously adjust their parameters in response to new evidence without requiring manual re-validation at every change. Adaptive Pattern refinement. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Evolution Layer integration. Automatic parameter adjustment within validated bounds. Change-triggered re-validation. |
| **Constraints** | Self-evolution operates within the Pattern's validated scope. Scope changes require explicit re-validation. Change history is fully recorded. |

#### Simulation-Derived Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Patterns discovered through simulation rather than direct Observation. The system runs "what if" scenarios and identifies emergent structures. |
| **Architecture Fit** | New Pattern source (Simulation). Simulation-based Patterns carry lower confidence than Observation-based. |
| **Constraints** | Simulation-derived Patterns are labeled as such. They are validated against real-world Observations before Promotion to Validated. |

#### Emergent Macro Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | High-level Patterns that emerge from the interaction of many lower-level Patterns. The macro Pattern is not visible in any individual Observation — it emerges from the collective. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Composite Pattern with emergent properties. Macro-level reasoning. |
| **Constraints** | Emergent Patterns must be traceable to their constituent Patterns. The system can explain how the macro Pattern emerges from micro Patterns. |

#### Meta-Pattern Intelligence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Patterns about the Pattern system itself: Pattern discovery effectiveness, Pattern lifecycle dynamics, Pattern quality trends, Pattern interaction patterns. The system understands how it understands Patterns. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Meta-Pattern category extended. Evolution Layer integration. Self-referential Pattern analysis. |
| **Constraints** | Meta-Patterns serve the Evolution Layer. They do not directly inform business recommendations. Infinite regress is bounded by architectural constraints. |
