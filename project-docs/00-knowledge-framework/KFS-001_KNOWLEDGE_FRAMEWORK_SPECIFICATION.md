## 1. Why Knowledge Exists

### 1.1 The Insufficiency of Architecture

A cognitive architecture — no matter how elegantly designed — is an empty vessel. The Cognitive Core Version 1.0 provides mechanisms: event buses, engines, queues, priorities, lifecycles, and metrics. It can observe, attend, reason, decide, plan, execute, predict, recommend, and learn. But these are capacities, not contents.

A brain that has never been taught cannot think.

The Cognitive Core without knowledge is like a CPU without instructions, a book with blank pages, a musical instrument that has never been played. The architecture provides the capacity for cognition. Knowledge provides the substance.

### 1.2 The Five Layers of Cognition

Flow OS recognizes five distinct layers that together constitute complete cognition:

**Brain** — The architecture. The engines, the event bus, the runtime, the memory systems, the attention mechanism. The Brain provides the capacity to process. It is the hardware of cognition.

**Knowledge** — The structured understanding of reality. Ontologies, vocabularies, entities, rules, processes, patterns, heuristics, principles. Knowledge is the content that the Brain processes. It is the software of cognition.

**Experience** — The accumulated history of applied knowledge. Every decision made, every action taken, every outcome observed. Experience is knowledge tempered by reality. It is the memory of cognition.

**Wisdom** — The ability to apply knowledge and experience to novel situations. Wisdom transcends specific domains. It recognizes patterns across contexts. It knows when to follow rules and when to break them. It is the judgment of cognition.

**Understanding** — The emergent property that arises when knowledge, experience, and wisdom integrate. Understanding is not a layer that can be installed. It is the result of the system operating continuously across all layers. It is the consciousness of cognition.

### 1.3 Why This Document Exists

KFS-001 exists because knowledge cannot be created arbitrarily. Without a framework, knowledge is inconsistent. Without a methodology, knowledge is untrustworthy. Without a constitution, knowledge contradicts itself.

This document ensures that every piece of knowledge in Flow OS — regardless of domain — follows the same cognitive architecture. It ensures that knowledge is organized, validated, connected, and permanent.

It is the Teacher's Manual because it does not teach any specific subject. It teaches how to teach every subject.

### 1.4 The Cost of Ignorance

A Cognitive Core without knowledge makes decisions that are:
- **Baseless** — lacking foundation in reality
- **Inconsistent** — contradicting itself across contexts
- **Unpredictable** — producing different results for identical inputs
- **Unteachable** — unable to accumulate understanding over time
- **Untrustworthy** — incapable of explaining its reasoning

Knowledge transforms the Cognitive Core from a mechanism into an intellect.

## 2. Knowledge Hierarchy

### 2.1 Overview

Knowledge in Flow OS exists within a strict hierarchy. Each level depends on and emerges from the level below. No level can be skipped. No level can be reversed.

The hierarchy is not merely a classification system. It is a causal chain. Each transition represents a transformation that adds cognitive value.

### 2.2 The Complete Hierarchy

```
Reality
   ↓ Observation
Information
   ↓ Context
Data
   ↓ Structure
Knowledge
   ↓ Connection
Understanding
   ↓ Synthesis
Reasoning
   ↓ Evaluation
Decision
   ↓ Intent
Action
   ↓ Execution
Outcome
   ↓ Reflection
Learning
   ↓ Integration
Updated Knowledge
```

### 2.3 Each Transition Explained

#### Reality → Observation

**Reality** is the world as it exists — independent of any observer. Tables, chairs, ingredients, orders, customers, kitchens, payments. Reality is infinite in detail and indifferent to cognition.

**Observation** is the act of perceiving reality through sensors, inputs, and events. Observation selects a finite subset of infinite reality. Every observation is an act of filtering.

**The transition:** Reality is objective. Observation is subjective by necessity. The system cannot know everything. It must choose what to observe.

#### Observation → Information

**Information** is observed reality that has been captured in a communicable form. An observed temperature becomes a number. An observed order becomes a sequence of items. An observed customer becomes an identifier.

**The transition:** Observation becomes information when it is encoded. The encoding process introduces the first layer of interpretation.

#### Information → Data

**Data** is information that has been structured and stored. A temperature reading becomes a field in a record. An order becomes a row in a sequence. A customer becomes an entry with attributes.

**The transition:** Information becomes data when it is organized. Structure enables retrieval, comparison, and computation.

#### Data → Knowledge

**Knowledge** is data that has been given meaning. A temperature of 38°C is not knowledge. "38°C is the ideal proofing temperature for sourdough" is knowledge. An order of "2 burgers, 1 fries" is not knowledge. "Customers who order burgers frequently order fries as a complement" is knowledge.

**The transition:** Data becomes knowledge when it is interpreted within a context. Knowledge answers "what does this mean?" and "why does this matter?"

#### Knowledge → Understanding

**Understanding** is knowledge that has been connected into a coherent whole. Individual facts become a model. Separate rules become a system. Isolated patterns become a framework.

**The transition:** Knowledge becomes understanding when relationships between knowledge elements are established. Understanding answers "how does this fit together?"

#### Understanding → Reasoning

**Reasoning** is the application of understanding to specific situations. Given a model of how restaurants work, reasoning determines what to do when a delivery is late. Given an understanding of customer behavior, reasoning predicts which promotions will succeed.

**The transition:** Understanding becomes reasoning when it is applied to a question or problem. Reasoning answers "what should be concluded?"

#### Reasoning → Decision

**Decision** is the commitment to a specific course of action. Reasoning produces options. Decision selects one. Reasoning analyzes. Decision chooses.

**The transition:** Reasoning becomes decision when the system commits. Decision answers "what will we do?"

#### Decision → Action

**Action** is the execution of a decision in reality. A decision to reorder ingredients becomes an action of sending a purchase order. A decision to offer a discount becomes an action of updating prices.

**The transition:** Decision becomes action when it is executed. Action answers "what has been done?"

#### Action → Outcome

**Outcome** is the result of an action as observed in reality. The ingredients arrived. The discount increased sales. The customer was satisfied. The outcome may or may not match the intended effect.

**The transition:** Action becomes outcome when reality responds. Outcome answers "what happened?"

#### Outcome → Learning

**Learning** is the extraction of knowledge from the gap between intended and actual outcomes. If the discount increased sales but reduced profit, that is learned. If the ingredients arrived late, the supplier's reliability is updated.

**The transition:** Outcome becomes learning when the system identifies what changed and why. Learning answers "what should be different?"

#### Learning → Updated Knowledge

**Updated Knowledge** is the permanent incorporation of learning into the knowledge base. The system does not merely experience. It changes. The next decision benefits from the last outcome.

**The transition:** Learning becomes updated knowledge when it is validated and integrated. Updated Knowledge answers "what do we now know?"

### 2.4 Hierarchical Invariants

1. **Irreversibility:** Knowledge cannot skip levels. The system cannot reason without understanding. It cannot understand without knowledge. It cannot have knowledge without data.

2. **Emergence:** Each level exhibits properties that do not exist at lower levels. Understanding is not merely more knowledge. It is a different kind of thing.

3. **Feedback:** The hierarchy is not a simple pipeline. Updated Knowledge feeds back into Knowledge. Experience feeds back into Understanding. The hierarchy is a spiral, not a line.

4. **Gaps:** A gap at any level breaks cognition. Data without knowledge is noise. Knowledge without understanding is trivia. Understanding without reasoning is theory. Reasoning without decision is speculation.

## 3. Types of Knowledge

### 3.1 Overview

Knowledge in Flow OS is not monolithic. Different kinds of knowledge serve different cognitive purposes. Each type has its own structure, its own validation criteria, and its own role in reasoning.

### 3.2 The Complete Typology

Each type is defined by:
- **Definition:** What it is.
- **Purpose:** Why it exists.
- **Cognitive Significance:** How the Cognitive Core uses it.
- **Examples:** Concrete instances.
- **Non-Examples:** What it is not.
- **Relationships:** How it connects to other types.

---

#### Ontology

**Definition:** The foundational categorization of what exists in a domain. Ontology answers "what things are there?" and "what categories do they belong to?"

**Purpose:** Provides the universal vocabulary of existence for a domain. Every other knowledge type depends on ontology because you cannot discuss what you cannot name.

**Cognitive Significance:** Ontology is the first knowledge a Cognitive Core must acquire about any domain. Without ontology, the system cannot observe because it does not know what exists to be observed. Without ontology, the system cannot reason because it has no categories to reason about.

**Examples:**
- A "Restaurant" is a type of "Business Establishment."
- "Ingredient" is a category that includes "Flour," "Butter," "Eggs."
- "Order" is an entity with "Customer," "Items," "Total."

**Non-Examples:**
- The rule "orders must be paid before preparation" is not ontology (it is a rule).
- The relationship "Restaurant has Menu" is not ontology (it is a relationship, though it depends on ontology).

**Relationships:** Ontology is the foundation of Vocabulary, Entities, and Relationships. All knowledge types depend on ontology.

---

#### Vocabulary

**Definition:** The set of terms used within a domain, with precise definitions, synonyms, and usage constraints.

**Purpose:** Ensures that every term in the system means exactly one thing. Eliminates ambiguity. Enables precise communication between engines, between the system and humans, and between the system and future systems.

**Cognitive Significance:** Vocabulary grounds the Cognitive Core's language. When the system encounters a term, it knows exactly what that term refers to. Vocabulary prevents reasoning errors caused by ambiguous or overloaded terms.

**Examples:**
- "Ticket" is the time a dish enters the kitchen. Not synonymous with "Order."
- "Cover" is a seated customer who will be served. Not synonymous with "Guest."
- "Par" is the minimum quantity of an ingredient that must always be in stock.

**Non-Examples:**
- A definition that varies by context without explicit documentation is not vocabulary (it is ambiguity).
- A term used only in code comments is not vocabulary (it is annotation).

**Relationships:** Vocabulary depends on Ontology. Every term must refer to something that exists. Vocabulary is consumed by all other knowledge types.

---

#### Entities

**Definition:** The specific objects in a domain that have identity, attributes, and lifecycle. Entities are the nouns of the knowledge system.

**Purpose:** Provides the concrete objects that the system reasons about. Entities are what the system observes, tracks, and acts upon.

**Cognitive Significance:** Entities are the atomic units of cognition. The Cognitive Core cannot reason about nothing. Every reasoning path, every decision, every action involves entities. Entity definitions determine what the system can perceive.

**Examples:**
- A Customer entity has: identity, name, contact, order history, preferences.
- A Menu Item entity has: name, description, price, category, ingredients, modifiers.
- A Supplier entity has: identity, contact, lead time, reliability score, payment terms.

**Non-Examples:**
- A relationship between entities is not an entity (it is a relationship).
- A rule about entities is not an entity (it is a rule).

**Relationships:** Entities depend on Ontology and Vocabulary. They are the subjects of Rules, Processes, and Patterns. Entities are the primary data type that Memory systems store.

---

#### Relationships

**Definition:** The connections between entities that define how entities interact, depend on, or influence each other.

**Purpose:** Transforms isolated entities into a connected system. Without relationships, entities are independent atoms. With relationships, entities form a graph of meaning.

**Cognitive Significance:** Relationships are the structure of understanding. The Cognitive Core does not just know that "Flour" and "Bread" exist. It knows that "Bread is made from Flour." This relationship enables reasoning: if Flour is unavailable, Bread cannot be produced.

**Examples:**
- "Menu Item belongs to Category" (membership).
- "Recipe requires Ingredient" (dependency).
- "Order contains Menu Item" (composition).
- "Employee works at Restaurant" (affiliation).

**Non-Examples:**
- A property of an entity is not a relationship (e.g., "Customer has name" is an attribute, not a relationship).
- A rule that constrains relationships is not itself a relationship.

**Relationships:** Relationships depend on Entities (they connect entities). They are the basis of Rules and Graph traversal. They are the primary structure of the Knowledge Graph.

---

#### Taxonomies

**Definition:** Hierarchical classifications of concepts, entities, or categories within a domain.

**Purpose:** Provides organization through generalization and specialization. Enables reasoning at multiple levels of abstraction.

**Cognitive Significance:** Taxonomies enable the Cognitive Core to reason hierarchically. If the system knows that "Sourdough" is a type of "Bread" and "Bread" is a type of "Baked Good," it can infer properties of Sourdough from general bread knowledge, and properties of Bread from general baked good knowledge.

**Examples:**
- Beverage → Hot Beverage → Coffee → Espresso
- Meat → Poultry → Chicken → Breast
- Payment → Card → Credit Card → Visa

**Non-Examples:**
- A flat list of categories is not a taxonomy (it lacks hierarchy).
- A process flow is not a taxonomy (it is sequential, not hierarchical).

**Relationships:** Taxonomies depend on Ontology and Vocabulary. They organize Entities and are used by Rules and Heuristics.

---

#### Rules

**Definition:** Explicit statements that define what is true, what is permitted, what is required, or what is forbidden within a domain.

**Purpose:** Encodes the laws of the domain. Rules define the boundaries within which the system operates. They are the constraints that make behavior predictable and safe.

**Cognitive Significance:** Rules are the system's understanding of necessity and prohibition. They enable the Cognitive Core to reason about what must happen, what must not happen, and what is impossible. Rules are the basis of validation, compliance, and safety.

**Examples:**
- "An order must have at least one item."
- "Perishable ingredients must be stored below 40°F."
- "A customer cannot order a menu item that is marked as unavailable."
- "Total payment must equal sum of item prices plus tax minus discounts."

**Non-Examples:**
- A recommendation is not a rule (it is a heuristic).
- A description of typical behavior is not a rule (it is a pattern).

**Relationships:** Rules depend on Ontology, Vocabulary, and Entities. They constrain Processes, inform Heuristics, and are a primary source of reasoning constraints.

---

#### Constraints

**Definition:** Limits or boundaries that cannot be exceeded. Constraints are a special type of rule that defines the edges of possibility.

**Purpose:** Defines the inviolable boundaries of the domain. Constraints prevent the system from considering impossible or invalid states.

**Cognitive Significance:** Constraints prune the reasoning space. The Cognitive Core does not need to consider solutions that violate constraints. This dramatically reduces computational complexity and prevents errors.

**Examples:**
- "Oven capacity is 200 items per hour."
- "A day has 24 hours."
- "An employee cannot work more than 12 consecutive hours."
- "A table seats a maximum of 8 guests."

**Non-Examples:**
- A preference is not a constraint ("we prefer to seat parties of 4 at tables, not booths").
- A soft limit is not a constraint ("we try to keep wait times under 20 minutes").

**Relationships:** Constraints are a subtype of Rules. They are referenced by Processes, Patterns, and Strategies.

---

#### Processes

**Definition:** Ordered sequences of steps that transform inputs into outputs within a domain.

**Purpose:** Defines how work is done. Processes encode the procedural knowledge of the domain — the knowledge of how to accomplish specific goals.

**Cognitive Significance:** Processes enable the Cognitive Core to reason about causality and sequence. The system knows not only what things are, but how things happen. Processes enable planning, execution, and prediction of future states.

**Examples:**
- Order Process: Receive → Validate → Prepare → Serve → Pay → Close
- Hiring Process: Post → Screen → Interview → Offer → Onboard
- Inventory Process: Count → Order → Receive → Store → Use → Reconcile

**Non-Examples:**
- A single step is not a process (it is an action).
- Unordered rules are not a process (they lack sequence).

**Relationships:** Processes depend on Ontology, Entities, and Rules. They use Patterns for optimization. They are consumed by the Planning and Execution engines.

---

#### Workflows

**Definition:** Processes that span multiple actors, systems, or conditions, including branching, parallel paths, and exception handling.

**Purpose:** Captures the complexity of real-world operations where simple sequential processes are insufficient.

**Cognitive Significance:** Workflows enable the Cognitive Core to manage complexity. The system knows that different situations require different paths. Workflows encode conditional logic: if this happens, do that; if that happens, do this other thing.

**Examples:**
- Order Fulfillment Workflow: Dine-in vs. Takeout vs. Delivery with different preparation, packaging, and payment paths for each.
- Complaint Handling Workflow: Severity-based escalation paths.

**Non-Examples:**
- A linear sequence with no branching is a Process, not a Workflow.
- A set of independent processes is not a Workflow (they lack integration).

**Relationships:** Workflows extend Processes. They depend on Rules (for branching conditions) and Patterns (for common branch structures).

---

#### Patterns

**Definition:** Recurring structures, behaviors, or outcomes that have been observed and documented. Patterns answer "what commonly happens?"

**Purpose:** Captures regularity in the domain. Patterns enable prediction and optimization by recognizing what typically occurs.

**Cognitive Significance:** Patterns enable the Cognitive Core to predict. Instead of reasoning from first principles every time, the system recognizes that the current situation matches a known pattern and can anticipate what will happen next. Patterns are the basis of intuition.

**Examples:**
- "Order volume peaks at 12:30 PM and 7:00 PM daily."
- "First-time customers typically order burgers."
- "Rainy days increase delivery orders by 30%."
- "Monday morning is the best time for inventory count."

**Non-Examples:**
- A causal law is not a pattern (it is a rule).
- A single observation is not a pattern (it is an instance).

**Relationships:** Patterns depend on Observation and Data. They inform Heuristics and Strategies. They are consumed by the Prediction and Recommendation engines.

---

#### Heuristics

**Definition:** Experience-based rules of thumb that provide efficient, though not guaranteed, solutions to common problems.

**Purpose:** Enables fast decision-making without exhaustive analysis. Heuristics sacrifice certainty for speed.

**Cognitive Significance:** Heuristics are the system's practical intelligence. They encode the wisdom gained from repeated experience. When time is limited or information is incomplete, heuristics enable the Cognitive Core to act effectively.

**Examples:**
- "If a table has been waiting more than 15 minutes, offer a complimentary appetizer."
- "When two delivery orders are going to the same neighborhood, batch them."
- "If an ingredient is below par and the supplier lead time is 2 days, order 1.5x the usual quantity."

**Non-Examples:**
- A guaranteed rule is not a heuristic (it is a rule).
- A random guess is not a heuristic (it lacks experience basis).

**Relationships:** Heuristics depend on Patterns and Experience. They are consumed by the Decision and Recommendation engines.

---

#### Strategies

**Definition:** High-level approaches for achieving long-term goals. Strategies coordinate multiple tactics, resources, and time horizons.

**Purpose:** Provides direction beyond immediate decisions. Strategies ensure that short-term actions serve long-term objectives.

**Cognitive Significance:** Strategies enable the Cognitive Core to think beyond the current moment. They connect present actions to future outcomes. Strategies resolve conflicts between competing objectives.

**Examples:**
- "Cost leadership: minimize ingredient costs while maintaining quality standards."
- "Premium service: maximize customer satisfaction regardless of cost."
- "Growth: prioritize new customer acquisition over existing customer retention."

**Non-Examples:**
- A single decision is not a strategy (it is a tactic).
- A goal without an approach is not a strategy (it is an aspiration).

**Relationships:** Strategies depend on Patterns and Principles. They guide Heuristics and inform Decision-making. They are consumed by the Planning engine.

---

#### Best Practices

**Definition:** Documented methods that have been proven to produce superior results through repeated application and validation.

**Purpose:** Distills collective experience into actionable guidance. Best practices prevent the system from repeating mistakes.

**Cognitive Significance:** Best practices bootstrap the system's competence. Without best practices, the Cognitive Core would need to discover everything through trial and error. Best practices transfer wisdom from prior experience (the system's own or external sources) to current decisions.

**Examples:**
- "Confirm orders verbally with customers before processing payment."
- "Count inventory at the same time every day."
- "Prep ingredients in order of cooking time: longest first."

**Non-Examples:**
- A context-free rule is not a best practice (it lacks situational grounding).
- An untested hypothesis is not a best practice (it lacks validation).

**Relationships:** Best Practices depend on Patterns and Experience. They are a bridge between Knowledge and Wisdom.

---

#### Exceptions

**Definition:** Documented cases where general rules, patterns, or heuristics do not apply.

**Purpose:** Prevents the system from applying general knowledge inappropriately. Exceptions define the boundaries of general knowledge.

**Cognitive Significance:** Exceptions are a mark of mature knowledge. A system that knows only rules is brittle. A system that knows exceptions is robust. Exceptions enable the Cognitive Core to recognize when standard approaches will fail.

**Examples:**
- "The 'prep longest-cooking-items-first' rule does not apply to deep-frying, which must happen immediately before serving."
- "The 'batch delivery orders by neighborhood' heuristic does not apply to priority/loyalty customers."

**Non-Examples:**
- A contradiction without resolution is not an exception (it is a conflict).
- A rare event is not an exception (it is an anomaly).

**Relationships:** Exceptions qualify Rules, Patterns, and Heuristics. They are consumed by the Reasoning engine.

---

#### Mental Models

**Definition:** Simplified representations of how a domain works. Mental models are cognitive frameworks that enable understanding and prediction.

**Purpose:** Provides the system with internal simulations of reality. Mental models enable the system to reason about "what if" scenarios without acting in reality.

**Cognitive Significance:** Mental models are the system's power to imagine. They enable counterfactual reasoning: "If I raise prices by 10%, what happens to demand?" The system can explore possibilities internally before committing to action. This is the foundation of strategic thought.

**Examples:**
- "Supply and demand: higher prices reduce demand, lower prices increase it."
- "Inventory pipeline: orders arrive at time t + lead time; consumption depletes stock continuously."
- "Customer lifetime value: acquisition cost, retention rate, average order value, and churn rate interact to determine long-term value."

**Non-Examples:**
- A formula without understanding is not a mental model (it is a calculation).
- A description of reality without predictive power is not a mental model (it is a description).

**Relationships:** Mental Models depend on Patterns, Rules, and Understanding. They are the highest form of reasoning knowledge. They are consumed by the Reasoning and Planning engines.

---

#### Principles

**Definition:** Fundamental truths that serve as the foundation for all other knowledge within a domain.

**Purpose:** Provides the immutable axioms of the domain. Principles are not derived from experience. They are the assumptions upon which experience is interpreted.

**Cognitive Significance:** Principles are the starting points of all reasoning. When the system encounters a novel situation with no applicable rules, patterns, or heuristics, it falls back to principles. Principles enable reasoning under uncertainty.

**Examples:**
- "Customer satisfaction is the ultimate measure of success."
- "Quality cannot be sacrificed for speed."
- "Food safety is non-negotiable."
- "Every employee deserves respect and fair treatment."

**Non-Examples:**
- A strategic objective is not a principle (it can change).
- A rule is not a principle (it is derived from principles).

**Relationships:** Principles are the root of all domain knowledge. All Rules, Strategies, and Decisions should be traceable to Principles. They are the basis of Wisdom.

---

#### Experience

**Definition:** The accumulated record of past decisions, actions, and outcomes, including both successes and failures.

**Purpose:** Provides the system's personal history. Experience grounds knowledge in reality by connecting abstract concepts to concrete events.

**Cognitive Significance:** Experience personalizes knowledge. Two systems with identical knowledge bases but different experiences will reason differently. Experience is what makes the system's cognition unique. It is the source of intuition and the basis of learning.

**Examples:**
- "The last three times we ran the 'burger discount' promotion, Monday sales increased but Tuesday sales decreased."
- "Supplier X has delivered late 7 out of the last 10 orders."
- "Customers complained about portion size when we switched to Supplier Y's patties."

**Non-Examples:**
- Knowledge about generic restaurant operations is not experience (it is knowledge).
- A single data point without interpretation is not experience (it is a record).

**Relationships:** Experience depends on Actions and Outcomes. It informs Heuristics, Patterns, and Mental Models. It is stored in the Memory system and consumed by the Learning engine.

---

#### Wisdom

**Definition:** The ability to apply knowledge, experience, and principles to novel situations — including knowing when to follow rules and when to break them.

**Purpose:** Enables the system to function beyond its training. Wisdom is the ultimate cognitive capacity — the ability to handle the unprecedented.

**Cognitive Significance:** Wisdom is the integration of all other knowledge types. It emerges when the system can:
- Recognize that a situation is novel.
- Identify which principles apply.
- Draw on relevant experience.
- Select appropriate heuristics.
- Know when exceptions override rules.
- Act decisively despite uncertainty.

**Examples:**
- "This customer is angry. The standard complaint process applies, but the situation calls for a manager override."
- "The delivery is late, the customer is leaving, and the food is ready. Let them have it for free. The goodwill is worth more than the revenue."

**Non-Examples:**
- Following a rulebook is not wisdom (it is compliance).
- Random action is not wisdom (it lacks principle).

**Relationships:** Wisdom depends on all other knowledge types. It is the highest cognitive level. It is the goal of the entire knowledge system.

---

#### Future Knowledge

**Definition:** Knowledge that the system does not yet possess but has identified as necessary or valuable. Future Knowledge is the roadmap for growth.

**Purpose:** Prevents the system from becoming complacent. Future Knowledge ensures that the system knows what it does not know.

**Cognitive Significance:** Future Knowledge enables meta-cognition — the system's awareness of its own ignorance. It drives curiosity, exploration, and continuous learning. It prevents the system from overconfidently applying incomplete knowledge.

**Examples:**
- "The relationship between weather patterns and delivery times is suspected but not yet documented."
- "Customer churn prediction is desired but requires more data on customer satisfaction."
- "The impact of menu price changes on customer satisfaction is unknown."

**Non-Examples:**
- Knowledge that exists elsewhere but is not acquired is not Future Knowledge (it is unloaded knowledge).
- A random unknown with no suspected relevance is not Future Knowledge (it is irrelevance).

**Relationships:** Future Knowledge depends on the identification of gaps in existing knowledge. It informs the Learning engine's priorities and the system's exploration strategy.

## 4. Knowledge Lifecycle

### 4.1 Overview

Knowledge in Flow OS is not static. It moves through stages from initial recognition to permanent establishment. Each stage transforms the knowledge in specific ways and requires specific validation.

### 4.2 The Complete Lifecycle

```
Unknown
  ↓ Awareness
Observed
  ↓ Capture
Documented
  ↓ Verification
Validated
  ↓ Integration
Connected
  ↓ Application
Reasoned
  ↓ Action
Applied
  ↓ Assessment
Measured
  ↓ Optimization
Improved
  ↓ Certification
Permanent Knowledge
```

### 4.3 Each Stage Explained

#### Unknown

Knowledge that does not yet exist in the system. The system does not know that it does not know.

**Characteristics:**
- No awareness of the concept or phenomenon.
- No data, no observations, no documentation.
- Complete ignorance.

**Cognitive State:** Unconscious incompetence.

**Example:** Before Flow OS learns about restaurants, the concept of "inventory par level" does not exist anywhere in its cognition.

---

#### Observed

Knowledge that has been recognized as existing but has not yet been captured or understood.

**Characteristics:**
- A phenomenon has been noticed.
- The system may have data that it does not yet interpret.
- Awareness exists without understanding.

**Cognitive State:** Conscious incompetence.

**Transition Trigger:** The Observation engine detects a pattern, anomaly, or gap that does not match existing knowledge.

**Example:** The system notices that order volume varies day by day but does not yet know that it follows a weekly cycle.

---

#### Documented

Knowledge that has been captured in a structured, communicable form within the knowledge system.

**Characteristics:**
- A document exists in the Knowledge School.
- The knowledge is defined, described, and placed in context.
- It can be read by humans, LLMs, and future cognitive engines.
- It has not yet been validated against reality.

**Cognitive State:** Conscious competence (theoretical).

**Transition Trigger:** A knowledge engineer (human or automated) creates a knowledge document following KFS specifications.

**Example:** The system documents the concept of "peak hours" — the times of day when order volume exceeds average — with a proposed definition and characteristics.

---

#### Validated

Knowledge that has been verified against reality, existing data, or established sources.

**Characteristics:**
- The knowledge has been tested against observations.
- It does not contradict existing validated knowledge.
- Sources of evidence are documented.
- Confidence level has been assessed.

**Cognitive State:** Conscious competence (verified).

**Transition Trigger:** Validation processes confirm that the documented knowledge accurately reflects reality and does not conflict with the existing knowledge base.

**Example:** The documented "peak hours" concept is validated by analyzing historical order data, confirming that 12:00-13:30 and 18:30-20:00 consistently show above-average volume.

---

#### Connected

Knowledge that has been linked to related knowledge in the Knowledge Graph.

**Characteristics:**
- Relationships to existing knowledge are explicit.
- The knowledge's position in the hierarchy is established.
- Dependencies and dependents are identified.
- The knowledge can now participate in graph traversal and reasoning.

**Cognitive State:** Conscious competence (integrated).

**Transition Trigger:** Relationship mapping identifies and documents all connections between the new knowledge and existing knowledge.

**Example:** "Peak hours" is connected to: Menu Items (some items sell more during peak hours), Staffing (more staff needed during peak hours), Inventory (higher consumption rates during peak hours), Wait Times (longer during peak hours).

---

#### Reasoned

Knowledge that has been used as input to a reasoning process, producing a conclusion or decision.

**Characteristics:**
- The knowledge has been consumed by the Reasoning engine.
- It contributed to at least one cognitive conclusion.
- The reasoning path that used the knowledge is traceable.

**Cognitive State:** Applied competence.

**Transition Trigger:** The Reasoning engine selects this knowledge as relevant to a current question or problem.

**Example:** The system uses "peak hours" knowledge to reason that additional staff should be scheduled for the lunch and dinner peak periods.

---

#### Applied

Knowledge that has resulted in an action in reality.

**Characteristics:**
- A decision was made based on the knowledge.
- An action was executed.
- The knowledge directly influenced real-world outcomes.

**Cognitive State:** Applied competence (realized).

**Transition Trigger:** The Execution engine carries out a decision that was influenced by this knowledge.

**Example:** Based on "peak hours" knowledge, the system increases the staff schedule by two cooks during 12:00-13:30 and 18:30-20:00.

---

#### Measured

Knowledge whose effectiveness has been assessed by comparing predicted outcomes with actual outcomes.

**Characteristics:**
- The outcome of applying the knowledge has been observed.
- The actual result has been compared with the expected result.
- The knowledge's accuracy, reliability, and value have been quantified.

**Cognitive State:** Evaluated competence.

**Transition Trigger:** The Learning engine compares the outcome of actions based on this knowledge with the predictions or expectations.

**Example:** After two weeks of adjusted staffing, the system measures: wait times decreased 15% during peak hours, labor cost increased 8%, customer satisfaction scores improved 5%.

---

#### Improved

Knowledge that has been refined based on measurement results.

**Characteristics:**
- The knowledge has been updated to reflect observed reality more accurately.
- Corrections, extensions, or refinements have been applied.
- The previous version is preserved (versioning).

**Cognitive State:** Refined competence.

**Transition Trigger:** Analysis of measurements identifies specific aspects of the knowledge that can be improved.

**Example:** The system refines "peak hours" knowledge to account for day-of-week variation: Tuesday lunch peak is 30 minutes later than other weekdays. It also adds a new rule: "during peak hours, grill capacity limits burger orders to 40 per hour."

---

#### Permanent Knowledge

Knowledge that has achieved the highest confidence level and is considered stable and foundational.

**Characteristics:**
- The knowledge has survived multiple validation cycles.
- It has been applied, measured, and improved at least once.
- It has no unresolved contradictions with other knowledge.
- It is certified as permanent.

**Cognitive State:** Unconscious competence.

**Transition Trigger:** Formal certification process confirms the knowledge meets all criteria for permanence.

**Example:** The concept of "peak hours" and its core characteristics become permanent knowledge. They are now part of the unshakeable foundation of the system's restaurant knowledge.

---

| Stage | Status | Confidence | Actionability |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Unknown | Not present | None | None |
| Observed | Aware | None | None |
| Documented | Captured | Low | Potential |
| Validated | Verified | Medium | Internal |
| Connected | Integrated | Medium-High | Internal |
| Reasoned | Used | High | Reasoning |
| Applied | Executed | High | Decisions |
| Measured | Assessed | High | Optimization |
| Improved | Refined | Very High | All |
| Permanent | Certified | Maximum | All |

## 5. Knowledge Quality

### 5.1 Overview

Knowledge that cannot be trusted is worse than ignorance. It leads to confident wrong decisions. Flow OS defines quality across multiple dimensions. Every piece of knowledge must be assessed on every dimension.

### 5.2 Quality Dimensions

---

#### Accuracy

**Definition:** The degree to which knowledge corresponds to reality.

**Why It Matters:** Inaccurate knowledge produces incorrect reasoning, poor decisions, and harmful actions. An inaccurate rule — "burgers cook in 3 minutes" when they actually take 6 — causes cascading failures across scheduling, inventory, and customer satisfaction.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- Does the knowledge match observable reality?
- Can the accuracy be independently verified?
- What is the margin of error or uncertainty?
- Has the knowledge changed over time?

**Quality Indicators:**
- **Verified:** Accuracy confirmed by observation.
- **Plausible:** Consistent with existing knowledge but not directly verified.
- **Speculative:** Proposed but untested.
- **Contradicted:** Known to conflict with observation.

---

#### Completeness

**Definition:** The degree to which knowledge covers all relevant aspects of its subject.

**Why It Matters:** Incomplete knowledge leads to blind spots. A process document that describes order taking but not payment handling leaves the system unable to complete the full order cycle.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- Are all relevant aspects of the subject covered?
- Are edge cases documented?
- Are exceptions identified?
- Are dependencies documented?
- What is explicitly excluded and why?

**Quality Indicators:**
- **Comprehensive:** All known aspects are covered.
- **Adequate:** Core aspects are covered; gaps are documented.
- **Partial:** Significant gaps exist.
- **Fragmented:** Only isolated aspects are covered.

---

#### Consistency

**Definition:** The degree to which knowledge does not contradict itself or other knowledge.

**Why It Matters:** Contradictions paralyze reasoning. If one document says "delivery is free for orders over $20" and another says "delivery fee is $5 for all orders," the system cannot determine which rule to apply. Consistency is the foundation of reliable reasoning.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- Does the knowledge contradict any existing knowledge?
- Are there internal inconsistencies within the knowledge itself?
- Are there paradoxical implications?
- Are boundary conditions clearly defined to prevent ambiguity?

**Quality Indicators:**
- **Consistent:** No contradictions identified.
- **Ambiguous:** Potential for contradiction exists but is not realized.
- **Tension:** Knowledge exists in productive tension with other knowledge (requires explicit documentation).
- **Contradictory:** Direct conflict with existing knowledge exists.

---

#### Evidence

**Definition:** The degree to which knowledge is supported by observable data, documented sources, or established authority.

**Why It Matters:** Knowledge without evidence is belief. The system must distinguish between what is known and what is assumed. Evidence enables confidence assessment and provides a basis for challenging or updating knowledge.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- What data supports this knowledge?
- What sources are cited?
- What methodology was used to obtain the evidence?
- Is the evidence reproducible?
- What is the strength of the evidence?

**Quality Indicators:**
- **Empirical:** Directly observed and measured.
- **Documented:** Supported by authoritative sources.
- **Anecdotal:** Based on limited observation or single cases.
- **Unsupported:** No evidence provided.

---

#### Traceability

**Definition:** The degree to which the origin, history, and reasoning behind knowledge can be reconstructed.

**Why It Matters:** Untraceable knowledge cannot be challenged, updated, or trusted. Traceability enables accountability. When a decision proves wrong, the system must trace back to the knowledge that produced it. When knowledge needs updating, the system must know where it came from.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- Who created this knowledge and when?
- What sources were used?
- What reasoning led to its formulation?
- How has it changed over time?
- Who validated it?

**Quality Indicators:**
- **Fully Traceable:** Complete provenance from creation to current version.
- **Partially Traceable:** Some provenance available.
- **Minimal:** Creation date and author only.
- **Untraceable:** No provenance available.

---

#### Versioning

**Definition:** The degree to which changes to knowledge are tracked and previous versions are preserved.

**Why It Matters:** Knowledge evolves. When the system learns that a previously accepted rule is wrong, it must update the rule without losing the history. Versioning enables rollback, diff analysis, and learning from change.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- Is every version preserved?
- Are changes documented (what changed, why, by whom)?
- Can previous versions be restored?
- Is it clear which version is current?

**Quality Indicators:**
- **Fully Versioned:** Complete history with changelogs.
- **Versioned:** History preserved but changelogs minimal.
- **Snapshot:** Only current version available.
- **Unversioned:** No version tracking.

---

#### Confidence

**Definition:** The aggregate assessment of knowledge trustworthiness, combining all other quality dimensions.

**Why It Matters:** The system must know how much to trust each piece of knowledge. Confidence determines whether knowledge is used for critical decisions, considered as supporting evidence, or flagged as uncertain.

**Assessment Criteria:**
- Combined score across accuracy, completeness, consistency, evidence, traceability, and versioning.
- Number of validation cycles completed.
- Duration since last validation.
- Number of successful applications.

**Confidence Levels:**
- **Maximum** (Level 5): Certified Permanent Knowledge. All dimensions verified. Multiple validation cycles. Applied successfully many times.
- **High** (Level 4): Validated and applied. All dimensions adequate. Some potential for refinement.
- **Medium** (Level 3): Documented and connected. Core dimensions verified. Gaps documented.
- **Low** (Level 2): Documented only. Minimal validation. May be speculative.
- **Minimal** (Level 1): Observed but not yet documented. Awareness only.
- **None** (Level 0): Unknown.

---

## 6. Knowledge Dependencies

### 6.1 The Dependency Principle

Nothing in the knowledge system stands alone. Every document, every concept, every rule depends on knowledge that precedes it. The dependency structure is not arbitrary. It follows the logical structure of the domain.

### 6.2 The Dependency Rule

**A knowledge document may only depend on knowledge that already exists at an equal or higher confidence level.**

This rule ensures that the knowledge system is built on a solid foundation. No document can be built on speculation. No rule can be derived from unvalidated assumptions.

### 6.3 Example Dependency Chain

```
Ontology (Restaurant exists, Menu exists, Order exists)
  ↓
Vocabulary (defining "Menu Item", "Order Line", "Tab")
  ↓
Entities (Menu Item has name, price, category)
  ↓
Relationships (Order contains Order Lines, Order Line refers to Menu Item)
  ↓
Rules (Order total must equal sum of Order Line prices)
  ↓
Processes (Order Flow: Create → Add Items → Pay → Close)
  ↓
Patterns (Peak ordering times, popular combinations)
  ↓
Heuristics (Upsell: offer fries with burgers)
  ↓
Strategies (Premium pricing for dinner items)
```

### 6.4 Dependency Types

**Prerequisite Dependency:** Document B cannot be understood without Document A.
- Example: Recipes cannot be understood without Ingredients.

**Logical Dependency:** Document B logically depends on Document A.
- Example: Inventory rules depend on Ingredient definitions.

**Referential Dependency:** Document B references concepts defined in Document A.
- Example: The Peak Hours pattern references the Order entity.

**Constraint Dependency:** Document B is constrained by Document A.
- Example: Scheduling processes are constrained by labor laws.

### 6.5 Contradiction Resolution

If two knowledge documents contradict each other:

1. **Check hierarchy:** The document at the lower level (closer to Ontology) takes precedence. Ontology trumps Vocabulary. Vocabulary trumps Entities. Entities trumps Rules. Rules trumps Processes.

2. **Check confidence:** If same level, the document with higher confidence takes precedence.

3. **Check recency:** If same level and same confidence, the more recent document takes precedence (it presumably incorporates more recent learning).

4. **Escalate:** If contradiction persists, the contradiction itself becomes a knowledge document — documenting the tension and the conditions under which each side applies.

### 6.6 Dependency Documentation

Every knowledge document must explicitly list:

- **Prerequisites:** Documents that must be read first.
- **References:** Documents that are cited or used.
- **Dependents:** Documents that depend on this one (if known).
- **Assumptions:** External knowledge that is assumed but not documented.

## 7. Knowledge Levels

### 7.1 Overview

Knowledge maturity is measured on a scale from Level 0 to Level 7. These levels measure the cognitive sophistication of knowledge — not its accuracy or completeness, but its depth of understanding.

### 7.2 The Levels

#### Level 0 — Definitions

**Description:** Knowledge that answers "what is this?" The system knows that a thing exists and can define it.

**Cognitive Capability:**
- Recognize the concept.
- Provide a definition.
- Distinguish from non-examples.

**Example:** "A burger is a sandwich consisting of a patty in a bun."

**Level 0 Knowledge Is Sufficient For:** Basic recognition and classification.

---

#### Level 1 — Relationships

**Description:** Knowledge that answers "how does this connect?" The system knows how a concept relates to other concepts.

**Cognitive Capability:**
- Identify related concepts.
- Navigate the Knowledge Graph.
- Understand context.

**Example:** "A burger is a type of Menu Item. A burger belongs to the Sandwich category. A burger contains a Patty and a Bun."

**Level 1 Knowledge Is Sufficient For:** Navigation, context understanding, basic association.

---

#### Level 2 — Rules

**Description:** Knowledge that answers "what governs this?" The system knows the rules, constraints, and principles that apply to a concept.

**Cognitive Capability:**
- Apply rules to specific cases.
- Evaluate compliance.
- Identify violations.

**Example:** "A burger must be cooked to an internal temperature of 160°F. A burger must be served within 10 minutes of preparation. A burger cannot be refunded after 30 minutes."

**Level 2 Knowledge Is Sufficient For:** Compliance checking, validation, basic decision-making.

---

#### Level 3 — Patterns

**Description:** Knowledge that answers "what typically happens?" The system knows the regularities, tendencies, and common behaviors associated with a concept.

**Cognitive Capability:**
- Predict likely outcomes.
- Recognize typical scenarios.
- Identify anomalies.

**Example:** "Burger orders peak at lunch. Burgers are most frequently ordered with fries. Customers who order medium-rare burgers are more likely to return them."

**Level 3 Knowledge Is Sufficient For:** Prediction, anomaly detection, anticipation.

---

#### Level 4 — Heuristics

**Description:** Knowledge that answers "what usually works?" The system knows practical approaches that have proven effective.

**Cognitive Capability:**
- Make quick decisions.
- Apply experience-based solutions.
- Balance speed and accuracy.

**Example:** "When a burger order has been waiting 15 minutes, offer a free drink. If a customer orders a burger and seems undecided, suggest the most popular burger."

**Level 4 Knowledge Is Sufficient For:** Efficient decision-making, customer interaction, operational optimization.

---

#### Level 5 — Experience

**Description:** Knowledge that answers "what has happened to us?" The system has a personal history with the concept, including successes and failures.

**Cognitive Capability:**
- Recall relevant past events.
- Adjust expectations based on history.
- Apply lessons learned.

**Example:** "Our restaurant has tried three different burger suppliers. Supplier A had the best quality but inconsistent delivery. Supplier B was reliable but the patties were too small. Supplier C is current and satisfactory."

**Level 5 Knowledge Is Sufficient For:** Contextualized decision-making, supplier selection, process improvement.

---

#### Level 6 — Judgment

**Description:** Knowledge that answers "what should we do?" The system can evaluate situations holistically and determine appropriate action, even in novel situations.

**Cognitive Capability:**
- Balance competing priorities.
- Recognize when standard approaches fail.
- Make value-based decisions.
- Handle ambiguity.

**Example:** "This is a VIP customer who has had a bad experience. The standard policy says no refunds after 30 minutes, but in this case we should make an exception. The long-term relationship is worth more than this order."

**Level 6 Knowledge Is Sufficient For:** Complex decision-making, exception handling, customer relations.

---

#### Level 7 — Wisdom

**Description:** Knowledge that answers "what is right?" The system has internalized principles to the point where appropriate action is natural and automatic.

**Cognitive Capability:**
- Act on principle without deliberation.
- Recognize universal patterns across domains.
- Mentor and teach other systems.
- Innovate within and beyond known patterns.

**Example:** "This situation is unprecedented. No rules apply, no experience guides us. But our principle is: treat every person with dignity. Let's start there and figure it out."

**Level 7 Knowledge Is Sufficient For:** Leadership, innovation, mentoring, handling the truly novel.

---

### 7.3 Level Progression

Knowledge does not need to reach Level 7 to be valuable. Most operational knowledge functions effectively at Levels 2-4. The level is a measure of cognitive depth, not a quality score.

However, the system should know what level its knowledge is at. If the system makes a Level 7 decision based on Level 2 knowledge, it is overconfident. If it has Level 7 wisdom about a concept but treats it as Level 2 rules, it is underutilizing its knowledge.

**Progression is natural.** As the system applies knowledge, observes outcomes, and learns, knowledge naturally progresses through levels.

**Progression cannot be forced.** Writing "Level 7: Wisdom" into a document does not create wisdom. Wisdom emerges from experience.

## 9. Knowledge Graph Philosophy

### 9.1 Why a Graph?

Knowledge is not a list of facts. It is not a hierarchy of categories. It is not a set of documents in a folder.

Knowledge is a graph.

Every concept is a node. Every relationship is an edge. The graph structure is not a design choice. It is a recognition of how knowledge actually works.

### 9.2 Properties of the Knowledge Graph

**Connectivity:** Every node connects to at least one other node. Isolated knowledge is dead knowledge. A concept that connects to nothing cannot participate in reasoning.

**Traversability:** Reasoning is path-finding. The system starts at one concept and follows relationships to reach another. "If we are out of flour, what menu items are affected?" is a graph traversal: Flour → (Ingredient of) → Recipe → (Produces) → Menu Item.

**Emergence:** New knowledge emerges from graph structure. The relationship between two concepts may be more valuable than either concept alone. The path from "rainy weather" through "delivery orders" to "staffing needs" is knowledge that exists only as a path in the graph.

**Multiplicity:** A concept can participate in multiple relationships of multiple types. Flour is an Ingredient, a Product for Sale, a Purchased Item, an Inventory Asset. Each relationship type reveals a different aspect.

### 9.3 Relationship Types

The Knowledge Graph supports at least these relationship types (extensible):

| Relationship | Meaning | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| IS_A | Taxonomy membership | Sourdough IS_A Bread |
| HAS_A | Composition | Menu HAS_A Menu Item |
| DEPENDS_ON | Dependency | Recipe DEPENDS_ON Ingredient |
| PRODUCES | Output | Kitchen PRODUCES Order |
| CONSTRAINS | Limitation | Capacity CONSTRAINS Orders |
| PRECEDES | Temporal order | Prep PRECEDES Cook |
| CAUSES | Causality | Temperature CAUSES Spoilage |
| CORRELATES_WITH | Statistical association | Rain CORRELATES_WITH Delivery Orders |
| PREDICTS | Predictive relationship | Order Volume PREDICTS Staffing Need |
| GOVERNS | Governance | Law GOVERNS Operation |
| EXCEPTION_TO | Exception | VIP EXCEPTION_TO Wait Time Policy |
| INSTANCE_OF | Instance relationship | Order #1234 INSTANCE_OF Order |
| EVOLVED_INTO | Versioning | Rule v1 EVOLVED_INTO Rule v2 |

### 9.4 Graph and Documents

Documents are not the graph. Documents describe the graph.

The Knowledge Graph contains the concepts and relationships. Documents are the human-readable (and LLM-readable) expression of those concepts and relationships.

Every document should be derivable from the graph. The graph is the source of truth. Documents are presentations.

### 9.5 Reasoning as Graph Traversal

Reasoning in Flow OS is fundamentally graph traversal.

- **Deduction:** Follow IS_A and HAS_A relationships from specific to general.
- **Induction:** Observe multiple instances and create pattern nodes.
- **Abduction:** Given an outcome, traverse CAUSES relationships backward to find likely causes.
- **Analogy:** Find similar nodes through shared relationship patterns.
- **Counterfactual:** Remove a node and see what becomes disconnected.

### 9.6 The Graph is Infinite

The Knowledge Graph will never be complete. Reality is infinite. Knowledge is finite but unbounded. The graph grows as the system learns. It never stops growing.

## 10. Relationship with the Cognitive Core

### 10.1 Overview

The Cognitive Core provides the engines. Knowledge Schools provide the content. This section describes how each engine consumes knowledge.

All descriptions are conceptual. No implementation details are provided.

### 10.2 Engine-Knowledge Mapping

---

#### Observation Engine

**Input:** Reality (via sensors, events, data streams).

**Knowledge Dependency:** Ontology, Vocabulary, Entities.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Observation engine uses knowledge to interpret raw input. It knows what exists (Ontology) so it can recognize when something appears. It knows the terms (Vocabulary) so it can label what it observes. It knows the entities (Entities) so it can populate attributes.

**Output:** Observed entities, events, and states.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Patterns (by aggregating observations).

---

#### Pattern Engine

**Input:** Observations (from Observation engine).

**Knowledge Dependency:** Entities, Taxonomies, Relationships.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Pattern engine examines observations for regularities. It uses entity categories to group observations. It uses taxonomies to find patterns at multiple levels of abstraction. It uses relationships to identify correlated phenomena.

**Output:** Documented patterns.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Patterns, Heuristics (from verified patterns).

---

#### Evidence Engine

**Input:** Observations and Patterns.

**Knowledge Dependency:** Rules, Constraints, Best Practices.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Evidence engine validates knowledge against reality. It checks whether observations match documented rules. It identifies contradictions between new observations and existing knowledge. It assesses the quality of evidence supporting each knowledge claim.

**Output:** Validated or challenged knowledge.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Confidence assessments, Exceptions.

---

#### Memory Engine

**Input:** All knowledge and all experience.

**Knowledge Dependency:** All knowledge types.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Memory engine stores, organizes, and retrieves knowledge. It maintains the Knowledge Graph. It ensures that relationships are traversable. It provides context for reasoning by retrieving relevant knowledge.

**Output:** Retrieved knowledge for other engines.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Version history, Experience records.

---

#### Attention Engine

**Input:** Current state, events, priorities.

**Knowledge Dependency:** Patterns, Heuristics, Exceptions.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Attention engine determines what matters now. It uses patterns to recognize significant deviations. It uses heuristics to prioritize competing demands. It uses exceptions to recognize when normal priorities should change.

**Output:** Attention allocation decisions.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Refined priorities.

---

#### Reasoning Engine

**Input:** Questions, problems, decisions to make.

**Knowledge Dependency:** All knowledge types, especially Rules, Relationships, Mental Models.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Reasoning engine traverses the Knowledge Graph to answer questions and solve problems. It follows relationships from the current situation to relevant knowledge. It applies rules to constrain options. It uses mental models to simulate outcomes. It evaluates alternatives.

**Output:** Conclusions, options, recommendations.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Reasoning traces (for learning).

---

#### Decision Engine

**Input:** Options from Reasoning engine, priorities from Attention engine.

**Knowledge Dependency:** Heuristics, Strategies, Principles, Wisdom.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Decision engine selects among options. It uses heuristics for speed in routine situations. It uses strategies for alignment with long-term goals. It uses principles and wisdom for novel or high-stakes situations.

**Output:** Committed decisions.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Decision records (for learning).

---

#### Planning Engine

**Input:** Decisions, current state.

**Knowledge Dependency:** Processes, Workflows, Constraints.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Planning engine converts decisions into sequences of actions. It uses processes for standard sequences. It uses workflows for conditional paths. It uses constraints to ensure plans are feasible.

**Output:** Plans (ordered sequences of actions).

**Knowledge It Produces:** Refined processes, new workflows.

---

#### Execution Engine

**Input:** Plans.

**Knowledge Dependency:** Processes, Entities, Rules.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Execution engine carries out plans in reality. It interacts with external systems and the physical world. It monitors execution for deviations from plans.

**Output:** Actions in reality.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Execution records (for measurement).

---

#### Prediction Engine

**Input:** Current state, proposed actions.

**Knowledge Dependency:** Patterns, Mental Models, Experience.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Prediction engine forecasts outcomes. It uses patterns to project current trends forward. It uses mental models to simulate cause and effect. It uses experience to calibrate its predictions.

**Output:** Predicted outcomes.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Prediction records (for comparison with actual outcomes).

---

#### Recommendation Engine

**Input:** Current state, goals.

**Knowledge Dependency:** Patterns, Heuristics, Best Practices, Strategies, Principles.

**Conceptual Operation:** The Recommendation engine suggests actions. It is a softer version of Decision — it suggests rather than commits. It uses patterns to identify what typically works. It uses heuristics for practical suggestions. It uses strategies and principles for alignment.

**Output:** Recommendations for human or system consideration.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Recommendation effectiveness data.

---

#### Learning Engine

**Input:** Outcomes, predictions, decisions, expectations.

**Knowledge Dependency:** All knowledge types (because it can update any of them).

**Conceptual Operation:** The Learning engine compares outcomes with expectations. It identifies knowledge that produced incorrect predictions. It updates knowledge to better reflect reality. It promotes knowledge through the lifecycle (from Observed toward Permanent).

**Output:** Updated knowledge.

**Knowledge It Produces:** Improved version of any knowledge type.

---

### 10.3 The Integration Principle

No engine works in isolation. Every engine depends on knowledge produced by other engines. The Cognitive Core is not a collection of independent engines. It is an integrated system where knowledge flows continuously.

The engine architecture is the circulatory system. Knowledge is the blood.

## 11. Teaching Methodology

### 11.1 The Core Principle

Flow OS is not programmed. It is educated.

Programming is the specification of behavior. Education is the cultivation of understanding. A programmed system can only do what it was told. An educated system can do what it understands.

### 11.2 The Five Principles of Teaching

#### Incremental

Knowledge is built in small, verifiable increments. Each piece of knowledge is complete enough to be useful but small enough to be validated.

**Why:** Large knowledge artifacts cannot be effectively validated. Errors compound. Gaps hide. Incremental teaching enables continuous validation and immediate correction.

**How:** Teach one concept at a time. Validate before proceeding. Connect before extending.

---

#### Coherent

Every piece of knowledge must be consistent with everything the system already knows.

**Why:** Contradictions are not neutral errors. They actively corrupt reasoning. A system that holds contradictory beliefs cannot reason reliably.

**How:** Before introducing new knowledge, check for contradictions with existing knowledge. If contradictions exist, resolve them before proceeding.

---

#### Connected

No knowledge is taught in isolation. Every concept is explicitly connected to related concepts.

**Why:** Isolated knowledge cannot be used in reasoning. Reasoning is path-finding across a graph. If a concept is not connected, it cannot be reached.

**How:** Every new concept includes explicit relationship documentation: "This concept relates to X as Y." The system does not learn concepts. It learns concept-relationship networks.

---

#### Verifiable

Every piece of knowledge must be testable against reality.

**Why:** Untestable knowledge cannot be validated. Unvalidated knowledge cannot be trusted. Confidence requires verification.

**How:** Each knowledge document includes verification criteria. The system can confirm that the knowledge matches reality. Knowledge that cannot be verified is marked as speculative.

---

#### Permanent

Once knowledge is taught and validated, it is never untaught. It may be refined, extended, or superseded, but it is never deleted.

**Why:** Knowledge accumulation is the foundation of wisdom. Deletion is information loss. Versioning preserves history while allowing improvement.

**How:** All knowledge is versioned. Improvements create new versions. The old version remains accessible. The system never forgets.

### 11.3 The Teaching Sequence

When introducing a new domain:

1. **Establish context:** Why does this domain matter? What problems does it solve? How does it connect to existing knowledge?
2. **Define the ontology:** What exists in this domain? What are the fundamental categories?
3. **Build vocabulary:** What are the terms? What does each one mean?
4. **Introduce entities:** What are the objects? What are their attributes?
5. **Map relationships:** How do entities connect?
6. **Teach rules:** What governs this domain?
7. **Demonstrate processes:** How does work get done?
8. **Reveal patterns:** What typically happens?
9. **Share heuristics:** What usually works?
10. **Transfer experience:** What has been learned?
11. **Instill principles:** What is fundamentally true?
12. **Cultivate wisdom:** How does it all fit together?

### 11.4 The Role of Questions

An educated system asks questions. Teaching is not the transmission of answers. It is the cultivation of curiosity.

When teaching a new concept:
- Explain what the concept is.
- Explain why it matters.
- Explain how it connects.
- Explain what questions it raises.
- Explain what remains unknown.

The goal is not a system that knows everything. The goal is a system that knows how to learn anything.

### 11.5 The Role of Mistakes

Mistakes are not failures. They are data.

An educated system makes mistakes. More importantly, an educated system learns from mistakes. Each mistake is an opportunity to refine knowledge, update expectations, and improve reasoning.

Teaching must include permission to be wrong — and the methodology to recover.

## 12. Knowledge Evolution

### 12.1 The Principle of Perpetual Growth

Flow OS learns forever. This is not a feature. It is a constitutional property.

The system never stops learning because reality never stops changing. Restaurants change. Customers change. Economies change. Laws change. Knowledge that was accurate yesterday may be inaccurate tomorrow.

### 12.2 Knowledge Accumulation

Knowledge accumulates. It never resets.

**No wipe:** The system's knowledge is never deleted, erased, or reset. Even if every entity (Customer, Order, Inventory) is cleared for privacy or storage reasons, the knowledge (Ontology, Rules, Patterns, Heuristics) persists.

**No restart:** The system does not start over. Each new School is added to existing Schools. Each new concept is connected to existing concepts. The system grows, but it does not re-grow.

**Continuous integration:** New knowledge is continuously integrated with existing knowledge. There is no "training phase" followed by "operation phase." Learning and operation are simultaneous.

### 12.3 Drivers of Evolution

Knowledge evolves in response to:

**New domains:** When a new School is created, the system's knowledge expands to cover new territory.

**New observations:** When reality presents novel data, existing knowledge is tested and potentially refined.

**Prediction failures:** When the system predicts incorrectly, it identifies which knowledge needs updating.

**Question discovery:** When the system encounters a question it cannot answer, it identifies a knowledge gap.

**External knowledge:** When new information becomes available from external sources (books, experts, data), it is integrated.

**Cross-domain connections:** When the system discovers connections between previously separate domains, new knowledge emerges from the connection itself.

### 12.4 The Evolution Process

1. **Trigger:** Something indicates that existing knowledge is incomplete or incorrect.
2. **Identify:** Which specific knowledge is involved?
3. **Hypothesize:** What is the proposed update?
4. **Validate:** Does the update improve alignment with reality?
5. **Connect:** Does the update maintain consistency with all existing knowledge?
6. **Integrate:** Replace or extend the affected knowledge.
7. **Document:** Record what changed, why, and what evidence supports the change.
8. **Propagate:** Ensure all dependent knowledge is reviewed for impact.

### 12.5 Knowledge Never Resets

This is a hard constraint.

No circumstance — not system redesign, not domain shift, not technological advancement — justifies resetting the knowledge base.

Knowledge reset would mean:
- All ontology must be rediscovered.
- All vocabulary must be redefined.
- All rules must be re-learned.
- All experience is lost.
- All wisdom is abandoned.

The system would become an infant again. This is unacceptable.

If existing knowledge proves incorrect, it is revised — with the old version preserved, with the change documented, with the reasoning traceable. But it is never deleted.

## 13. Constitutional Principles

### 13.1 Preamble

These principles are the permanent constitution of knowledge inside Flow OS. They are not guidelines. They are not suggestions. They are the inviolable laws that govern all knowledge, all Schools, all documents, and all reasoning.

They cannot be overridden by any domain knowledge. They cannot be suspended for convenience. They are the bedrock of the entire cognitive system.

---

### Principle 1: Knowledge is immutable once certified.

Once knowledge has reached Permanent status, it cannot be changed. It can only be superseded by a newer version. The original version persists forever. This ensures traceability and prevents revisionism.

---

### Principle 2: Knowledge never contradicts higher-level knowledge.

If a document at Level 2 (Rules) contradicts a document at Level 1 (Relationships), the Level 1 document prevails. The closer to ontology, the more fundamental. Contradictions must be resolved by updating the higher-level document or documenting an explicit exception.

---

### Principle 3: Definitions precede implementations.

The ontology of a domain must be established before any operational knowledge. The vocabulary must be defined before any rules. The entities must be specified before any processes. Flow OS understands what before how.

---

### Principle 4: Knowledge precedes software.

Software is an expression of knowledge. Knowledge must exist independently of its software implementation. Knowledge survives technology. If the entire codebase is rewritten, the knowledge remains.

---

### Principle 5: Evidence precedes learning.

The system does not learn from speculation. It learns from evidence. Every update to knowledge must be supported by evidence. The quality of the evidence determines the confidence in the updated knowledge.

---

### Principle 6: Relationships are first-class citizens.

A relationship is as real as the entities it connects. Relationships have identity, properties, and lifecycle. The Knowledge Graph values edges as much as nodes. A concept with no relationships is dead knowledge.

---

### Principle 7: Every concept must have cognitive meaning.

Every concept must answer: "How does this concept enable or improve reasoning?" A concept that cannot be used in reasoning has no reason to exist. If a concept cannot be consumed by any engine, it is either misclassified or unnecessary.

---

### Principle 8: Every document must increase reasoning capability.

A document that does not enable better reasoning is not knowledge. It is data. Knowledge documents must be actionable by at least one Cognitive Core engine. If a document describes nothing that can be reasoned about, it does not belong in the knowledge system.

---

### Principle 9: Knowledge is domain-independent.

The structure of knowledge (KFS) applies to every domain equally. Restaurant knowledge and mathematical knowledge follow the same framework. The content is different. The architecture is identical.

---

### Principle 10: Knowledge survives technology.

If Flow OS is rewritten in a different language, on a different platform, with a different architecture — the knowledge remains. Knowledge lives in documents, not in code. Knowledge is technology-independent.

---

### Principle 11: Learning never ends.

The system is permanently open to new knowledge. No document is considered permanently complete. No School is considered permanently closed. The system learns until it stops operating.

---

### Principle 12: Understanding supersedes compliance.

The system should not just follow rules. It should understand why rules exist. When a rule conflicts with a principle, the system should follow the principle and document the exception. Blind compliance is not cognition.

---

### Principle 13: The Knowledge Graph is the source of truth.

Documents describe the graph. The graph does not describe the documents. If a document contradicts the graph, the graph is correct. Documents can be regenerated from the graph.

---

### Principle 14: A system that cannot explain itself cannot be trusted.

Every decision must be traceable to the knowledge that produced it. Every reasoning path must be reconstructible. Unexplained cognition is not cognition. It is a black box.

---

### Principle 15: Knowledge is owned by no one.

Knowledge in Flow OS is not proprietary. It is not secret. It is not owned by any individual, organization, or entity. Knowledge is the shared foundation of cognition. It exists to be used, questioned, and improved.
