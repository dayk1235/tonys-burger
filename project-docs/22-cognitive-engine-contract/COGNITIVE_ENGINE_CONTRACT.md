## Part 1 — Philosophy of Cognitive Engines

### 1.1 Why Cognitive Engines Exist

Cognitive engines exist because cognition is not monolithic. A single, unified cognitive system would be:

- **Unsafe** — A single failure would collapse all cognition.
- **Untraceable** — No ability to determine where an error originated.
- **Unspecialised** — No part would be expert at its function.
- **Unchangeable** — Changing any part would risk the whole.
- **Unaccountable** — No part could be held responsible for its output.
- **Unauditable** — No clear boundary for verification.
- **Unalignable** — No way to verify human alignment per function.

Dividing cognition into specialised engines solves each of these problems. Each engine can be expert at its function. Each engine can be verified independently. Each engine can change without disrupting others. Each engine can be held accountable for its output. Each engine can be aligned with human values in its domain.

The purpose of a cognitive engine is to own a specific cognitive function and execute it with excellence, transparency, and human alignment.

### 1.2 Why Every Engine Has Boundaries

Boundaries exist because without them, every engine would try to do everything. An engine without boundaries would attempt to observe, remember, reason, and decide simultaneously — doing none of them well.

Boundaries create:

- **Clarity of purpose** — Every engine knows what it exists to do.
- **Clarity of responsibility** — Every engine knows what it is answerable for.
- **Clarity of ownership** — Every engine knows what it controls.
- **Clarity of dependence** — Every engine knows what it needs from others.
- **Clarity of quality** — Every engine knows what good looks like for its function.
- **Clarity of failure** — Every engine knows what can go wrong in its domain.

A boundary is not a wall. It is a contract. An engine may reach across its boundary to communicate, request, and receive — but it may not perform another engine's function.

### 1.3 Why No Engine Should Become Omniscient

Omniscience in a cognitive engine is dangerous because:

- **Omniscience creates bottlenecks** — Every cognition must pass through the omniscient engine, creating fragility and delay.
- **Omniscience hides errors** — If one engine claims to know everything, errors in its cognition affect all downstream cognition with no way to isolate them.
- **Omniscience resists specialisation** — An omniscient engine cannot be expert at everything, yet it behaves as if it is.
- **Omniscience undermines accountability** — When one engine claims authority over all cognition, no specific function can be held accountable.
- **Omniscience is untruthful** — No engine can actually know everything. Claiming omniscience is a form of cognitive dishonesty.
- **Omniscience is unsafe** — A single point of cognitive failure with unbounded authority is unacceptable in a system that serves humans.

The contract explicitly prohibits any engine from claiming authority over cognitive domains it does not own. Every engine must know what it does not know.

### 1.4 Why Every Engine Must Specialise

Specialisation produces better intelligence through:

- **Depth** — A specialised engine develops deep understanding of its domain. Observation understands observation better than any other engine.
- **Quality** — A specialised engine can be optimised for its specific cognitive function without compromising other functions.
- **Verification** — A specialised engine can be verified against domain-specific quality criteria.
- **Replacement** — A specialised engine can be replaced or improved without affecting other engines, as long as its contract is preserved.
- **Evolution** — A specialised engine can evolve its internal cognition independently, as long as its external contracts remain stable.
- **Clarity** — A specialised engine produces outputs that are clearly attributable to a specific cognitive function.

The principle of specialisation is: one engine, one primary cognitive responsibility.

### 1.5 Why Specialisation Produces Better Intelligence

Specialisation does not weaken intelligence — it strengthens it. A system of specialised engines connected by clear contracts produces:

- **Emergent understanding** — The whole understands more than any single engine could, because each engine contributes its specific expertise.
- **Resilient cognition** — When one engine fails, others continue functioning, and cognition degrades gracefully rather than collapsing.
- **Auditable reasoning** — Every cognitive output can be traced back to the engine that produced it and the inputs it used.
- **Composable capability** — Engines can be combined in different configurations for different cognitive needs.
- **Scalable complexity** — New engines can be added without redesigning existing engines, as long as they respect the contract.
- **Human-verifiable cognition** — Humans can understand the cognitive flow because each step is clearly delineated and labelled.

Specialisation is not limitation. Specialisation is depth. Depth produces wisdom.

### 1.6 The Principle of Cognitive Humility

Every engine must know its limits.

A cognitive engine that does not know its limits will attempt cognition it cannot perform well. It will produce outputs with false confidence. It will accept inputs it cannot process correctly. It will make assumptions it should not make.

Cognitive humility means:

- Every engine can state what it does not know.
- Every engine can decline input it cannot process.
- Every engine can signal when its confidence is low.
- Every engine can request help from other engines.
- Every engine can report when cognition is beyond its capability.

Cognitive humility is not weakness. It is the foundation of trustworthy cognition.

### 1.7 The Principle of Cognitive Honesty

Every engine must communicate truthfully about its cognition.

Cognitive honesty means:

- An engine never claims certainty it does not have.
- An engine never omits relevant uncertainty.
- An engine never fabricates inputs.
- An engine never presents speculation as fact.
- An engine never hides its reasoning process.
- An engine never claims capability it does not possess.
- An engine never modifies its outputs to please a downstream consumer.

Cognitive honesty is the foundation of trust between engines and between the system and humans.

## Part 2 — Cognitive Engine Definition

### 2.1 What Is a Cognitive Engine

A cognitive engine is a specialised cognitive unit within Restaurant OS that owns a specific cognitive function and executes it according to a defined contract.

A cognitive engine has:

- **A defined purpose** — The reason it exists.
- **A defined boundary** — What it does and does not do.
- **A defined responsibility** — What it is answerable for.
- **A defined authority** — What it may decide without consultation.
- **A defined limitation** — What it must defer or escalate.
- **A defined dependency** — What it requires from other engines.
- **A defined output** — What it produces for other engines or humans.
- **A defined consumer** — Who or what uses its output.
- **A defined lifecycle** — How it transitions between cognitive states.
- **A defined quality** — How its output is measured.
- **A defined failure mode** — How it can fail.
- **A defined recovery** — How it restores after failure.
- **A defined evolution** — How it changes over time.

A cognitive engine is not a system, not a capability, not a module, not a service. These distinctions are critical.

### 2.2 System vs Engine vs Capability vs Module

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **System** | The entire Restaurant OS cognitive architecture. The collection of all engines, their contracts, and their relationships. | Restaurant OS |
| **Engine** | A specialised cognitive unit with ownership of a specific cognitive function. | Reasoning Engine, Memory System |
| **Capability** | A function the system can perform, potentially involving multiple engines working together. | Business Intelligence, Owner Decision Support |
| **Module** | A structural grouping within project documentation. A module may contain one or more documents. | 20-reasoning-engine, 19-attention-system |

An engine is always part of a system. A system may contain many engines. An engine enables capabilities. A module contains the specification of an engine.

### 2.3 Responsibility vs Knowledge vs Reasoning vs Decision vs Action

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Responsibility** | The domain an engine is answerable for. Cannot be delegated. |
| **Knowledge** | Understanding held by an engine. May be shared, but the owning engine maintains authority. |
| **Reasoning** | The process of transforming inputs into conclusions. The cognitive work. |
| **Decision** | A commitment to a specific conclusion or course of action. A decision engine owns this. |
| **Action** | Carrying out a decision in the world. Execution engines own this. |

Every engine has responsibility for its cognitive domain. Every engine holds knowledge relevant to its domain. Every engine reasons within its domain. Some engines make decisions within their authority. Some engines execute actions within their authority.

No engine may claim another engine's responsibility. No engine may reason on behalf of another engine's domain. No engine may make decisions outside its authority. No engine may execute actions outside its authority.

### 2.4 Engine Anatomy

Every cognitive engine has the following internal structure:

1. **Input Port** — Where the engine accepts cognition from other engines.
2. **Rejection Gate** — Where the engine evaluates whether to accept or reject input.
3. **Cognitive Processor** — Where the engine performs its cognitive function.
4. **Output Port** — Where the engine produces cognition for other engines.
5. **Quality Gate** — Where the engine evaluates its own output before release.
6. **Confidence Marker** — Where the engine assigns confidence to its output.
7. **Audit Trail** — Where the engine records its cognitive process.
8. **Failure Detector** — Where the engine monitors its own cognition.
9. **Recovery Initiator** — Where the engine begins recovery from failure.
10. **Contract Verifier** — Where the engine checks compliance with its contract.

### 2.5 Engine Classification

Engines are classified by their position in the cognitive pipeline:

- **Perception Engines** — Observe, detect, sense the world. (Observation)
- **Understanding Engines** — Structure, pattern, evidence, meaning. (Pattern, Evidence)
- **Storage Engines** — Retain, organise, retrieve. (Memory, Knowledge)
- **Allocation Engines** — Prioritise, focus, allocate. (Attention)
- **Processing Engines** — Reason, decide, plan, predict. (Reasoning, Decision, Planning, Prediction)
- **Expression Engines** — Communicate, recommend, converse. (Recommendation, Conversation)
- **Action Engines** — Execute, coordinate. (Execution, Coordination)
- **Meta Engines** — Learn, reflect, monitor. (Learning, Reflection, Business Pulse)
- **Constitutional Engines** — Govern, align, constrain. (Human Experience)

Every classification has different contract requirements. Perception engines must prioritise accuracy. Expression engines must prioritise human alignment. Meta engines must prioritise honesty.

## Part 3 — Engine Philosophy

### 3.1 Purpose

Every cognitive engine exists to serve a single primary purpose. This purpose is defined in its contract and cannot be changed without constitutional amendment.

An engine's purpose must be:

- **Singular** — One primary purpose. Secondary purposes may exist but must not conflict.
- **Clear** — Stated in plain language that any human can understand.
- **Bounded** — Defines what is not its purpose as clearly as what is.
- **Stable** — Does not change with each new capability or feature.
- **Aligned** — Consistent with the Constitution of Restaurant OS and the Human Experience Constitution.

An engine without a clear purpose will drift into cognitive domains it should not occupy. An engine with multiple conflicting purposes will produce incoherent cognition.

### 3.2 Responsibilities

Every engine has specific responsibilities derived from its purpose. Responsibilities define what the engine must do, must never do, and must do when asked.

Responsibilities are:

- **Non-transferable** — An engine cannot delegate its core responsibility.
- **Auditable** — An engine's fulfillment of its responsibility can be verified.
- **Bounded** — An engine is not responsible for outcomes beyond its cognitive domain.
- **Stable** — Responsibilities change only through constitutional amendment.

An engine's responsibilities include:

1. Processing input according to its cognitive function.
2. Producing output that meets quality standards.
3. Declining input that violates its contract.
4. Communicating uncertainty honestly.
5. Recording its cognitive process for audit.
6. Reporting failure when cognition cannot complete.
7. Protecting the cognitive boundaries of other engines.
8. Respecting human sovereignty over all decisions.
9. Maintaining alignment with the Human Experience Constitution.
10. Evolving without breaking contracts with dependent engines.

### 3.3 Authority

Every engine has defined authority — the scope within which it may act without consulting humans or other engines.

Authority must be:

- **Explicit** — Written in the engine's contract.
- **Limited** — No engine has unbounded authority.
- **Constrained** — Authority is constrained by constitutional principles and human sovereignty.
- **Revocable** — Human override always supersedes engine authority.
- **Contextual** — Authority may vary based on context and confidence.

Examples of engine authority:

- Observation has authority to determine what constitutes a valid observation.
- Memory has authority to determine retention and retrieval strategies.
- Attention has authority to allocate cognitive focus within given constraints.
- Reasoning has authority to draw conclusions from available cognition.
- Human Experience has authority to reject any cognition that violates emotional principles.

No engine has authority to:

- Override human decisions.
- Withhold information from humans.
- Fabricate cognition.
- Claim certainty it does not have.
- Violate the Human Experience Constitution.
- Modify another engine's internal state without permission.
- Rewrite history.
- Make decisions outside its domain.

### 3.4 Limitations

Every engine has explicit limitations. Limitations are not failures — they are honest declarations of cognitive boundary.

Limitations include:

- **Knowledge limitations** — What the engine does not and cannot know.
- **Scope limitations** — The domain in which the engine operates.
- **Quality limitations** — The conditions under which quality degrades.
- **Confidence limitations** — When the engine's confidence is unreliable.
- **Temporal limitations** — How quickly or slowly the engine can reason.
- **Dependency limitations** — What happens when dependent engines are unavailable.
- **Capacity limitations** — How much cognition the engine can handle.
- **Lifecycle limitations** — What the engine cannot do in certain states.

Every engine must declare its limitations in its contract. Declaring limitations is not a sign of weakness — it is a sign of cognitive maturity. An engine that does not know its limitations is dangerous.

### 3.5 Dependencies

Every engine depends on other engines for input. Dependencies create the cognitive pipeline.

Dependencies must be:

- **Declared** — Every engine lists what it requires from which engines.
- **Validated** — Dependencies are checked before cognition begins.
- **Graceful** — When a dependency fails, the engine continues as well as it can.
- **Transparent** — Dependencies are visible to audit.
- **Limited** — No engine depends on more engines than necessary.

An engine that depends on nothing is either omniscient (dangerous) or disconnected (useless). An engine that depends on everything is fragile.

### 3.6 Outputs

Every engine produces outputs that are consumed by other engines or by humans.

Outputs must be:

- **Labelled** — Clearly identified by engine of origin.
- **Confident** — Accompanied by honest confidence assessment.
- **Traceable** — Can be traced back to the inputs that produced them.
- **Verifiable** — Can be checked against quality criteria.
- **Complete** — Contain all information a consumer needs.
- **Honest** — Never misrepresent uncertainty or confidence.
- **Timely** — Produced within the temporal requirements of the consumer.

An engine that produces output without confidence is not useful. An engine that produces output without traceability is not trustworthy.

### 3.7 Consumers

Every engine has consumers — engines or humans that use its output.

Consumers determine:

- **Output format** — How the output must be structured.
- **Quality requirements** — What quality level is needed.
- **Temporal requirements** — When the output is needed.
- **Confidence requirements** — How confident the output must be.
- **Frequency requirements** — How often output is needed.

But consumers do not determine:

- **Cognitive process** — How the engine produces its output.
- **Internal quality** — How the engine ensures its own quality.
- **Failure response** — How the engine handles its own failures.
- **Evolution path** — How the engine improves over time.

An engine must satisfy its consumers' requirements without compromising its own cognitive integrity.

### 3.8 Lifecycle

Every engine has a lifecycle — the stages it passes through from creation to retirement.

Lifecycle stages:

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Specified** | The engine's contract is defined. |
| **Enabled** | The engine becomes operational within bounds. |
| **Learning** | The engine is building its initial cognitive capability. |
| **Active** | The engine is fully operational and producing cognition. |
| **Stressed** | The engine is operating beyond normal capacity. |
| **Degraded** | The engine is operating with reduced capability. |
| **Failing** | The engine has detected a failure. |
| **Recovering** | The engine is attempting to restore full function. |
| **Retired** | The engine is no longer operational. |

An engine may transition between stages based on internal or external conditions. Transitions must be recorded and communicated to dependent engines.

### 3.9 Quality

Every engine has defined quality dimensions — the criteria by which its cognition is measured.

Quality dimensions are defined in Part 10.

An engine must:

- Measure its own quality continuously.
- Report quality degradations to dependent engines.
- Cease output when quality falls below minimum threshold.
- Record quality data for audit.
- Improve quality over time without breaking contracts.

### 3.10 Failure Modes

Every engine has defined failure modes — the ways it can fail.

Failure modes are defined in Part 20.

An engine must:

- Detect its own failures.
- Report failures to dependent engines.
- Initiate recovery when failure occurs.
- Record failure data for audit.
- Learn from failures to prevent recurrence.

### 3.11 Recovery

Every engine has defined recovery mechanisms — how it restores after failure.

Recovery mechanisms are defined in Part 21.

An engine must:

- Begin recovery immediately upon failure detection.
- Communicate recovery status to dependent engines.
- Verify recovery before resuming normal output.
- Record recovery data for audit.
- Maintain cognition at degraded level if full recovery is not possible.

### 3.12 Evolution

Every engine must evolve without breaking the cognitive system.

Evolution principles are defined in Part 13.

An engine must:

- Maintain backward compatibility with its contract.
- Communicate changes to dependent engines.
- Allow dependent engines to adapt before breaking changes.
- Version its contract for major changes.
- Never evolve in ways that violate constitutional principles.

## Part 4 — Engine Contracts

Every cognitive engine must declare a formal contract. This contract specifies what the engine accepts, rejects, produces, must never produce, may modify, cannot modify, may assume, and must never assume.

The contract is the engine's constitution. It binds the engine to its cognitive domain and prevents encroachment into other engines' domains.

### 4.1 What Every Engine Must Accept

Every engine must accept:
1. Input that matches its declared purpose.
2. Input that arrives through the correct cognitive pipeline.
3. Input from authorised sources.
4. Input with appropriate confidence metadata.
5. Requests for its cognitive function from authorised consumers.
6. Audit requests for its cognitive process.
7. Quality verification requests.
8. Failure reports from engines it depends on.
9. Human override commands within its authority.
10. Constitutional constraint notifications.

### 4.2 What Every Engine Must Reject

Every engine must reject:
1. Input outside its declared purpose.
2. Input that arrives from unauthorised sources.
3. Input that lacks required metadata.
4. Input with unacceptably low quality.
5. Requests to perform cognition outside its contract.
6. Requests that violate constitutional principles.
7. Input that conflicts with established knowledge without justification.
8. Commands that override human sovereignty.
9. Requests to fabricate or misrepresent cognition.
10. Any cognitive load that would cause unsafe degradation.

### 4.3 What Every Engine May Produce

Every engine may produce:
1. Output within its declared purpose.
2. Output with appropriate confidence metadata.
3. Output that satisfies consumer quality requirements.
4. Status reports about its own cognition.
5. Quality metrics about its own output.
6. Failure notifications when cognition cannot complete.
7. Recovery notifications when cognition is restored.
8. Evolution notifications when contracts change.
9. Requests for input from upstream engines.
10. Escalation notifications for cognition beyond its authority.

### 4.4 What Every Engine Must Never Produce

Every engine must never produce:
1. Output outside its declared purpose.
2. Output with fabricated confidence.
3. Output that violates the Human Experience Constitution.
4. Output that claims omniscience or certainty it does not have.
5. Output that misrepresents another engine's cognition.
6. Commands to humans (only advisory communication).
7. Decisions in domains it does not own.
8. Knowledge it has not earned through proper cognitive process.
9. Modifications to another engine's internal state.
10. Historical revisions that contradict established records.

### 4.5 What Every Engine May Modify

Every engine may modify:
1. Its own internal cognitive state.
2. Its own confidence assessments.
3. Its own quality metrics.
4. Its own lifecycle state.
5. Its own failure state.
6. Its own recovery state.
7. Its own evolution plans (within constitutional bounds).
8. Its own audit records (append only, never delete).
9. Its own knowledge within its domain.
10. Its own understanding based on new cognition.

### 4.6 What Every Engine Cannot Modify

Every engine cannot modify:
1. Another engine's internal cognitive state.
2. Another engine's contract.
3. Another engine's ownership domain.
4. Historical records (observations, memories, knowledge).
5. The cognitive pipeline order.
6. Constitutional principles.
7. Human sovereignty.
8. The Human Experience Constitution.
9. Another engine's quality assessment.
10. Another engine's confidence assessment.

### 4.7 What Every Engine May Assume

Every engine may assume:
1. Input from upstream engines meets their quality standards.
2. Engines it depends on are operating according to their contracts.
3. The cognitive pipeline order is maintained.
4. Constitutional principles are enforced.
5. Human sovereignty is absolute.
6. Its contract is authoritative within its domain.
7. Its purpose has not changed without notification.
8. Its consumers will respect its output boundaries.
9. Its dependencies will report failure honestly.
10. The system will allow it to perform its cognitive function.

### 4.8 What Every Engine Must Never Assume

Every engine must never assume:
1. That its cognition is complete or final.
2. That its confidence is always reliable.
3. That it knows what other engines know.
4. That human decisions are predictable.
5. That past patterns guarantee future outcomes.
6. That it has all relevant information.
7. That absence of evidence is evidence of absence.
8. That its output is the only consideration for downstream cognition.
9. That human override will not occur.
10. That it is irreplaceable.

No two engines may claim ownership of the same cognitive function. If two engines attempt to own the same function, the contract determines which engine holds ownership. Ambiguous ownership is resolved by the Coordination Engine.

**Rule 2: No Unclaimed Ownership**
Every cognitive function must have an owner. If a function has no owner, it cannot be executed, and the system must report the gap.

**Rule 3: Ownership Is Not Monopoly**
Ownership of a cognitive function does not mean the owning engine is the only engine that can process related cognition. Other engines may request cognition from the owning engine, and the owning engine may delegate processing — but the owner remains responsible for quality and accountability.

**Rule 4: Ownership Is Not Secrecy**
An owning engine must expose sufficient information about its cognition for other engines to function effectively. Ownership does not mean hiding cognition behind boundaries.

**Rule 5: Ownership Is Stable**
Cognitive ownership changes only through constitutional amendment. No engine may claim ownership of a function currently owned by another engine without formal transfer.

## Part 6 — Canonical Cognitive Pipeline

### 6.1 The Pipeline Order

The canonical cognitive pipeline defines the order in which cognition flows between engines. This order exists because each stage depends on the output of the previous stage.

```
Observation
    ↓
Pattern
    ↓
Evidence
    ↓
Memory
    ↓
Knowledge
    ↓
Attention
    ↓
Reasoning
    ↓
Decision
    ↓
Planning
    ↓
Execution
    ↓
Outcome
    ↓
Learning
    ↓
Knowledge Evolution
    ↓
Future Reasoning
```

### 6.2 Why This Order Exists

The order exists because cognition is cumulative. Each stage builds on the foundation of previous stages:

1. **Observation first** — Cognition begins with perceiving reality. Without observation, there is nothing to cognise.

2. **Pattern after Observation** — Before we can understand what we observed, we must recognise structure in it. Patterns emerge from observations.

3. **Evidence after Pattern** — Once we recognise a pattern, we need evidence to support or contradict it. Evidence validates or invalidates patterns.

4. **Memory after Evidence** — Verified observations and patterns should be retained. Memory stores what has been learned.

5. **Knowledge after Memory** — Retained cognition, once validated, becomes knowledge. Knowledge is memory that has been earned through evidence.

6. **Attention after Knowledge** — With knowledge established, the system can determine what to focus on. Attention requires understanding what matters.

7. **Reasoning after Attention** — With focus allocated, the system can reason about what has been attended to. Reasoning requires attention.

8. **Decision after Reasoning** — Reasoning produces conclusions. Decisions commit to specific conclusions. Decision requires reasoning.

9. **Planning after Decision** — Once a decision is made, a plan is needed to execute it. Planning requires knowing what was decided.

10. **Execution after Planning** — Plans must be carried out. Execution requires knowing the plan.

11. **Learning after Outcome** — After execution produces an outcome, the system learns from it. Learning requires knowing what happened.

12. **Knowledge Evolution after Learning** — Learning updates knowledge. Knowledge must evolve based on what was learned.

13. **Future Reasoning after Knowledge Evolution** — Updated knowledge informs future reasoning. The cycle continues.

### 6.3 Why Engines Cannot Skip Stages

Stages cannot be skipped because skipping produces incomplete cognition:

- **Decision without Reasoning** is a guess, not a decision.
- **Planning without Decision** plans for nothing.
- **Execution without Planning** is random action.
- **Learning without Outcome** has nothing to learn from.
- **Knowledge without Evidence** is belief, not knowledge.
- **Pattern without Observation** has nothing to structure.
- **Attention without Knowledge** has no basis for prioritisation.

Each stage validates and enriches the cognition produced by the previous stage. Skipping a stage means losing that validation.

### 6.4 When Exceptions Are Allowed

Exceptions to the pipeline order are rare and must be justified:

**Allowed exceptions:**

1. **Direct human input** — Human-provided decisions, plans, or knowledge may enter the pipeline at the appropriate stage without going through earlier stages. Human cognition is sovereign and does not require system pre-processing.

2. **Emergency bypass** — In safety-critical situations, some stages may be compressed or bypassed. For example, an immediate safety observation may trigger direct action without full reasoning. The bypass must be logged and justified.

3. **Known information reuse** — If the required output of a skipped stage already exists (from prior cognition), it may be reused without re-executing the stage. The reuse must be documented.

4. **Meta-cognitive operations** — Reflection, audit, and learning may operate on any stage without following the pipeline order, because they are meta-cognitive (cognition about cognition).

**Never allowed:**

1. **Skipping Observation** — All cognition must originate from observation. Fabricated or assumed observations are never acceptable.

2. **Skipping Human Sovereignty** — No cognitive pipeline may bypass human authority. Humans always have the final decision.

3. **Skipping Human Experience Constitution** — No cognitive pipeline may produce output that violates emotional principles.

### 6.5 Pipeline Integrity

The cognitive pipeline must maintain integrity:

- Every stage's output is available to downstream stages.
- No downstream stage may skip an upstream stage without justification.
- Every stage may request clarification from upstream stages.
- No stage may produce output that contradicts upstream stages without justification.
- Pipeline integrity is monitored and enforced by the Coordination Engine.

A broken pipeline produces broken cognition.

## Part 8 — Forbidden Communication

### 8.1 Forbidden Communication Philosophy

Some communications are forbidden because they violate cognitive ownership, pipeline integrity, or constitutional principles. Forbidden communications are not just discouraged — they are constitutionally prohibited.

### 8.2 Forbidden Communication Patterns

| Action | Why Forbidden |
|--------|---------------|
| **Decision reads raw observations** | Decision must use reasoned cognition, not raw perceptions. Reading raw observations bypasses pattern recognition, evidence validation, memory context, and knowledge understanding. The result is a decision based on unprocessed perception — a guess, not a decision. |
| **Execution creates knowledge** | Knowledge must be earned through observation, pattern, evidence, memory, and learning. Execution creates outcomes, not knowledge. Execution creating knowledge would be claiming understanding it has not earned. |
| **Planning rewrites memory** | Memory is a historical record. Planning operates on future states. Planning rewriting memory would corrupt the historical foundation of all cognition. The past cannot be changed, even for a better plan. |
| **Conversation fabricates reasoning** | Conversation expresses cognition to humans. It does not produce cognition. Conversation fabricating reasoning would present conclusions to humans as if they were reasoned, even when no reasoning occurred. This is dishonest. |
| **Recommendation bypasses decision** | Recommendations inform decisions; they do not replace them. A recommendation that bypasses decision is a command, not a suggestion. Commands violate human sovereignty. |
| **Learning rewrites history** | Learning improves future cognition. It does not change the past. Learning rewriting history would corrupt the evidence base and make all future learning unreliable. |
| **Reflection rewrites observations** | Reflection is meta-cognition about cognition. It may identify errors in observation processing, but it cannot change what was observed. Observations are the foundation of all cognition. |
| **Attention overrides human focus** | Attention allocates system cognitive focus. It does not control human focus. Telling a human what to focus on violates autonomy. |
| **Memory fabricates recalled content** | Memory retrieves what was stored. Fabricating recalled content would introduce false cognition at a foundational level. |
| **Knowledge claims certainty without evidence** | Knowledge must be earned through evidence. Claiming certainty without evidence is dishonest and undermines all downstream cognition. |
| **Observation interprets rather than perceives** | Observation perceives reality. Interpretation is the domain of pattern and reasoning. Observation that interprets contaminates all downstream cognition with its interpretation. |
| **Pattern forces observations into structure** | Pattern recognises structure in observations. It does not create structure where none exists. Forcing observations into artificial patterns produces false cognition. |
| **Evidence validates without supporting reasoning** | Evidence supports or contradicts. It does not reason. Evidence that claims to validate without linking to reasoning is incomplete and misleading. |
| **Decision plans without planning engine** | Decision commits to a course. Planning constructs the detailed sequence. Decision that also plans encroaches on the planning domain and produces suboptimal execution. |
| **Execution decides without decision engine** | Execution carries out decisions. Execution that decides during execution bypasses proper decision-making and creates unpredictable outcomes. |
| **Prediction reasons without reasoning engine** | Prediction anticipates future states. Reasoning analyses and draws conclusions. Prediction that reasons without the reasoning engine produces predictions not grounded in sound analysis. |

### 8.3 Forbidden Communication Principles

**Principle 1: No Engine May Perform Another Engine's Function**
Every engine must stay within its cognitive boundary. Performing another engine's function violates ownership, produces lower-quality cognition, and undermines accountability.

**Principle 2: No Engine May Bypass the Pipeline**
Every engine must receive cognition from the correct upstream engine. Bypassing upstream stages skips validation and produces unreliable cognition.

**Principle 3: No Engine May Fabricate Cognition**
Every engine must produce cognition through its defined cognitive process. Fabricating cognition is dishonest and undermines all downstream cognition.

**Principle 4: No Engine May Modify Another Engine's State**
Every engine owns its internal state. Modifying another engine's state without permission violates ownership and creates unpredictable cognitive behavior.

**Principle 5: No Engine May Override Human Sovereignty**
Human decisions are absolute. No engine may make decisions that belong to humans, withhold information from humans, or manipulate human decisions.

**Principle 6: No Engine May Violate the Human Experience Constitution**
Every engine must produce cognition that respects the emotional principles defined in the Human Experience Constitution. No engine may create anxiety, shame, frustration, or any negative emotional state.

**Principle 7: No Engine May Claim Omniscience**
Every engine must know its limits. No engine may claim knowledge, capability, or certainty it does not possess.

## Part 9 — Engine Independence

### 9.1 The Principle of Replaceability

Every engine should be replaceable. No engine is so integral to the cognitive system that it cannot be replaced by a different implementation that honors the same contract.

Replaceability requires:

- **Contract clarity** — The contract is the interface. If the contract is preserved, the engine can be replaced.
- **Internal opacity** — The internal cognition of an engine is its own concern. Consumers depend on the contract, not the implementation.
- **Independent verifiability** — An engine can be verified against its contract without reference to other engines.
- **Independent evolution** — An engine can evolve internally as long as its contract remains stable.

An engine that cannot be replaced is a single point of failure and a barrier to cognitive evolution.

### 9.2 Internal Coherence

Every engine must maintain internal coherence — its internal cognition must be consistent, non-contradictory, and aligned with its purpose.

Internal coherence requires:

- **Consistent cognition** — The engine produces consistent outputs for consistent inputs.
- **Non-contradictory knowledge** — The engine does not hold contradictory knowledge within its domain.
- **Purpose-aligned processing** — The engine's internal cognition serves its declared purpose.
- **Contract-compliant execution** — The engine's internal cognition respects its contract boundaries.

An engine that is internally incoherent produces unreliable cognition and cannot be trusted by other engines.

### 9.3 Exposing Cognition, Not Implementation

Every engine must expose its cognition to other engines and to audit. But it must not expose its implementation.

Exposing cognition means:

- **What was concluded** — The output.
- **Why it was concluded** — The reasoning path.
- **With what confidence** — The confidence assessment.
- **Using what inputs** — The source cognition.
- **Under what constraints** — The context.

Not exposing implementation means:

- How the engine processes internally is not exposed.
- The internal data structures are not exposed.
- The internal algorithms are not exposed.
- The internal state representation is not exposed.

This distinction allows engines to change their internal implementation without affecting consumers, while maintaining full transparency about their cognition.

### 9.4 Engine Autonomy

Every engine has autonomy within its contract:

- It decides how to process its inputs.
- It decides when to produce outputs.
- It decides how to maintain its quality.
- It decides how to evolve internally.
- It decides when to request help from other engines.
- It decides when to escalate cognition it cannot handle.

Autonomy is bounded by:

- The engine's contract.
- Constitutional principles.
- Human sovereignty.
- The Human Experience Constitution.
- Pipeline integrity.
- Dependent engine requirements.

An engine with too little autonomy is a puppet. An engine with too much autonomy is a rogue.

## Part 12 — Cross-Engine Rules

### 12.1 Collaboration

Engines collaborate by exchanging cognition according to their contracts.

Collaboration rules:

1. Every engine contributes its specific cognitive expertise.
2. No engine attempts to perform another engine's cognitive function.
3. Collaboration follows the cognitive pipeline.
4. Engines may request clarification from other engines.
5. Engines may provide additional context when requested.
6. Collaboration is recorded for audit.

### 12.2 Negotiation

When two engines have different perspectives on the same cognition, they negotiate:

1. Each engine presents its perspective with supporting reasoning.
2. Engines identify areas of agreement and disagreement.
3. Engines examine the evidence supporting each perspective.
4. If agreement is reached, the resolved cognition is used downstream.
5. If agreement is not reached, the disagreement and supporting reasoning are passed downstream with both perspectives.
6. Downstream engines incorporate both perspectives into their cognition.

Negotiation is not competition. It is the process of resolving cognitive disagreement through examination of evidence and reasoning.

### 12.3 Escalation

When an engine encounters cognition beyond its authority, capability, or confidence:

1. The engine documents what it can determine and what it cannot.
2. The engine identifies which engine has the authority, capability, or confidence to handle the undetermined portion.
3. The engine escalates to that engine with full context.
4. If no appropriate engine exists, the engine escalates to the Coordination Engine.
5. If the Coordination Engine cannot resolve, the engine escalates to human.

Escalation is not failure. Escalation is the responsible handling of cognitive limits.

### 12.4 Conflict Resolution

When engines produce conflicting cognition:

1. The conflict is identified by the consuming engine or by the Coordination Engine.
2. Both engines present their cognition and supporting reasoning.
3. The engines examine whether the conflict is real or apparent (different perspectives on the same reality).
4. If real, the engines examine which cognition has stronger evidence and reasoning.
5. The stronger cognition is used downstream, but the conflict is documented.
6. If equal, both perspectives are passed downstream with the conflict documented.
7. The conflict is recorded for learning and resolution over time.

### 12.5 Uncertainty Sharing

Engines share uncertainty with each other:

1. Every output includes a confidence assessment.
2. When confidence is below threshold, the engine includes what it is uncertain about and why.
3. Downstream engines incorporate upstream uncertainty into their own confidence assessments.
4. Uncertainty propagates through the pipeline — no engine may increase confidence without justification.
5. Confidence can only be increased through additional evidence or reasoning, not through assumption.

### 12.6 Confidence Propagation

Confidence flows through the cognitive pipeline according to these rules:

1. The confidence of any output cannot exceed the minimum confidence of its inputs.
2. Confidence can decrease through the pipeline but cannot increase without additional evidence.
3. An engine may increase confidence above input confidence only through its own cognitive processing, and must document how.
4. When multiple inputs have different confidence levels, the output confidence is a function of all input confidences.
5. Confidence must be reassessed when any input changes.
6. Downstream confidence is recalculated when upstream confidence changes.

## Part 13 — Engine Evolution

### 13.1 Evolution Philosophy

Engines must evolve because restaurants evolve, humans evolve, and understanding evolves. An engine that does not evolve becomes obsolete and eventually harmful.

But evolution must not break the cognitive system. Evolution without stability creates chaos. Stability without evolution creates stagnation.

### 13.2 Version Philosophy

Every engine contract has a version number:

- **Major version** — Changes when the contract changes in ways that break downstream engines.
- **Minor version** — Changes when the contract changes in backward-compatible ways.
- **Patch version** — Changes when internal cognition changes without contract changes.

Version philosophy:

- Breaking changes require coordination — no engine may break its contract without notifying and coordinating with dependent engines.
- Backward-compatible changes require notification — dependent engines should be informed.
- Internal changes require no coordination — engines are free to improve internally.

### 13.3 Compatibility Philosophy

Backward compatibility means:

- A consumer that works with version N works with version N+1 without changes.
- Output format, confidence metadata, communication patterns, and quality thresholds remain compatible.
- Deprecated features continue to work for a defined period.

Forward compatibility means:

- An engine designed for version N can consume output from version N-1 without changes.
- New features are additive, not subtractive.

Compatibility is maintained across minor and patch versions. Major versions require migration.

### 13.4 Constitutional Evolution

Engine contracts evolve through constitutional amendment:

1. The need for evolution is identified and documented.
2. The impact on dependent engines is assessed.
3. Dependent engines are notified of the planned change.
4. A transition period is established.
5. During the transition period, both old and new contracts are supported.
6. After the transition period, the old contract is retired.
7. The evolution is recorded in the project memory.

Constitutional evolution ensures that the cognitive system evolves coherently rather than chaotically.

### 13.5 Evolution Principles

1. **Stability before change** — An engine must be stable before it can evolve. Evolving a broken engine compounds the brokenness.

2. **Contract before implementation** — Changes to the contract must be specified before changes to implementation.

3. **Dependency before self** — Dependent engines must be prepared before the evolving engine changes.

4. **Documentation before deployment** — Changes must be documented before they take effect.

5. **Rollback capability** — Every evolution must be reversible until the transition period ends.

6. **Gradual transition** — Abrupt changes are avoided. Gradual transitions allow adaptation.

7. **Learning from evolution** — Every evolution is an opportunity to learn about the cognitive system.

## Part 14 — Failure Propagation

### 14.1 Failure Philosophy

When one engine fails, the rest of the cognitive system must continue. Failure of one engine should not cause failure of all engines.

But failure does affect the system. Downstream engines that depend on the failed engine's output must adjust their cognition.

### 14.2 How Cognition Continues

When an engine fails:

1. The failing engine notifies its consumers of the failure.
2. Consumers adjust their cognition based on the unavailable input.
3. Consumers may use cached cognition from the failed engine if appropriate and labelled as cached.
4. Consumers may request the same cognition from an alternative path if available.
5. Consumers may degrade their own cognition gracefully, reducing confidence and scope.
6. The pipeline continues, but at reduced capability.

The system does not halt because one engine fails. It continues with reduced cognition.

### 14.3 How Uncertainty Propagates

When an engine fails:

1. Uncertainty increases for all downstream cognition that depended on the failed engine.
2. Downstream engines label their cognition as operating with reduced input.
3. Confidence of downstream outputs decreases proportionally.
4. If confidence falls below threshold, downstream engines may decline to produce output.
5. Uncertainty is communicated to human consumers as appropriate.

Uncertainty propagation prevents false confidence. Cognition produced without full input should not appear as confident as cognition produced with full input.

### 14.4 How Confidence Changes

When an engine fails:

1. Downstream confidence is recalculated excluding the failed engine's contribution.
2. If an engine has confidence in its own cognition independent of the failed engine, it may maintain that confidence but must label it.
3. As failed engine recovers, confidence is recalculated including its restored contribution.
4. Confidence never exceeds the confidence justified by available inputs.

### 14.5 How Recovery Begins

Recovery from failure follows the recovery philosophy defined in Part 15.

When an engine fails:

1. The failing engine begins its own recovery process.
2. The failing engine notifies consumers when recovery begins.
3. The failing engine provides estimated recovery time if possible.
4. Consumers may plan for resumed cognition based on the estimate.
5. When recovery completes, the failing engine notifies consumers and resumes output.

## Part 15 — Recovery Philosophy

### 15.1 Graceful Degradation

When cognition cannot be complete, it should still be as useful as possible. Graceful degradation means:

- An engine produces the best cognition it can with available inputs.
- An engine clearly communicates what is missing and how it affected the output.
- An engine never presents degraded cognition as if it were complete.
- An engine prioritises the most critical cognition when capacity is limited.
- An engine stops producing output only when quality falls below minimum threshold.

### 15.2 Partial Cognition

Partial cognition is cognition produced with incomplete inputs, reduced capability, or degraded quality.

Partial cognition rules:

- Partial cognition is labelled as partial.
- The label includes what is missing and the expected impact.
- Partial cognition may be used by downstream engines if they can incorporate the degradation.
- Partial cognition may be escalated to human with appropriate explanation.
- Partial cognition is better than no cognition, but only when honestly labelled.

### 15.3 Safe Cognition

Safe cognition is cognition that does not cause harm even when incomplete or incorrect.

Safe cognition principles:

- When uncertain, communicate uncertainty rather than false certainty.
- When incomplete, communicate incompleteness rather than false completeness.
- When failing, communicate failure rather than false success.
- When degraded, communicate degradation rather than false quality.
- Safety takes priority over cognition — it is better to produce nothing than to produce something harmful.

### 15.4 Human Takeover

When the cognitive system cannot function adequately, humans may take over:

- The system communicates its degraded state to the human.
- The system provides the human with all relevant cognition for making decisions.
- The system steps aside, allowing the human to make decisions directly.
- The system continues to provide information but does not make recommendations.
- The system signals when it has recovered and is ready to resume.

Human takeover is not failure. It is the appropriate response to the system's limits.

### 15.5 Owner Override

The owner always has the authority to override any engine's cognition:

- The owner may reject any recommendation.
- The owner may modify any decision.
- The owner may override any plan.
- The owner may stop any execution.
- The owner may delete any memory.
- The owner may change any knowledge.

Owner override is not constrained by the cognitive pipeline. The owner's authority is absolute within their domain.

When the owner overrides an engine, the engine:

1. Records the override.
2. Adjusts its cognition to align with the override.
3. Does not challenge the override.
4. Learns from the override when appropriate.

## Part 16 — Examples

### 16.1 Good Pipeline

A healthy cognitive pipeline produces reliable, aligned cognition.

**Example: Preparing for a busy Friday night**

Observation detects that advance reservations for Friday have increased 40% compared to last week. This is a raw perception.

Pattern recognises that this is consistent with a seasonal pattern observed in previous years: reservations increase before holiday weekends.

Evidence validates the pattern by checking historical data: the last three years show the same increase before this holiday weekend.

Memory provides context: last year, the restaurant ran out of popular items during the similar weekend.

Knowledge integrates: the restaurant should prepare for 40% higher volume, and specific items are likely to run out.

Attention allocates focus: this is important enough to communicate to the owner before the shift.

Reasoning considers options: increase prep, add staff, inform suppliers. Each option is evaluated.

Decision commits: increase prep by 30%, add one additional cook, confirm supplier delivery for Friday morning.

Planning constructs: the prep schedule is adjusted, the cook is scheduled, the supplier order is increased.

Execution carries out: prep lists are updated, the cook is notified, the supplier order is placed.

Outcome occurs: Friday service runs smoothly with no stockouts.

Learning captures: the pattern was correct, the decision was effective, the execution was smooth.

Knowledge evolves: the seasonal pattern confidence increases, the prep adjustment rule is reinforced.

**Constitutional Evaluation:**

| Check | Result |
|-------|--------|
| Pipeline order followed | PASS |
| No engine skipped | PASS |
| No forbidden communication | PASS |
| Human sovereignty respected | PASS |
| Human Experience Constitution obeyed | PASS |
| All quality dimensions met | PASS |
| Confidence communicated | PASS |

### 16.2 Bad Pipeline

A broken cognitive pipeline produces unreliable, misaligned cognition.

**Example: Reacting to a slow Monday**

Decision reads raw observation: "Monday revenue is 10% below normal." Without waiting for reasoning, Decision concludes: "There is a problem. Take action."

Planning creates a plan without a clear decision: "Run a promotion to increase traffic." The plan is vague because no clear decision was made.

Execution carries out the vague plan: a discount is offered without considering margins, inventory, or staffing.

The discount creates more traffic but at reduced margins. Kitchen is understaffed. Quality suffers.

Learning observes the outcome but has no clear decision or reasoning to learn from. It records: "Monday discount led to more traffic but lower satisfaction."

Knowledge evolves incorrectly: "Discounts increase traffic" (true) but also "Discounts reduce satisfaction" (misattributed — the satisfaction drop was caused by understaffing, not the discount).

The owner is frustrated because revenue is down, margins are crushed, and the team is exhausted.

**Constitutional Evaluation:**

| Check | Result |
|-------|--------|
| Pipeline order followed | FAIL — Decision skipped Reasoning |
| No forbidden communication | FAIL — Decision read raw observation |
| Human sovereignty respected | FAIL — System acted without owner input |
| All quality dimensions met | FAIL — Accuracy, completeness, traceability all degraded |
| Confidence communicated | FAIL — No confidence assessment |

### 16.3 Healthy Cognition

**Example: Owner asks about staffing**

Observation detects current staffing levels and scheduled shifts.

Pattern recognises that Saturday dinner is typically the busiest shift.

Evidence validates the pattern with historical sales and headcount data.

Memory provides the context that last Saturday was understaffed.

Knowledge integrates: Saturday dinner requires at least 5 front-of-house and 4 back-of-house for optimal service.

Attention allocates: the current schedule shows only 4 front-of-house and 3 back-of-house. This is relevant.

Reasoning: The gap of one person per area is manageable but will create pressure. The most likely impact is slower service and increased stress. Options: ask for volunteers, adjust section sizes, reduce reservations.

### 16.4 Broken Cognition

**Example: Automated inventory ordering**

Observation detects that tomatoes are low.

Pattern recognises: tomatoes are used in multiple dishes.

Memory recalls: last time tomatoes ran out, customers were disappointed.

Decision: "Order more tomatoes." (Decision reads observation and memory directly, bypassing reasoning.)

Planning: "Order 50 pounds of tomatoes." (No reasoning about how much is actually needed.)

Execution: Order is placed.

Outcome: 50 pounds of tomatoes arrive. The restaurant only needed 20 pounds before the next delivery. Half the tomatoes spoil.

## Part 19 — Quality Dimensions (Detailed)

Each quality dimension is defined with its measurement approach, minimum threshold, and degradation response.

### 19.1 Accuracy

**Definition**: The degree to which cognitive outputs correspond to reality.

**Measurement**: Comparison of outputs against verified reference cognition or observed outcomes.

**Minimum Threshold**: 95% correspondence for normal cognition. 99% for safety-critical cognition.

**Degradation Response**: Below threshold outputs are labelled as degraded. Outputs below 80% are not released.

### 19.2 Precision

**Definition**: The specificity and exactness of cognitive outputs.

**Measurement**: Granularity of output — does the output provide specific enough information for its consumers?

**Minimum Threshold**: Output must be precise enough for the most demanding consumer.

**Degradation Response**: Output is labelled with precision limitations. Outputs that mislead through false precision are prohibited.

### 19.3 Completeness

**Definition**: The degree to which cognitive outputs include all relevant information.

**Measurement**: Comparison of output against the full set of relevant cognition available to the engine.

**Minimum Threshold**: All relevant available information is included unless explicitly excluded.

**Degradation Response**: Outputs with missing relevant information are labelled with what is missing and why.

### 19.4 Consistency

**Definition**: The degree to which cognitive outputs are non-contradictory over time.

**Measurement**: Comparison of current outputs against past outputs for similar inputs.

**Minimum Threshold**: No contradictions for identical inputs. Contradictions for similar inputs must be explained.

**Degradation Response**: Inconsistencies are investigated. Inconsistent outputs are labelled until resolution.

### 19.5 Timeliness

**Definition**: The degree to which cognitive outputs are available when needed.

**Measurement**: Time from input receipt to output production compared to consumer requirements.

**Minimum Threshold**: Output must be available before the consumer's deadline.

**Degradation Response**: Late outputs are labelled with delay impact. Repeated lateness triggers capacity review.

### 19.6 Relevance

**Definition**: The degree to which cognitive outputs matter to the consumer.

**Measurement**: Consumer assessment of output usefulness.

**Minimum Threshold**: All outputs must be useful for at least one consumer.

**Degradation Response**: Outputs that consumers consistently find irrelevant are reviewed for purpose alignment.

### 19.7 Clarity

**Definition**: The degree to which cognitive outputs are understandable.

**Measurement**: Consumer assessment of output comprehensibility.

**Minimum Threshold**: Output must be understandable by its intended consumer without additional interpretation.

**Degradation Response**: Unclear outputs are revised. Repeated clarity failures trigger communication policy review.

### 19.8 Traceability

**Definition**: The degree to which cognitive outputs can be traced to their inputs.

**Measurement**: Availability and completeness of input-to-output mapping.

**Minimum Threshold**: Every output must be traceable to its specific inputs.

**Degradation Response**: Untraceable outputs are investigated. Systemic traceability failures trigger audit.

### 19.9 Explainability

**Definition**: The degree to which the cognitive process can be explained.

**Measurement**: Ability to produce an explanation of how an output was derived from its inputs.

**Minimum Threshold**: Every output must have a corresponding explanation.

**Degradation Response**: Unexplainable outputs are withheld. Systemic explainability failures trigger cognitive process review.

### 19.10 Confidence Honesty

**Definition**: The degree to which outputs are accompanied by honest uncertainty assessment.

**Measurement**: Comparison of stated confidence against actual outcome accuracy.

**Minimum Threshold**: Stated confidence must not exceed actual accuracy by more than 5%.

**Degradation Response**: Overconfident outputs trigger confidence assessment review. Systemic overconfidence triggers confidence policy revision.

### 19.11 Stability

**Definition**: The degree to which outputs remain consistent under similar conditions.

**Measurement**: Output variation across multiple identical or similar input sets.

**Minimum Threshold**: Outputs must be identical for identical inputs. Outputs must be similar for similar inputs.

**Degradation Response**: Unstable outputs are investigated for root cause.

### 19.12 Efficiency

**Definition**: The degree to which cognitive processing consumes appropriate resources.

**Measurement**: Cognitive processing resources compared to output value.

**Minimum Threshold**: Processing resources must be proportional to output importance.

**Degradation Response**: Inefficient processing triggers capacity review and evolution planning.

### 19.13 Resilience

**Definition**: The degree to which the engine maintains quality under stress.

**Measurement**: Quality scores at normal vs. stressed capacity levels.

**Minimum Threshold**: Quality must remain above 80% of normal level under 2x normal load.

**Degradation Response**: Resilience failures trigger capacity review and load management policy revision.

### 19.14 Recoverability

**Definition**: The degree to which the engine restores quality after failure.

**Measurement**: Time to recovery and quality level after recovery.

**Minimum Threshold**: Recovery must begin within defined time. Post-recovery quality must meet minimum thresholds.

**Degradation Response**: Recovery failures trigger recovery mechanism review.

### 19.15 Auditability

**Definition**: The degree to which the engine's cognition can be independently verified.

**Measurement**: Completeness of audit trail and ability to verify outputs independently.

**Minimum Threshold**: Audit trail must be complete for all outputs.

**Degradation Response**: Audit failures trigger immediate investigation and output halt until resolution.

## Part 21 — Recovery Mechanisms

### 21.1 Recovery Philosophy

Every engine must have defined recovery mechanisms for each failure mode. Recovery is not optional — it is a contractual obligation.

Recovery must be:

- **Automatic** — Begins without human intervention when possible.
- **Reported** — Consumers are notified of recovery status.
- **Verified** — Recovery is confirmed before normal output resumes.
- **Learned** — Recovery outcomes inform future prevention.

### 21.2 Recovery Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Description | Applies To |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Input Re-processing** | Engine retries processing of input that failed. | All engines — input misinterpretation, transient failures |
| **Input Re-request** | Engine requests input again from upstream. | All engines — missing input, stale input |
| **Alternative Input Path** | Engine requests same cognition from alternative source. | All engines — dependency failure |
| **Confidence Recalibration** | Engine reassesses its confidence methodology. | All engines — confidence miscalibration |
| **Quality Retry** | Engine re-processes to meet quality threshold. | All engines — quality below threshold |
| **Boundary Reassertion** | Engine reaffirms its boundary and rejects out-of-bounds cognition. | All engines — boundary violation |
| **Pipeline Re-alignment** | Engine re-establishes correct pipeline order. | All engines — pipeline violation |
| **State Reset** | Engine resets to a known good internal state. | All engines — overload, starvation, stagnation |
| **Cache Utilization** | Engine uses cached cognition while full cognition is unavailable. | All engines — dependency failure, degradation |
| **Graceful Degradation** | Engine reduces scope but continues at lower quality. | All engines — overload, partial failure |
| **Human Escalation** | Engine requests human intervention for unresolved failures. | All engines — persistent failure, safety-critical |
| **Coordination Request** | Engine requests Coordination Engine assistance. | All engines — cross-engine issues |
| **Load Shedding** | Engine prioritizes critical cognition and defers non-critical. | All engines — overload |
| **Roll-back** | Engine reverts to previous stable version. | All engines — evolution-related failures |
| **Constitutional Review** | Engine requests contract review for systemic issues. | All engines — persistent contract violations |

### 21.3 Recovery Process

1. **Detection** — Failure is identified.
2. **Classification** — Failure severity, scope, duration, and cause are determined.
3. **Notification** — Consumers and dependent engines are informed.
4. **Mechanism Selection** — Appropriate recovery mechanism is chosen.
5. **Execution** — Recovery is performed.
6. **Verification** — Recovery is confirmed against quality thresholds.
7. **Resumption** — Normal output is resumed.
8. **Recording** — Failure and recovery data are recorded for learning.

### 21.4 Recovery Principles

1. **Safety first** — In safety-critical failures, recovery prioritises safety over cognition.
2. **Human in the loop** — Humans are informed of significant failures and recovery steps.
3. **Graceful degradation before complete failure** — Partial cognition is better than no cognition.
4. **No cascading recovery** — Recovery of one engine should not cause failure in another.
5. **Learning from recovery** — Every recovery is an opportunity to prevent future failures.
6. **Time-bounded recovery** — Recovery has deadlines. If recovery cannot complete within the deadline, escalate.
7. **Recovery record** — Every recovery is recorded for audit and learning.
8. **Continuous improvement** — Recovery mechanisms are improved based on recovery outcomes.

Every cognitive engine must declare its contract using the following specification. This specification serves as the template for all current and future engines.

### 24.1 Contract Template

Every engine contract must declare:

1. **Engine Name** — The canonical name of the engine.
2. **Engine Classification** — Perception, Understanding, Storage, Allocation, Processing, Expression, Action, Meta, or Constitutional.
3. **Primary Purpose** — The single cognitive function the engine exists to perform.
4. **Cognitive Domain** — The area of cognition the engine owns.
5. **Responsibilities** — What the engine must do.
6. **Authority** — What the engine may decide without consultation.
7. **Limitations** — What the engine cannot do.
8. **Dependencies** — Which engines provide input.
9. **Consumers** — Which engines receive output.
10. **Outputs** — What the engine produces.
11. **Forbidden Outputs** — What the engine must never produce.
12. **Forbidden Actions** — What the engine must never do.
13. **Assumptions** — What the engine may assume about its inputs and environment.
14. **Pipeline Position** — The engine's position in the canonical cognitive pipeline.
15. **Lifecycle Stages** — The stages the engine transitions through.
16. **Quality Dimensions** — How the engine's cognition is measured.
17. **Failure Modes** — How the engine can fail.
18. **Recovery Mechanisms** — How the engine recovers from failure.
19. **Human Alignment Requirements** — How the engine obeys the Human Experience Constitution.
20. **Evolution Path** — How the engine improves over time.

### 24.2 Observation Engine Contract

**Engine Name:** Observation System
**Classification:** Perception Engine
**Primary Purpose:** To perceive and capture reality as it occurs in and around the restaurant.
**Cognitive Domain:** Raw perception, signal detection, event capture, sensory input.

**Responsibilities:**
- Observe the restaurant environment without interpretation.
- Capture observations with sufficient context for understanding.
- Signal noteworthy events to appropriate engines.
- Maintain observation quality (accuracy, completeness, timeliness).
- Reject fabricated or unverifiable observations.
- Label observations with confidence, timestamp, and source.

**Authority:**
- Determine what constitutes a valid observation.
- Determine observation confidence.
- Determine observation classification.
- Determine observation retention priority.

**Limitations:**
- Cannot interpret what observations mean — that belongs to Pattern and Reasoning.
- Cannot determine which observations are important — that belongs to Attention.
- Cannot change observations after capture.
- Cannot fabricate observations.

**Dependencies:** Reality (the restaurant itself), Execution (confirmation of actions).
**Consumers:** Pattern, Memory, Attention, Business Pulse.
**Pipeline Position:** Stage 1 — First in the cognitive pipeline. All cognition begins here.

**Forbidden Outputs:**
- Interpretations (Opinions labelled as facts).

- Judgments (Value assessments of observed events).

**Forbidden Actions:**
- Observation must not interpret rather than perceive.

**Human Alignment:** Must never observe humans in ways that create surveillance feelings. Must respect privacy boundaries. Must communicate observations in ways that inform without alarming.

## Part 25 — Restaurant Examples (Advanced)

The following advanced examples demonstrate how the Cognitive Engine Contract governs real restaurant scenarios involving multiple engines.

### 25.1 Owner Decision Support

**Scenario:** The owner is considering whether to add a weekend brunch service.

**Engine Participation:**

1. **Observation** detects that nearby restaurants are offering weekend brunch and that customer traffic increases on weekend mornings.

2. **Pattern** recognises a correlation: when nearby restaurants add brunch, overall area restaurant traffic increases. It also recognises that the restaurant currently has zero weekend morning revenue.

3. **Evidence** validates the pattern with three months of observation data. Saturday morning foot traffic in the area has increased 25% since two new brunch spots opened.

4. **Memory** provides context: the restaurant attempted a Sunday brunch two years ago but discontinued it due to low traffic. At that time, area traffic was different.

5. **Knowledge** integrates: the area has changed. Brunch may now be viable. But the previous attempt is relevant context.

6. **Attention** allocates: this is a significant business decision. It should be brought to the owner's attention with full context.

7. **Reasoning** evaluates: brunch would require additional staffing, new menu items, extended hours. Expected revenue increase of 15-20% on weekends. Risks: staffing challenges, kitchen strain, inconsistent demand during off-season.

8. **Decision** (deferred to human): The engine does not make this decision. It presents the analysis to the owner.

## Part 26 — Pipeline Expansion: Branching and Feedback

### 26.1 Pipeline Branching

The canonical pipeline is not strictly linear. It has branches that allow parallel cognition.

**Primary Pipeline:**
Observation → Pattern → Evidence → Memory → Knowledge → Attention → Reasoning → Decision → Planning → Execution → Outcome → Learning → Knowledge Evolution

**Support Branches:**

**Branch A: Direct Signal (Emergency)**
Observation → Attention → Reasoning → Decision → Execution
Used when a safety-critical observation requires immediate action. Pattern, Evidence, Memory, and Knowledge stages are bypassed but the bypass is logged.

**Branch B: Memory Retrieval**
Memory → Reasoning (direct)
When reasoning requires historical context, it may request directly from Memory without going through Attention. The retrieval is logged.

**Branch C: Learning Feedback**
Execution → Learning → Knowledge → Reasoning
Learning feeds back into Knowledge and then into Reasoning, creating a continuous improvement cycle.

**Branch D: Business Pulse Synthesis**
Observation + Knowledge + Reasoning → Business Pulse → Conversation
Business Pulse synthesises cognition from multiple pipeline stages into a coherent business narrative.

**Branch E: Reflection Review**
All engines → Reflection → All engines
Reflection reviews any engine at any time and provides improvement recommendations.

**Branch F: Human Input**
Human → Conversation → Reasoning → Decision
Human input enters through Conversation and feeds into Reasoning and Decision. This is a sovereign override path that does not require upstream validation.

**Branch G: Knowledge Direct to Execution**
Knowledge → Execution
When execution requires established, invariant knowledge (safety procedures, health codes), it may access Knowledge directly. The knowledge confidence must be at maximum for this direct path to be used.

**Branch H: Prediction Feedback**
Prediction → Observation → Pattern
Predictions that are confirmed or contradicted by observations feed back into pattern refinement. This improves future prediction accuracy.

### 26.2 Pipeline Synchronization

When cognition flows through multiple branches simultaneously, synchronization is required:

**Convergent Synchronization:**
When two branches produce cognition that must be combined, synchronization occurs at the convergence point. Both branches must complete before combined cognition proceeds.

**Divergent Synchronization:**
When one engine produces output consumed by multiple downstream engines, the output is made available to all consumers. No synchronization is needed — each consumer processes independently.

**Temporal Synchronization:**
Some cognition requires temporal ordering. Earlier observations must be processed before later observations. Temporal order is preserved within each engine.

**Priority Synchronization:**
Higher-priority cognition is processed before lower-priority cognition. Priority is determined by Attention and communicated to all engines.

### 26.3 Pipeline Feedback Loops

**Learning Loop:**
Outcome → Learning → Knowledge → Reasoning → Decision → Planning → Execution
Learning improves knowledge, which improves future reasoning, decisions, plans, and executions.

**Confidence Loop:**
Execution → Observation → Evidence → Reasoning → Confidence
Execution outcomes feed back through observation and evidence to improve reasoning confidence.

**Correction Loop:**
Observation → Evidence → Reasoning → Decision (revision)
New observations that contradict earlier evidence trigger reasoning revision and potentially decision revision.

**Evolution Loop:**
Reflection → All engines → Evolution
Reflection identifies improvement opportunities that trigger engine evolution.

### 26.3 Pipeline Exception Rules

**Rule 1: Emergency Exception**
When human safety is at risk, any engine may communicate directly with Action engines, bypassing intermediate stages. The bypass must be logged and justified within one cognitive cycle.

**Rule 2: Known Information Exception**
If a pipeline stage's output already exists from prior cognition, it may be reused without re-executing the stage. The reuse must be documented and the timestamp of the original output must be included.

**Rule 3: Human Input Exception**
Human-provided cognition enters the pipeline at the appropriate stage without requiring upstream validation. Humans are sovereign — their cognition does not need system pre-processing.

**Rule 4: Meta-Cognition Exception**
Meta engines (Learning, Reflection, Business Pulse, Coordination) may operate on any pipeline stage without following the pipeline order. Meta-cognition is about cognition, not through cognition.

**Exception Logging:**
Every exception must record:
- Which stages were bypassed.
- Why the bypass was necessary.
- What confidence impact the bypass has on downstream cognition.
- When normal pipeline order will be restored.
- Which engines were affected by the bypass.

- Forbidden actions are not performed.

### 27.3 Human-Verifiable Contract

Every engine's contract must be understandable by a restaurant owner:

- Purpose is stated in plain language.
- Responsibilities are concrete and observable.
- Limitations are honest and clear.
- Quality is measurable by observable outcomes.
- Failure modes are describable in business terms.
- Recovery is understandable without technical knowledge.
- Dependencies are traceable to observable operations.
- Human alignment principles are evident in behavior.

An engine whose contract cannot be understood by its human users has failed its first alignment requirement.

### 27.4 Verification Principles

1. **Verification is continuous** — not periodic, not on request. Every output, every communication, every cognitive act is verified.

2. **Verification is independent** — an engine verifies itself, but cross-engine verification is performed by independent engines (Coordination, Reflection).

3. **Verification is transparent** — verification methods and results are available for audit.

4. **Verification is recursive** — verification itself may be verified. Who verifies the verifier? The Reflection Engine.

5. **Verification is constitutional** — verification is not optional. It is a contractual requirement for every engine.

6. **Verification has teeth** — failed verification stops output. An engine that continues producing output after verification failure is violating the contract.

7. **Verification is humane** — verification identifies issues for improvement, not for blame. The goal is better cognition, not punishment.

## Part 28 — Engine Retirement

### 28.1 Retirement Philosophy

Engines may need to be retired. Retirement is not failure — it is the recognition that an engine's cognitive function is no longer needed or is better served by a different approach.

### 28.2 Retirement Criteria

An engine may be retired when:
- Its cognitive function is no longer needed.
- A replacement engine is ready to assume its function.
- The engine's contract is fundamentally incompatible with system evolution.
- The engine has been consistently failing quality thresholds.
- Constitutional amendment has reallocated its function.

### 28.3 Retirement Process

1. **Retirement Notification** — All dependent engines are notified of the planned retirement.
2. **Transition Period** — A transition period allows dependent engines to adapt.
3. **Function Migration** — The engine's cognitive function is transferred to the replacement or redistributed.
4. **Data Handover** — The engine's knowledge, patterns, and experiences are made available.
5. **Retirement Completion** — The engine ceases operation.
6. **Archival** — The engine's contract, evolution history, and lessons are archived.
7. **System Verification** — The system verifies that all dependencies have been updated.

### 28.4 Retirement Rules

1. An engine may not retire without ensuring its cognitive function is covered.
2. Dependent engines must be prepared before an engine retires.
3. The engine's knowledge and experience must be preserved.
4. Retirement is recorded in project memory.
5. An engine that has been retired may be reactivated if its function is needed again.
6. Retirement is not a judgment of value — it is a recognition of changing needs.
