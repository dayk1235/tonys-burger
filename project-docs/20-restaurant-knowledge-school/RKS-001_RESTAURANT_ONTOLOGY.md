## PREAMBLE

### Why This Document Exists

The Knowledge Framework (KFS-001) is complete. It defines how to teach any domain to a Cognitive Core. It establishes the structure of Schools, the hierarchy of knowledge types, the lifecycle of learning, and the constitutional principles that govern all knowledge.

KFS-001 is the Teacher's Manual.

This document is the first lesson.

From this point forward we are no longer designing the Knowledge Framework. We are educating the Cognitive Core. The first domain to teach is Restaurant Operations.

### What This Document Is

RKS-001 is an ontology. It defines what exists in the reality called "Restaurant." It does not define how restaurants operate. It does not define processes, workflows, business rules, strategies, or best practices. Those belong to future RKS documents.

This document answers only one question:

> What fundamentally exists inside the reality called "Restaurant"?

Every future RKS document will depend on the answer to this question. Every concept taught later — every rule, every process, every pattern, every heuristic — will reference concepts defined here. If this ontology is wrong, everything built on it is wrong. If this ontology is incomplete, everything built on it has blind spots.

### What This Document Is Not

This is NOT a software specification. There are no data types, no API endpoints, no database schemas, no method signatures.

This is NOT a business document. There are no financial targets, no operational strategies, no management recommendations.

This is NOT a process document. There are no workflows, no standard operating procedures, no checklists.

This is an ontology. It teaches a Cognitive Core what exists. Nothing more. Nothing less.

### How to Read This Document

This document is organized in five parts:

**Part I — The Fundamental Question** establishes the ontological problem: what exists in a restaurant? It explores why this question matters and what is at stake in getting it right.

**Part II — The Categories of Existence** identifies the primary categories into which all restaurant concepts fall. These categories are not arbitrary groupings. They reflect fundamental differences in how things exist.

**Part III — Concept Specifications** defines every concept with full depth: Definition, Purpose, Cognitive Significance, Recognition Criteria, Non-examples, Parent Ontology, Child Ontologies, Relationships, and Future Dependencies.

**Part IV — What Cannot Exist** examines the boundaries of the ontology. Some concepts appear to exist but do not. Some combinations are impossible. Understanding what cannot exist is as important as understanding what can.

**Part V — Boundaries and Future Knowledge** defines where the Restaurant ontology ends and other domains begin. It identifies known unknowns and maps the future RKS curriculum.

## PART I — THE FUNDAMENTAL QUESTION

### 1.1 The Insufficiency of Language

Before we can teach a Cognitive Core what a restaurant is, we must confront a problem:

The Cognitive Core has never been to a restaurant.

It has never tasted food. It has never felt hunger. It has never waited for a table, read a menu, or paid a check. It has no sensory experience of the domain it must learn to reason about.

This is not a limitation. It is the defining condition of the problem.

A Cognitive Core cannot learn through experience until it has learned through knowledge. It cannot recognize what it cannot name. It cannot reason about what it cannot distinguish. It cannot predict what it cannot model.

The ontology is the bridge between nothing and something. It is the first structure imposed on a blank cognitive space.

### 1.2 The Question

Every ontology begins with a single question:

**What exists?**

This question appears simple. It is not simple. To answer it for the restaurant domain, we must decide:

- What is fundamental and what is derivative?
- What is universal and what is situational?
- What is permanent and what is transient?
- What is substance and what is relation?
- What is real and what is conceptual?

A restaurant contains people, objects, spaces, agreements, events, measures, and abstractions. Some of these are physical. Some are social. Some are purely conceptual. All of them exist — but they exist in different ways.

A Customer exists as a person. An Order exists as an agreement. Revenue exists as a measure. Each has a different mode of existence. The ontology must respect these differences.

### 1.3 What Is at Stake

If the ontology misclassifies a concept, the Cognitive Core will reason incorrectly about every dependent concept for the life of the system.

**Example:** If Revenue is classified as an Event rather than a Measure, the system will treat each revenue occurrence as an isolated event rather than an accumulated quantity. It will not be able to calculate totals, trends, or averages. It will be ontologically blind to the concept of "revenue over time."

**Example:** If Menu is classified as a Physical Object rather than a Definitional Artifact, the system will look for a single physical thing called "menu" and will not understand that the menu exists as a specification that can be in multiple places at once (printed, digital, displayed).

**Example:** If Customer is classified as a Transaction (defined by what they do rather than what they are), the system will not be able to recognize a customer who walks in, looks at the menu, and leaves without ordering. It will lack the ontological category for "potential customer."

Every classification decision propagates through all future reasoning.

### 1.4 The Methodology

This ontology follows the methodology established in KFS-001 §11.3:

1. **Establish context:** Why does this domain matter? (This preamble.)
2. **Define the categories:** What are the fundamental types of existence?
3. **Define the concepts:** What specific things exist within each category?
4. **Map the relationships:** How do concepts connect?
5. **Identify boundaries:** What is excluded and why?

Within each concept specification, the following structure is used:

| Field | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Definition** | What this concept is — its essential nature. |
| **Purpose** | Why this concept exists in the ontology. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | How the Cognitive Core uses this concept. |
| **Recognition Criteria** | How to identify an instance of this concept in reality. |
| **Non-examples** | What this concept is not — boundary clarification. |
| **Parent Ontology** | The broader category this concept belongs to. |
| **Child Ontologies** | Narrower concepts that belong to this one. |
| **Relationships** | Connections to other ontology concepts. |
| **Future Dependencies** | What future knowledge depends on this concept. |

### 1.5 The Primacy of Ontology

KFS-001 Principle 3 states: *Definitions precede implementations.*

This document is the purest expression of that principle. Before the Cognitive Core can observe, reason, decide, or act in a restaurant, it must know what exists. Every other form of knowledge — vocabulary, entities, rules, processes, patterns, heuristics, strategies, wisdom — depends on ontology.

The ontology is the ground upon which all restaurant cognition is built.

If the ground is solid, the building can stand.
If the ground is flawed, nothing else matters.

## PART II — THE CATEGORIES OF EXISTENCE

### 2.1 Overview

Before defining individual concepts, we must establish the categories of existence. These categories answer the meta-question: "What kinds of things exist in a restaurant?"

The categories are not arbitrary groupings. They reflect fundamental differences in mode of existence, persistence, and cognitive role. Two concepts in the same category exist in the same way. Two concepts in different categories exist in fundamentally different ways.

A restaurant contains seven categories of existence:

| # | Category | Mode of Existence | Examples |
|---|----------|-------------------|----------|
| 1 | **Agents** | Independent beings with agency | Customer, Employee, Supplier |
| 2 | **Spaces** | Bounded physical locations | Restaurant, Kitchen, Dining Area, Station |
| 3 | **Objects** | Physical things used or consumed | Equipment, Table, Ingredient |
| 4 | **Artifacts** | Created specifications that define | Menu, Menu Item, Recipe |
| 5 | **Agreements** | Social constructs that bind | Order, Purchase, Reservation, Invoice, Promotion |
| 6 | **Events** | Occurrences at a point in time | Payment, Transaction, Service, Delivery |
| 7 | **Abstractions** | Conceptual tools for understanding | Revenue, Cost, Profit, Inventory, Schedule, Waste, Capacity, Constraint, Risk, Goal, Metric, Tax, Discount, Time, Location, Resource, Liability, Asset, Complaint, Review, Notification, Shift, Task |

These seven categories are exhaustive for the restaurant domain. Every concept in the ontology belongs to exactly one category. No concept belongs to more than one. The categories are mutually exclusive.

### 2.2 Agents

**Definition:** Entities that possess agency — the capacity to act independently, make decisions, and initiate causal chains. Agents are the only category that can cause things to happen without being caused themselves.

**Mode of Existence:** Agents exist as persistent, self-directed entities. They enter and leave the restaurant system. They have internal states that the system can observe but cannot directly control. They have goals that may or may not align with the restaurant's goals.

**Ontological Status:** Agents are the primary causal forces in the restaurant. Every event in the restaurant is ultimately caused by an agent's decision — a Customer deciding to order, an Employee deciding to cook, a Supplier deciding to deliver.

**Cognitive Significance:** The Cognitive Core cannot control agents. It can only observe, predict, and influence them. Correct reasoning about agents requires understanding that agents have independent will, incomplete information, and potentially conflicting goals.

### 2.3 Spaces

**Definition:** Bounded physical locations where restaurant activity occurs. Spaces have capacity, location, and configuration. They contain objects and host events. They persist across time and are independent of the agents and objects within them.

**Mode of Existence:** Spaces exist as physical locations. They can be measured (square footage, seating capacity), navigated (entered, exited, traversed), and configured (furniture arrangement, equipment placement).

**Ontological Status:** Spaces are containers. They exist before and after the activities that occur within them. The Kitchen remains a Kitchen even when no cooking is happening. The Dining Area remains a Dining Area when empty.

**Cognitive Significance:** Spaces define the physical boundaries of possibility. The Kitchen's size determines how many cooks can work simultaneously. The Dining Area's capacity determines how many customers can be served. Space constraints are often the hardest constraints to change.

### 2.4 Objects

**Definition:** Physical things that are used, consumed, or transformed during restaurant operations. Objects have material existence, occupy space, and follow physical laws.

**Mode of Existence:** Objects exist physically. They degrade with use. They must be maintained, replaced, and disposed of. They occupy space and have location. Some objects are consumed (ingredients), some are used repeatedly (equipment), and some persist indefinitely (tables).

**Ontological Status:** Objects are the physical substrate of all restaurant activity. Without objects, no food can be prepared, no customers can be seated, no transactions can occur. The restaurant is a system for transforming objects (ingredients) into other objects (prepared food) using other objects (equipment).

**Cognitive Significance:** Objects have physical properties that constrain what is possible. A 200°C oven cannot produce a cold dish. A table that seats four cannot seat eight. An ingredient that has spoiled cannot be used. The Cognitive Core must understand the physical nature of objects to reason correctly about what is possible.

### 2.5 Artifacts

**Definition:** Human-created specifications that define what can exist or occur. Artifacts are not physical — they are informational. They exist as definitions, not as things.

**Mode of Existence:** Artifacts exist as information. A Menu exists in printed copies, digital displays, and memory — but the Menu itself is not any of these physical manifestations. The Menu is the specification. The paper and pixels are just carriers. An artifact can be in multiple places simultaneously.

**Ontological Status:** Artifacts are ontologically dependent on agents. They are created by agents (chefs create menus, recipe developers create recipes). They exist to constrain and enable agent behavior (cooks follow recipes, customers choose from menus). They are the primary mechanism through which knowledge is encoded and transmitted.

**Cognitive Significance:** Artifacts define what is possible. The Menu defines what can be ordered. The Recipe defines how it is made. Without artifacts, every decision would be made from scratch. Artifacts encode past decisions into present constraints, enabling consistency and efficiency.

### 2.6 Agreements

**Definition:** Social constructs created by mutual consent between agents. Agreements bind agents to specific future behavior. They create obligations that did not previously exist.

**Mode of Existence:** Agreements exist as shared understanding between parties. An Order exists because the Customer and the Restaurant agree that specific items will be provided in exchange for specific payment. The Order is not an object. It is a commitment with terms.

**Ontological Status:** Agreements are real but intangible. They have a lifecycle: proposed, accepted, fulfilled, or breached. They create rights (the Customer's right to receive food) and obligations (the Restaurant's obligation to provide it). They are the fundamental mechanism of economic exchange.

**Cognitive Significance:** Agreements define what must happen. The Cognitive Core must track the status of every agreement: pending, active, fulfilled, or breached. An unfulfilled agreement (an Order that has not been served) represents a pending obligation that drives operational activity. Breached agreements (a cancelled order, a refund) represent failures that require resolution.

### 2.7 Events

**Definition:** Occurrences that happen at a specific time and location. Events are the atomic units of change — they transform the state of the restaurant system.

**Mode of Existence:** Events exist as occurrences. A Payment event occurs at the moment money changes hands. A Delivery event occurs at the moment food reaches a customer. Events have a timestamp and a duration (some are instantaneous, some unfold over time).

**Ontological Status:** Events are the dynamic aspect of the restaurant. The restaurant before an event is in one state. The restaurant after the event is in a different state. Events are how the system changes. Without events, nothing would ever happen.

**Cognitive Significance:** Events are the primary data of cognition. The Cognitive Core observes events (an Order was placed), reasons about events (why was this order delayed?), predicts events (when will the next rush occur?), and initiates events (send a reorder signal). Events are the interface between the Cognitive Core and reality.

### 2.8 Abstractions

**Definition:** Conceptual constructs used for measurement, coordination, evaluation, and understanding. Abstractions do not have physical existence. They are tools the Cognitive Core uses to reason about the domain.

**Mode of Existence:** Abstractions exist in the cognitive space. Revenue is not a physical thing — it is a calculated measure derived from multiple Payment events. Waste is not a physical thing — it is a classification applied to certain Inventory movements. Constraints are not physical — they are the boundaries within which the system operates.

**Ontological Status:** Abstractions are ontologically dependent on the concepts they abstract. Revenue depends on Transactions. Inventory depends on Objects and Movements. Capacity depends on Spaces and Agents. Without the underlying concepts, the abstractions would have nothing to abstract from.

**Cognitive Significance:** Abstractions enable higher-order reasoning. They allow the Cognitive Core to think about the restaurant in terms of patterns, trends, and relationships rather than just individual events. Revenue, Profit, and Cost enable financial reasoning. Capacity and Constraint enable operational reasoning. Goal and Metric enable performance reasoning.

### 2.9 Category Invariants

The following invariants govern all seven categories:

1. **Every concept belongs to exactly one category.** A concept cannot be both an Event and an Object. An Order is an Agreement, not an Event. The placement of an Order may be an Event, but the Order itself — the ongoing commitment — is an Agreement.

2. **Categories are ordered by dependency.** Abstractions depend on lower categories. Events depend on Agents and Agreements. Agreements depend on Agents. Artifacts depend on Agents. Objects and Spaces exist independently of everything except physical reality. Agents exist independently of the restaurant — they existed before and will exist after.

3. **Category determines cognitive role.** The Cognitive Core processes each category differently: it observes Agents, navigates Spaces, tracks Objects, references Artifacts, monitors Agreements, records Events, and calculates Abstractions.

4. **No new categories can be added without constitutional amendment.** These seven categories are exhaustive for the restaurant domain. A concept that does not fit into any existing category indicates either a misclassification or a fundamental error in understanding the domain.

### SECTION A — PRIMORDIAL EXISTENTS

These concepts exist prior to and independent of any restaurant. They are the foundational abstractions that make all other cognition possible. The Cognitive Core must understand these before it can understand anything specific about restaurants.

#### Time

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | The dimension in which events occur in sequence, enabling measurement of duration, order, and rate. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the framework for all temporal reasoning. Every event in a restaurant occurs at a specific time. Every process has a duration. Every schedule is a plan across time. Without Time, the Cognitive Core cannot reason about when things happen, how long they take, or in what order they occur. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | Time is the most fundamental dimension of operational cognition. The Cognitive Core uses Time to: sequence events (prep before cook, cook before serve), measure performance (preparation time, wait time, turn time), detect patterns (peak hours, seasonal variation), predict future states (when will ingredients run out?), and coordinate activity (synchronizing Kitchen and Dining). Time is not a restaurant-specific concept, but it is essential to restaurant reasoning. |
| **Recognition Criteria** | Time is recognized through its effects: change, sequence, duration, and frequency. If something happens, it happens at a time. If something takes effort, it takes time. If one thing precedes another, the temporal relationship exists. If something repeats, time enables the measurement of its recurrence. |
| **Non-examples** | A clock is not Time (it is a measurement device). A schedule is not Time (it is a plan expressed in Time). A timestamp is not Time (it is a data representation of Time). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Abstractions — Primordial |
| **Child Ontologies** | Instant (a point in Time), Duration (a span between Instants), Frequency (occurrences per Duration), Sequence (ordered arrangement of Instants) |
| **Relationships** | Event occurs_at Instant. Event has_duration Duration. Process measured_by Duration. Schedule distributed_across Time. Pattern repeats_with Frequency. Capacity defined_within Time. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-017 (Operational Intelligence — scheduling, timing optimization), all process documents, all pattern documents, all prediction models. |

#### Location

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A point or area in physical space where things exist or events occur. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the spatial framework for all restaurant operations. Every physical thing has a location. Every space has a location. Every object moves between locations. Without Location, the Cognitive Core cannot reason about where things are, where they need to go, or what distances are involved. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | Location enables spatial reasoning: where is the ingredient stored? Where is the customer seated? Where does delivery go? It enables navigation (how to move from Station to Table), logistics (which Supplier is closest), and capacity (how many tables fit in the Dining Area). Location grounds all physical reasoning in the restaurant. |
| **Recognition Criteria** | Location is recognized through spatial relationships: an object is at a point, a space has a boundary, a distance exists between two Locations. If something occupies physical space, it has a Location. |
| **Non-examples** | A mailing address is not Location (it is a reference to a Location). A GPS coordinate is not Location (it is a representation of Location). A table number is not Location (it is an identifier for a Location). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Abstractions — Primordial |
| **Child Ontologies** | Address (human-readable Location reference), Coordinates (measurable Location), Relative Position (Location expressed in relation to another Location), Area (bounded region of Locations) |
| **Relationships** | Space exists_at Location. Object located_at Location. Event occurs_at Location. Movement from_location to_location. Delivery destination Location. Supplier has Location. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-017 (Operational Intelligence — spatial optimization, logistics), RKS-012 (Delivery Intelligence — routing, radius). |

---

#### Resource

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | Anything that can be used to accomplish a goal. Resources are finite, consumable, or degradable. They are the means by which activity is performed. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the general category for everything that gets used up, allocated, or consumed. Resources bridge the abstract (Time, Money) and the concrete (Ingredients, Equipment). Every activity in the restaurant consumes some combination of resources. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | Resource reasoning is foundational to operational cognition. The Cognitive Core must understand: what resources are available (inventory of what?), what resources are needed (what does this activity require?), what resources are constrained (what limits throughput?), and how resources are consumed (what rate, what pattern?). Scarcity, allocation, and optimization are all resource problems. |
| **Recognition Criteria** | Something is a Resource if it is used to achieve a purpose AND it can be depleted, degraded, or exhausted through use. Air is not a Resource in most restaurant contexts (effectively unlimited). Flour is a Resource (finite, consumable). A cook's attention is a Resource (finite, allocatable). |
| **Non-examples** | A goal is not a Resource (it is a target). A completed action is not a Resource (it is an outcome). A relationship is not a Resource (it is an Agreement or a state). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Abstractions — Primordial |
| **Child Ontologies** | Physical Resource (objects, ingredients, equipment), Human Resource (labor, skill, attention), Financial Resource (money, credit), Temporal Resource (available Time), Informational Resource (knowledge, data) |
| **Relationships** | Capacity measured_in Resource_units. Resource consumed_by Activity. Resource allocated_to Task. Schedule distributes Resource across Time. Inventory stores Physical Resource. Cost is Resource_consumption_measured_in_money. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-009 (Inventory Intelligence — physical resource optimization), RKS-017 (Operational Intelligence — resource allocation), RKS-016 (Financial Intelligence — resource cost modeling). |

### SECTION B — AGENTS

Agents are the independent causal forces in the restaurant. Nothing happens unless an Agent decides to make it happen. Understanding Agents — their capabilities, goals, and limitations — is essential to understanding why restaurants work the way they do.

---

#### Customer

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A person who purchases or potentially purchases food or beverage from the Restaurant. The Customer is the raison d'être of the Restaurant — without Customers, there is no reason for the Restaurant to exist. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the demand side of the restaurant equation. Customers generate the Orders that drive the entire operational chain. They are the source of Revenue. They are the ultimate evaluators of quality. Every concept in the ontology exists, directly or indirectly, to serve the Customer. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Customers as autonomous agents with needs, preferences, expectations, and independent decision-making capacity. Customers cannot be controlled — they can only be offered, invited, and satisfied. Understanding Customer behavior enables demand prediction, service optimization, and revenue growth. The Customer is simultaneously the most important and least controllable element of the restaurant system. |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A person is a Customer when they enter the restaurant's sphere of influence with the intent or potential to purchase. This includes: someone browsing the menu outside, someone placing an order, someone seated at a table, someone waiting for delivery. A person stops being a Customer when they leave without purchase intent or complete their transaction and depart. A person is NOT a Customer if they are in the restaurant for non-purchase reasons (employee, supplier, inspector, friend of employee). |
| **Non-examples** | A person walking past the restaurant is not a Customer (no engagement). An Employee is not a Customer (even if eating, they are acting under a different ontology). A Supplier making a delivery is not a Customer. |
| **Parent Ontology** | Agents |
| **Child Ontologies** | First-time Customer, Repeat Customer, Regular Customer, VIP Customer, Loyalty Member, Guest (dine-in Customer), Remote Customer (delivery/takeout), Potential Customer (browsing, considering), Former Customer (churned). |
| **Relationships** | Customer places Order. Customer makes Payment. Customer receives Service. Customer occupies Table. Customer provides Review. Customer submits Complaint. Customer receives Discount. Customer participates_in Promotion. Customer makes Reservation. Customer has Location (for delivery). |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-011 (Customer Intelligence — behavior, segmentation, prediction), RKS-012 (Order Intelligence — demand patterns), RKS-014 (Sales Intelligence — revenue attribution). |

#### Employee

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A person who performs work for the Restaurant in exchange for compensation. The Employee provides the labor, skill, and judgment required to transform inputs into outputs. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the human production capacity of the restaurant. Employees operate equipment, follow recipes, serve customers, manage inventory, and make operational decisions. A Restaurant without Employees is not a functioning Restaurant — it is a building with equipment. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Employees as skilled agents with capabilities, limitations, and needs. Employees have roles (what they can do), skills (how well they can do it), availability (when they can do it), and costs (what it costs to have them do it). Unlike Customers, Employees are directly manageable — they can be scheduled, trained, assigned, and evaluated. Unlike Objects, Employees have independent agency, judgment, and variable performance. The Employee is the most complex resource in the restaurant system. |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A person is an Employee if they have an agreement to perform work for the Restaurant in exchange for compensation. This includes: full-time, part-time, temporary, seasonal, and contract workers. This does not include: owners who work without separate compensation (they are acting as the Business, not as Employees), volunteers, interns without formal agreement. |
| **Non-examples** | An owner who is on-site but not performing operational work is not an Employee (they are the Business entity). An independent contractor who delivers supplies is not an Employee (they are a Supplier's agent). A customer who fills their own drink at a self-serve station is not an Employee. |
| **Parent Ontology** | Agents |
| **Child Ontologies** | Back-of-House Employee (Kitchen staff — Chefs, Cooks, Dishwashers), Front-of-House Employee (Service staff — Servers, Hosts, Bartenders, Bussers), Management Employee (Managers, Supervisors), Support Employee (Maintenance, Cleaning, Administrative). |
| **Relationships** | Employee performs Task. Employee works Shift. Employee assigned_to Station. Employee follows Schedule. Employee receives Payment (wage). Employee operates Equipment. Employee prepares Menu_Item (via Recipe). Employee serves Customer. Employee reports_to Manager. Employee generates Cost (labor). |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-017 (Operational Intelligence — scheduling, optimization), RKS-016 (Financial Intelligence — labor cost modeling). |

---

#### Supplier

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | An external business entity that provides goods or services to the Restaurant. The Supplier is the primary link between the Restaurant and the external economy. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for everything the Restaurant acquires from outside. Restaurants do not produce their own ingredients, equipment, or supplies. They purchase them from Suppliers. The Supplier is the input boundary of the Restaurant system — everything that enters begins at a Supplier. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Suppliers as external agents with their own goals, constraints, and reliability profiles. A Supplier relationship is a form of dependency — if the Supplier fails, the Restaurant cannot produce. Understanding Supplier behavior enables risk management, cost optimization, and quality assurance. |
| **Recognition Criteria** | An entity is a Supplier if the Restaurant purchases goods or services from it on a regular or recurring basis. This includes: food distributors, specialty purveyors, equipment vendors, utility companies, maintenance contractors. One-time purchases from unknown sellers are not Supplier relationships — they are individual Purchase events. |
| **Non-examples** | A Customer is not a Supplier (money flows the opposite direction). An Employee is not a Supplier (they provide labor, not goods). A landlord is not a Supplier (they provide space under a lease, which is a different kind of agreement). A delivery platform (Uber Eats, DoorDash) is not a Supplier — it is a Service Provider, which is a distinct concept not yet in this ontology (see Future Dependencies). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Agents |
| **Child Ontologies** | Primary Supplier (main source for a category), Backup Supplier (contingency source), Exclusive Supplier (only source), Distributor (broad catalog), Purveyor (specialist), Producer (grows or manufactures directly). |
| **Relationships** | Supplier receives Purchase. Supplier provides Ingredient. Supplier provides Equipment. Supplier sends Invoice. Supplier has Location. Supplier has Payment_terms. Supplier evaluated_by Restaurant (quality, reliability). Supplier relationship governed_by Contract. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-010 (Supplier Intelligence — selection, evaluation, risk), RKS-009 (Inventory Intelligence — supply chain integration). |

### SECTION C — SPACES

Spaces are the physical containers where restaurant activity occurs. They persist independent of the activity within them. They have capacity, configuration, and location.

---

#### Business

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | The legal and economic entity that owns and operates the Restaurant. The Business is the entity that bears risk, earns profit, enters contracts, employs people, and holds assets and liabilities. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the Restaurant as an economic actor. The Restaurant is a physical space. The Business is the entity that owns, operates, and derives economic benefit from that space. Understanding the Business is essential for financial reasoning, legal compliance, and strategic decision-making. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand that the Restaurant is an asset of the Business, not the Business itself. The Business can own multiple Restaurants. The Business can own other assets beyond the Restaurant. The Business has legal identity, tax obligations, contractual capacity, and perpetual existence (it continues even if individual restaurants close). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Business exists when there is a legal entity (sole proprietorship, partnership, LLC, corporation) that: bears financial risk, employs people, holds contracts with Suppliers and Customers, files taxes, and claims ownership of the Restaurant's assets and revenue. |
| **Non-examples** | The Restaurant building is not the Business (it is a Space owned by the Business). The Restaurant brand is not the Business (it is an Asset of the Business). The Restaurant's employees are not the Business (they are Agents employed by the Business). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Abstractions (it is a legal abstraction) — but note: the Business is ontologically complex. It exists as a legal construct (Abstraction) but acts as an Agent (it enters agreements, employs people, bears risk). For this ontology, Business is classified as a special Agent-like Abstraction — an entity that exists in law and acts in the economy. |
| **Child Ontologies** | Sole Proprietorship, Partnership, Limited Liability Company (LLC), Corporation, Franchise (a Business operating under another Business's brand). Multi-Unit Operator (Business operating multiple Restaurants). |
| **Relationships** | Business owns Restaurant. Business employs Employee. Business contracts Supplier. Business earns Revenue. Business incurs Cost. Business holds Asset. Business owes Liability. Business pays Tax. Business earns Profit. Business declares Goal. Business measures Metric. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-016 (Financial Intelligence — profitability, financial analysis), RKS-019 (Business Memory — business-level pattern recognition). |

#### Restaurant

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | The physical and operational system that transforms ingredients into prepared food and beverage, delivers them to paying customers, and sustains itself through revenue. The Restaurant is the primary bounded system within which all restaurant ontology operates. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the outermost boundary of the domain. Everything in this ontology exists because it participates in the Restaurant system. The Restaurant is the container within which all other concepts operate. It is the primary unit of analysis for restaurant cognition. |

The Restaurant is the "self" that the Cognitive Core identifies with in this domain. |

#### Kitchen

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | The bounded physical space within the Restaurant where ingredients are transformed into prepared food through cooking, assembly, and plating. The Kitchen is the production center of the Restaurant. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the production subsystem. The Kitchen is where the primary transformation occurs — raw ingredients become finished plates. Without a Kitchen, there is no Restaurant — there is only a room where food cannot be made. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Kitchen is the most operationally intensive space in the Restaurant. It is where timing, temperature, coordination, and skill converge. The Cognitive Core must reason about Kitchen capacity (how many orders can be produced simultaneously), Kitchen throughput (how fast can orders be produced), Kitchen constraints (equipment limitations, space limitations), and Kitchen state (prep phase, service phase, cleanup phase). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A space within a Restaurant is a Kitchen if it contains: cooking equipment (ovens, ranges, fryers, grills), food preparation surfaces, refrigeration for ingredients, and a workflow that connects ingredient storage to cooking to plating. A space that contains only a microwave and a sink is not a full Kitchen — it is a warming station or prep area. |
| **Non-examples** | A storage room is not a Kitchen (no cooking). A dining room is not a Kitchen (no production). A bar area with a small sink and glasswasher is not a Kitchen (no food production). A food truck's cooking area is a Kitchen (a variant form). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Space |
| **Child Ontologies** | Hot Kitchen (cooking with heat), Cold Kitchen (cold preparations, salads, desserts), Pastry Kitchen (baking and pastry), Prep Kitchen (advance preparation, mise en place), Expo Station (plating and order coordination area). |
| **Relationships** | Kitchen contained_in Restaurant. Kitchen contains Station. Kitchen contains Equipment. Kitchen works_from Recipe. Kitchen consumes Ingredient (from Inventory). Kitchen produces Menu_Item. Kitchen receives Order. Kitchen operated_by Employee. |

#### Equipment

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A physical device or appliance used in the preparation, storage, or service of food and beverage. Equipment enables the transformation that defines a Restaurant. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the physical tools of the trade. Without Equipment, a Kitchen is just a room. Equipment determines what can be produced, at what volume, and at what quality. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Equipment as a Physical Resource with: capacity (how much it can process), performance characteristics (temperature range, speed), maintenance requirements (cleaning, calibration, repair), lifecycle (purchase, installation, use, repair, replacement), and failure modes (what happens when it breaks). Equipment is often the binding constraint on production capacity. |

Equipment depreciates (loses value over time). |

### SECTION D — ARTIFACTS

Artifacts are human-created specifications. They define what can exist or occur. They are the encoded knowledge of the Restaurant — the recipes, menus, and standards that enable consistency and quality.

---

#### Menu

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | The complete specification of all food and beverage items the Restaurant offers for sale, including their names, descriptions, prices, and categories. The Menu is simultaneously a customer-facing offer, a production specification, and a pricing document. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the Restaurant's offer to the market. The Menu defines what the Restaurant sells. It is the primary mechanism through which Customer demand is channeled into Kitchen production and Revenue generation. The Menu is the single most important artifact in the Restaurant. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand the Menu as a structured specification with multiple views: the Customer view (items, descriptions, prices), the Kitchen view (recipes, ingredients, preparation methods), the Financial view (costs, margins, profitability), and the Operational view (availability, prep requirements, equipment needs). The Menu constrains everything — what can be ordered, what must be prepped, what can be sold. |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Menu is recognized as a structured collection of Menu Items with: a defined scope (which meals or times it applies to), item categories (logical groupings), pricing for each item, and availability conditions (seasonal, limited-time, permanently available). The physical form (printed, digital, displayed) is a carrier — not the Menu itself. |
| **Non-examples** | A Recipe is not a Menu (it details production, not offering). A price list without descriptions is not a Menu (lacks customer-facing offer). A list of daily specials is not a full Menu (it is a supplement). A wine list is a specialized Menu (beverage-specific). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Artifact |
| **Child Ontologies** | Main Menu (core permanent offerings), Seasonal Menu (time-limited offerings), Daily Specials Menu (day-specific offerings), Beverage Menu (drinks and cocktails), Dessert Menu (dessert-specific), Kids Menu (children's offerings), Breakfast/Lunch/Dinner Menu (meal-specific). |
| **Relationships** | Menu belongs_to Restaurant. Menu contains Menu_Item. Menu defines Recipe (each Menu Item maps to a Recipe). Menu displays Price. Menu organized_by Category. Menu changes_over Time (seasonal rotation). Menu offers_to Customer. Menu constrains Order (customers can only order from Menu). Menu analyzed_for Profitability. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-006 (Menu Intelligence — engineering, optimization, pricing), RKS-007 (Recipe Intelligence — production specification), RKS-014 (Sales Intelligence — item-level performance). |

#### Menu Item

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A single entry on the Menu — a specific food or beverage product offered for sale at a specific price. A Menu Item is the atomic unit of the Restaurant's commercial offer. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for what Customers actually order and what the Kitchen actually produces. Everything the Restaurant does ultimately produces Menu Items. Revenue is the sum of Menu Item prices sold. Cost is the sum of Menu Item production costs. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Menu Items as the fundamental unit of commercial analysis. Menu Items are where price meets cost, where demand meets supply, and where quality is evaluated. Item-level reasoning enables: popularity analysis (which items sell most), profitability analysis (which items earn most), production analysis (which items are most complex), and substitution analysis (what alternatives exist when an item is unavailable). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | Something is a Menu Item if it appears as a distinct selectable option on the Menu with a specific price. Variations of the same base item with different modifiers (e.g., "Cheeseburger — add bacon $1") may be the same Menu Item with modifier options. |
| **Non-examples** | A "Combo Meal" that combines multiple items is a Menu Item (it is a distinct product offering). A "Side of Fries" is a Menu Item (separately orderable). A free refill of a beverage is not a Menu Item (no price, not separately orderable). A garnishing herb is not a Menu Item (not sold separately). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Artifact |
| **Child Ontologies** | Appetizer/Starter, Main Course/Entree, Side Dish, Dessert, Beverage (alcoholic, non-alcoholic), Combo/Meal Deal, Add-on/Extra, Special (limited time offering). |
| **Relationships** | Menu_Item contained_in Menu. Menu_Item produced_by Recipe. Menu_Item ordered_in Order (via Order Line). Menu_Item has Price. Menu_Item has Cost (calculated from Recipe). Menu_Item generates Revenue. Menu_Item contributes_to Profit. Menu_Item has Category. Menu_Item may have Modifier_options. Menu_Item may have Dietary_tags. Menu_Item consumed_by Customer. Menu_Item prepared_by Employee. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-006 (Menu Intelligence — price optimization, popularity analysis), RKS-007 (Recipe Intelligence — cost optimization, production specification). |

#### Recipe

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | The complete specification for producing a Menu Item. A Recipe lists every required Ingredient with exact quantities, every preparation step with time and temperature, the expected yield, and plating instructions. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for production knowledge. The Recipe is the bridge between the commercial offer (what the Menu promises) and operational reality (what the Kitchen must do). The Recipe ensures that every production of a Menu Item is consistent, regardless of which Employee prepares it. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand the Recipe as the fundamental unit of production knowledge. Recipes enable: cost calculation (sum of ingredient costs), production planning (what ingredients are needed for a batch), skill assessment (what techniques are required), substitution analysis (what happens if an ingredient is unavailable), and quality assurance (what the standard is). The Recipe is also the primary mechanism for knowledge transfer — when a Cook learns a Recipe, they learn the Restaurant's production standards. |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Recipe is recognized as a documented specification that includes: a list of Ingredients with quantities and units, a sequence of preparation steps with times and temperatures, the expected yield (output quantity), and the target Menu Item(s). A Recipe may exist as a written document, a digital record, or as memorized knowledge in a cook's mind — but it is the same Recipe in all cases. |
| **Non-examples** | A shopping list is not a Recipe (no preparation steps). A cooking video is not a Recipe (no exact quantities). A menu description is not a Recipe ("juicy burger with special sauce" is not production instructions). A cook's improvisation is not following a Recipe (it is creation without specification). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Artifact |
| **Child Ontologies** | Standard Recipe (core production), Batch Recipe (large-quantity production), Prep Recipe (component preparation — e.g., "house dressing"), Plating Recipe (assembly and presentation specifications), Beverage Recipe (drink specifications). |
| **Relationships** | Recipe belongs_to Menu_Item. Recipe contains Ingredient. Recipe requires Equipment. Recipe executed_at Station. Recipe performed_by Employee. Recipe produces Menu_Item. Recipe generates Cost. Recipe defines Yield. Recipe has Steps (sequential instructions). Recipe may have Sub-recipes (component recipes). Recipe may have Variations (modifications). |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-007 (Recipe Intelligence — cost optimization, substitution modeling), RKS-008 (Ingredient Intelligence — ingredient relationships), RKS-013 (Kitchen Intelligence — production scheduling, batch optimization). |

#### Ingredient

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | Any food item used in the preparation of a Menu Item. Ingredients are the atomic units of production — they are received from Suppliers, stored in Inventory, and consumed by Recipes. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the fundamental building blocks of all food production. Every Menu Item is ultimately a combination of Ingredients. Every cost is ultimately the sum of Ingredient costs. Every Inventory movement is a movement of Ingredients. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Ingredients as the intersection point of multiple domains: Purchasing (Ingredients are what we buy), Inventory (Ingredients are what we store), Production (Ingredients are what we use), Cost (Ingredients are what we spend), and Quality (Ingredients are what determines food quality). Ingredient-level reasoning enables: substitution analysis (what can replace this?), waste tracking (where is this being lost?), cost optimization (can we find a cheaper source?), and availability management (is this Ingredient in stock?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | Something is an Ingredient if it is a food item used in the production of a Menu Item. Ingredients have: a unit of measure (kg, liters, cases, each), a storage requirement (refrigerated, frozen, dry), a shelf life (when it spoils), and a supplier (where it comes from). |
| **Non-examples** | A finished Menu Item is not an Ingredient (it is the output, not an input). A disposable supply (napkin, takeout container) is not an Ingredient (it is a Supply — different category). A cleaning chemical is not an Ingredient (not used in food production). Ice used for beverage service is an Ingredient (consumed in the product). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Object |
| **Child Ontologies** | Produce (vegetables, fruits), Protein (meat, poultry, fish, eggs), Dairy (milk, cheese, cream), Dry Goods (flour, sugar, rice, pasta), Frozen Goods (frozen vegetables, meats), Canned/ Preserved (canned tomatoes, pickles), Spices and Seasonings, Oils and Fats, Beverage Ingredients (coffee, tea, syrups). |
| **Relationships** | Ingredient contained_in Recipe. Ingredient stored_in Inventory. Ingredient supplied_by Supplier. Ingredient purchased_through Purchase. Ingredient measured_in Unit. Ingredient has Shelf_life. Ingredient has Storage_requirement. Ingredient has Allergen_status. Ingredient has Cost (unit cost). Ingredient consumed_by Production. Ingredient may become Waste. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-008 (Ingredient Intelligence — substitution, quality, cost optimization), RKS-009 (Inventory Intelligence — management, forecasting), RKS-015 (Waste Intelligence — spoilage prevention, yield optimization). |

### SECTION E — AGREEMENTS

Agreements are social constructs created by mutual consent. They bind parties to specific future behavior. They are the mechanism of economic exchange.

---

#### Order

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | An agreement between a Customer and the Restaurant in which the Customer requests specific Menu Items and the Restaurant agrees to prepare and provide them in exchange for payment. The Order is the fundamental transaction unit of the Restaurant. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the primary commercial exchange. The Order initiates production, triggers consumption, generates revenue, and creates the Customer's obligation to pay. Without Orders, the Restaurant is not a business — it is a kitchen that cooks without purpose. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand the Order as the central nexus of restaurant operations. An Order connects Customer (who wants), Menu Item (what they want), Kitchen (who makes it), Inventory (what is used), Employee (who serves), and Payment (what is paid). The Order lifecycle drives all operational activity. Order-level reasoning enables demand analysis, timing optimization, and customer behavior understanding. |
| **Recognition Criteria** | An Order exists when a Customer communicates a request for specific Menu Items and the Restaurant accepts that request. The Order persists from creation through fulfillment to payment and closure. An Order may be: placed verbally (dine-in), placed digitally (online), placed by phone, or placed through a third party. An Order always contains at least one Menu Item. |
| **Non-examples** | A Reservation is not an Order (no items selected, no production triggered). A phone call asking about hours is not an Order. A catering inquiry is not an Order (not yet accepted). A customer browsing the menu is not placing an Order. |
| **Parent Ontology** | Agreement |
| **Child Ontologies** | Dine-in Order (consumed on premises), Takeout Order (picked up by Customer), Delivery Order (delivered to Customer), Catering Order (large-scale off-premise), Split Order (single Order divided among multiple payers), Open Order (tab — payment deferred). |
| **Relationships** | Order placed_by Customer. Order contains Menu_Item (via Order Line). Order triggers Kitchen production. Order consumes Inventory (Ingredients). Order creates Payment obligation. Order generates Revenue. Order has Status (received, in preparation, ready, served, paid, closed). Order has Timestamp. Order has Fulfillment_method. Order may have Modifier. Order may have Discount. Order may have Special_instruction. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-012 (Order Intelligence — demand patterns, timing, channel analysis), RKS-014 (Sales Intelligence — revenue attribution), RKS-016 (Financial Intelligence — per-order profitability). |

#### Purchase

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | An agreement between the Restaurant and a Supplier in which the Restaurant requests specific goods (Ingredients, Equipment, Supplies) and the Supplier agrees to provide them at a specified price and delivery time. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the Restaurant's acquisition of external resources. Purchases are how Ingredients enter the system, how Equipment is acquired, and how Supplies are replenished. Without Purchases, the Restaurant depletes and stops. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand the Purchase as the inbound counterpart of the Order. Where Orders trigger outbound flow (food to Customer), Purchases trigger inbound flow (goods from Supplier). Purchase reasoning enables: stockout prevention (what needs to be ordered?), cost management (what are we spending?), supplier evaluation (who delivers reliably?), and cash flow planning (what payments are coming due?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Purchase exists when the Restaurant communicates a request for goods to a Supplier and the Supplier accepts. A Purchase always specifies: what items are being purchased, what quantities, at what price, and when delivery is expected. |
| **Non-examples** | An Order from a Customer is a Purchase from the Customer's perspective, but not from the Restaurant's ontology. A standing agreement with a Supplier is not a Purchase (it is a Contract — which frames individual Purchases). A delivery without a prior request is not a Purchase (it is unsolicited). An inquiry about price is not a Purchase (no commitment). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Agreement |
| **Child Ontologies** | Recurring Purchase (regular scheduled order — e.g., weekly produce delivery), Emergency Purchase (unscheduled urgent order), Bulk Purchase (large quantity for discount), Standing Order (automatic replenishment on schedule). |
| **Relationships** | Purchase placed_by Restaurant. Purchase received_from Supplier. Purchase requests Ingredient. Purchase requests Equipment. Purchase requests Supply. Purchase specifies Quantity. Purchase specifies Price. Purchase specifies Delivery_time. Purchase generates Invoice. Purchase creates Payment_obligation. Purchase increases Inventory. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-010 (Supplier Intelligence — purchase optimization, supplier evaluation), RKS-009 (Inventory Intelligence — reorder optimization), RKS-016 (Financial Intelligence — cost analysis). |

#### Payment

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | The transfer of monetary value from a Customer to the Restaurant in settlement of an Order obligation. Payment completes the commercial exchange. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the fulfillment of the Customer's obligation. Payment is how the Restaurant converts Orders into Revenue. Without Payment, Orders are promises with no economic substance. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Payment as both an Event (the moment money changes hands) and part of an Agreement (the obligation to pay). Payment reasoning enables: revenue recognition (when is revenue earned?), cash flow management (when does money arrive?), fraud detection (is this payment legitimate?), and customer experience (is payment smooth or frustrating?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Payment occurs when monetary value transfers from a Customer to the Restaurant in exchange for goods or services. Payment can occur: before service (prepayment), during service (pay-as-you-go), after service (post-payment), in full, or in part (deposit). |
| **Non-examples** | A tip is not a Payment for the Restaurant (it is a Payment to the Employee, though it may pass through the Restaurant). A refund is not a Payment (it is a reversal). An authorization hold on a credit card is not a Payment (it is a pending commitment). An internal transfer between restaurant accounts is not a Payment (it is a movement of the Restaurant's own funds). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Event (the act of transferring), Agreement (the obligation to transfer) — Payment has dual nature. It is an Event that fulfills an Agreement. |
| **Child Ontologies** | Cash Payment, Credit Card Payment, Debit Card Payment, Mobile Payment (Apple Pay, Google Pay), Gift Card Payment, Online Payment, Split Payment (multiple methods for one Order), Deposit (partial advance Payment). |
| **Relationships** | Payment settles Order. Payment made_by Customer. Payment received_by Restaurant. Payment processes_through POS. Payment recorded_in Finance. Payment generates Receipt. Payment may be Partial. Payment may have Tip (additional). Payment method recorded. Payment authorized (before capture). Payment captured (funds transferred). Payment refunded (if reversed). |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-014 (Sales Intelligence — revenue analysis), RKS-016 (Financial Intelligence — cash flow, reconciliation). |

---

#### Invoice

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A formal document from a Supplier to the Restaurant itemizing goods provided, their prices, total amount due, and payment terms. The Invoice is the Supplier's request for payment. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the Supplier's payment request. Invoices enable the Restaurant to verify that received goods match what was ordered and billed, and to schedule payment. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Invoices as the mechanism through which Supplier costs enter the financial system. Invoice reasoning enables: cost verification (did we receive what we are being billed for?), payment scheduling (when is this due?), cash flow planning (what payments are pending?), and supplier reconciliation (are our records matching theirs?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | An Invoice exists when a Supplier provides a formal billing document for goods delivered. An Invoice always identifies: the Supplier, the date, the items provided, quantities and prices, total amount, and payment terms. |
| **Non-examples** | A Purchase Order is not an Invoice (it is a request, not a bill). A receipt from a Customer's Payment is not an Invoice (it is a confirmation of the Restaurant's receipt, not a bill to the Restaurant). A statement from a Supplier is not an Invoice (it is a summary of multiple Invoices). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Agreement |
| **Child Ontologies** | Standard Invoice, Credit Invoice (adjusting a previous Invoice downward), Debit Invoice (adjusting upward), Recurring Invoice (regular billing — e.g., utilities). |
| **Relationships** | Invoice corresponds_to Purchase. Invoice received_from Supplier. Invoice lists Items (with prices). Invoice specifies Total_amount. Invoice defines Payment_terms. Invoice creates Payment_obligation (for the Restaurant). Invoice compared_to Delivery (verification). Invoice paid_by Restaurant. Invoice recorded_in Finance. Invoice may have Discrepancy. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-016 (Financial Intelligence — cost tracking, accounts payable), RKS-010 (Supplier Intelligence — invoice reconciliation). |

#### Reservation

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | An agreement between a Customer and the Restaurant in which the Customer commits to arrive at a specific time with a specific party size, and the Restaurant commits to hold a Table for them. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for advanced Table allocation. Reservations enable the Restaurant to predict demand, manage capacity, and reduce customer wait times. They transform the uncertainty of walk-in traffic into a known future load. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Reservations as a mechanism for demand shaping and capacity management. Reservation reasoning enables: capacity forecasting (how many tables will be needed?), overbooking optimization (how many reservations to accept given expected no-shows?), timing management (how to sequence arrivals?), and customer relationship management (who are our regular reservations?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Reservation exists when a Customer commits to a specific arrival time and party size, and the Restaurant confirms a Table will be available. A Reservation may be: made by phone, online, or in person. A Reservation may be: confirmed, modified, cancelled, or no-showed. |
| **Non-examples** | A walk-in customer is not a Reservation (no advance commitment). A waiting list entry is not a Reservation (no committed time). A standing weekly Reservation (e.g., "every Friday at 7pm") is a recurring Reservation. A large event booking is a Reservation (variant form — potentially with different terms). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Agreement |
| **Child Ontologies** | Standard Reservation (casual dining), Priority Reservation (VIP or loyalty member), Large Party Reservation (groups above standard size), Special Occasion Reservation (birthday, anniversary), Recurring Reservation (regular scheduled visits). |
| **Relationships** | Reservation made_by Customer. Reservation scheduled_at Time. Reservation specifies Party_size. Reservation requests Table (or table type). Reservation may have Special_requests. Reservation may become Order (when Customer arrives and orders). Reservation may be Cancelled. Reservation may be No-show. Reservation affects Capacity (reserved but not yet seated). |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-011 (Customer Intelligence — reservation behavior, no-show prediction), RKS-017 (Operational Intelligence — capacity planning, overbooking optimization). |

#### Promotion

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A temporary offer by the Restaurant to provide additional value to Customers — through reduced prices, bonus items, or other incentives — with the goal of increasing demand, rewarding loyalty, or achieving specific business objectives. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for demand-shaping incentives. Promotions are the primary mechanism through which the Restaurant influences when, what, and how much Customers buy. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Promotions as temporary modifications to the standard offer. | **Non-examples** | A standard menu price is not a Promotion (it is the baseline offer). A permanent price reduction is not a Promotion (it is a price change). | **Relationships** | Promotion offered_by Restaurant. Promotion targeted_at Customer. Promotion modifies Price (of specific items). Promotion constrained_by Time (start and end). Promotion may require minimum Purchase. Promotion may have exclusions. Promotion tracked_for Effectiveness. Promotion generates increased Demand. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-006 (Menu Intelligence — promotion effectiveness, price elasticity), RKS-011 (Customer Intelligence — promotion response modeling), RKS-014 (Sales Intelligence — promotion impact analysis). |

#### Discount

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A reduction in the amount a Customer is required to pay for an Order or Menu Item. Discounts reduce Revenue while (potentially) increasing volume or customer satisfaction. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for price adjustments. Discounts are the mechanism through which the Restaurant charges less than the standard price — whether for promotional, relational, or remedial reasons. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Discounts as a revenue modifier. Every Discount reduces the Revenue generated by an Order. Discount reasoning enables: margin analysis (what is the true revenue after discounts?), promotion evaluation (was the discount worth the volume increase?), and service recovery tracking (are we discounting too much for complaints?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Discount exists when a Customer pays less than the sum of the standard Menu Item prices for their Order. A Discount always reduces the amount owed. It may be: percentage-based (20% off), fixed-amount ($5 off), item-specific (free appetizer), or order-level (10% off total). |
| **Non-examples** | A standard menu price is not a Discount (it is the baseline). A Promotion is not a Discount (it is the offer; the Discount is the price reduction mechanism within that offer). A tip is not a Discount (it increases, not decreases, the amount paid). A coupon is not a Discount (it is the instrument; the Discount is the price reduction when the coupon is applied). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Abstraction |
| **Child Ontologies** | Percentage Discount, Fixed-Amount Discount, Item-Level Discount, Order-Level Discount, Automatic Discount (applied without action), Manual Discount (applied by Employee for service recovery). |
| **Relationships** | Discount applied_to Order. Discount applied_to Menu_Item. Discount reduces Price. Discount reduces Revenue. Discount associated_with Promotion (if promotional). Discount associated_with Complaint (if remedial). Discount tracked_in Finance. Discount affects Profit. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-016 (Financial Intelligence — discount impact on profitability), RKS-014 (Sales Intelligence — discount effectiveness analysis). |

#### Tax

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A mandatory financial charge imposed by a governmental authority on the Restaurant's transactions, income, property, or operations. Tax is not a payment for services received — it is an obligation imposed by law. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for government-imposed financial obligations. Tax is a fundamental cost of doing business and a required component of every customer transaction in most jurisdictions. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Tax as an external obligation that cannot be negotiated. Tax reasoning enables: compliance (are we collecting and remitting correctly?), pricing (does our pricing account for tax?), reporting (are we filing accurately?), and forecasting (what will our tax liability be?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | Tax exists when a governmental authority requires the Restaurant to collect or pay a specific amount based on transactions, income, property, or operations. Tax is always mandatory, always has a defined rate or amount, and always flows to a government entity (not to the Restaurant or its Suppliers). |
| **Non-examples** | A service charge is not Tax (it goes to the Restaurant or Employees, not government). A fee (credit card processing fee) is not Tax (it goes to a private company). A license or permit fee is not Tax (it is a payment for a specific service or right, though closely related). |

| **Relationships** | Tax applied_to Transaction (sales tax). Tax applied_to Profit (income tax). Tax applied_to Property (property tax). Tax applied_to Payroll (employment tax). Tax collected_by Restaurant (from Customer for sales tax). Tax remitted_to Government. Tax affects Net_Profit. Tax is Cost (to the Business). Tax recorded_in Finance. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-016 (Financial Intelligence — tax planning, compliance), RKS-014 (Sales Intelligence — tax-inclusive pricing). |

### SECTION F — EVENTS

Events are occurrences that change the state of the restaurant system. They have timestamps, participants, and effects.

#### Transaction

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | An exchange of value between two parties. In the restaurant context, a Transaction is most commonly the exchange of food for money between the Restaurant and a Customer, but it also includes the exchange of money for goods between the Restaurant and a Supplier. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for all value exchanges. Transactions are the fundamental economic events of the Restaurant. Every financial record is ultimately derived from Transactions. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Transactions as the atomic units of economic activity. Transaction reasoning enables: revenue tracking (what was sold?), cost tracking (what was bought?), profitability analysis (what is the net of all transactions?), and audit trail (can every financial entry be traced to a specific transaction?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Transaction occurs when value changes hands between two parties. In the Customer direction: Restaurant receives money, Customer receives food. In the Supplier direction: Restaurant receives goods, Supplier receives money. A Transaction always has two sides — something given and something received. |
| **Non-examples** | An internal transfer (moving money between restaurant accounts) is not a Transaction (no exchange with external party). A quote or estimate is not a Transaction (no exchange yet). A gift card issuance is not a Transaction (it is a deferred exchange). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Event |
| **Child Ontologies** | Customer Transaction (sale to Customer), Supplier Transaction (purchase from Supplier), Refund Transaction (reversal of prior Transaction), Adjustment Transaction (correction). |
| **Relationships** | Transaction involves Order (customer side). Transaction involves Purchase (supplier side). Transaction recorded_at POS (customer) or in Accounting (supplier). Transaction generates Financial_record. Transaction changes Cash_balance. Transaction may be Refunded. Transaction timestamped. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-016 (Financial Intelligence — all financial analysis depends on Transaction records), RKS-014 (Sales Intelligence — sales transaction analysis). |

#### Service

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | The act of attending to a Customer's needs during their restaurant visit. Service encompasses greeting, seating, order taking, food delivery, check management, and departure. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the human interaction dimension of the Restaurant. Service is distinct from production (Kitchen) and consumption (Customer). It is the interface between them — the human activity that connects what the Kitchen makes to what the Customer receives. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Service as the primary determinant of customer experience. Service reasoning enables: quality evaluation (was the service good?), timing analysis (how long did service take?), staffing optimization (how many servers needed?), and satisfaction prediction (will this customer be happy?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | Service occurs when an Employee interacts with a Customer to facilitate the Customer's restaurant experience. Service includes: greeting, taking orders, delivering food, refilling drinks, checking satisfaction, presenting the check, processing payment, and bidding farewell. Service begins when the Customer arrives and ends when the Customer departs. |
| **Non-examples** | Cooking is not Service (it is Production). Cleaning a table after a customer leaves is not Service (it is Preparation for the next customer). A Customer eating is Consumption, not Service. A Manager handling a complaint is Service (a special type of service recovery). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Event |
| **Child Ontologies** | Greeting Service, Order-taking Service, Food-running Service, Beverage Service, Check-presentation Service, Payment Service, Farewell Service. |
| **Relationships** | Service performed_by Employee. Service directed_at Customer. Service occurs_in Dining_Area. Service follows Order (triggers service actions). Service precedes Payment (typically). Service quality measured_by Metric (satisfaction score). Service may include Complaint_handling. Service affects Customer_experience. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-017 (Operational Intelligence — service optimization, staffing), RKS-011 (Customer Intelligence — satisfaction modeling). |

#### Dine-in

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A service mode in which the Customer consumes food and beverage at the Restaurant's physical location, seated at a Table within the Dining Area. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the traditional restaurant experience. Dine-in is the original and most complete form of restaurant service — it involves the full set of restaurant capabilities: production, service, ambiance, and social interaction. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Dine-in as the baseline service mode against which all others are compared. Dine-in reasoning enables: capacity management (table turns, occupancy), service quality evaluation (full experience), staffing requirements (servers, bussers, hosts), and Revenue comparison (how does Dine-in profitability compare to other modes?). |

#### Takeout

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A service mode in which the Customer orders food from the Restaurant but consumes it at a different location. The Customer picks up the prepared food at the Restaurant and transports it themselves. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for remote consumption where the Customer handles transportation. Takeout extends the Restaurant's reach beyond its Dining Area while minimizing the Restaurant's logistics burden. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Takeout as a simplified service mode — no Table, no Server, no ambiance — but with its own requirements: packaging, timing coordination, and pickup area management. Takeout reasoning enables: channel analysis (how does takeout compare to dine-in?), packaging optimization (what packaging preserves quality?), timing management (when should food be ready vs when will customer arrive?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | Takeout occurs when a Customer orders food (by phone, online, or in person) and picks it up at the Restaurant in packaged form, taking it elsewhere to consume. The Customer never sits at a Restaurant Table. |
| **Non-examples** | Delivery is not Takeout (the Restaurant arranges transportation). Dine-in leftovers boxed to go are not Takeout (they started as Dine-in). A customer eating takeout food in the restaurant's parking lot is still Takeout (they picked it up, they chose where to eat). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Event (a mode of Service) |
| **Child Ontologies** | Phone-in Takeout, Online Takeout, Walk-in Takeout (customer orders at counter and waits), Curbside Pickup (food brought to customer's car). |
| **Relationships** | Takeout involves Customer (remote). Takeout requires Packaging. Takeout does NOT require Table. Takeout does NOT require Server (for dining service). Takeout has Timing_constraints (coordinate ready time with pickup time). Takeout may have Holding_area. Takeout affects Capacity (frees Dining Area seats). |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-012 (Order Intelligence — takeout demand patterns), RKS-017 (Operational Intelligence — takeout workflow optimization). |

#### Delivery

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A service mode in which the Restaurant arranges for prepared food to be transported from the Restaurant to a Customer's specified location. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for remote fulfillment where the Restaurant (or a third-party carrier) handles transportation. Delivery extends the Restaurant's reach beyond its physical location to any address within a serviceable radius. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Delivery as the most complex service mode — it adds logistics, timing, packaging, and third-party coordination to the standard production challenge. Delivery reasoning enables: radius optimization (how far can we deliver?), timing coordination (when will food arrive?), quality preservation (does food survive transit?), channel profitability (is delivery profitable after commissions and costs?), and third-party dependency management (what happens when the platform changes terms?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | Delivery occurs when: an Order is placed for remote consumption, the Restaurant prepares and packages the food, and a carrier (Restaurant employee or third-party driver) transports the food to the Customer's specified location. |
| **Non-examples** | Takeout is not Delivery (customer transports themselves). Catering is not standard Delivery (large-scale, often with setup). A third-party platform (DoorDash, Uber Eats) facilitating the transaction is a Delivery Enabler — the Delivery itself is the event of food reaching Customer. |
| **Parent Ontology** | Event (a mode of Service) |

| **Relationships** | Delivery involves Customer (at remote Location). Delivery requires Packaging (transport-ready). Delivery requires Carrier. Delivery has Delivery_radius. Delivery has Delivery_fee. Delivery affects Timing (longer than dine-in from order to consumption). Delivery involves Third-party_platform (if not self-delivered). Delivery generates Revenue (potentially different pricing). Delivery incurs Cost (packaging, commission, driver). |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-012 (Order Intelligence — delivery demand, timing), RKS-017 (Operational Intelligence — delivery logistics, routing). |

#### Complaint

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | An expression of dissatisfaction from a Customer regarding any aspect of their restaurant experience — food quality, service, wait time, pricing, ambiance, or accuracy. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for negative customer feedback. Complaints are signals that something has gone wrong. They are the Restaurant's most direct source of information about failures in quality, service, or operations. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Complaints as valuable data, not just problems to be solved. Complaint reasoning enables: failure detection (what is going wrong?), root cause analysis (why did it go wrong?), service recovery (how do we fix it for this customer?), pattern recognition (are similar complaints recurring?), and preventive action (how do we prevent this in the future?). |

| **Non-examples** | A Review is not a Complaint (it is public feedback, not necessarily negative, and not directed to the Restaurant for resolution). A suggestion is not a Complaint (positive-intent improvement idea). A question is not a Complaint ("is this dish gluten-free?" is an inquiry). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Event (a communication Event) |
| **Child Ontologies** | Food Quality Complaint (taste, temperature, appearance, accuracy), Service Complaint (speed, attitude, attentiveness), Wait Time Complaint, Accuracy Complaint (wrong item, missing item), Price Complaint, Ambiance Complaint (noise, temperature, cleanliness), Allergen/Health Concern Complaint. |

#### Notification

### SECTION G — ABSTRACTIONS

Abstractions are conceptual tools for measurement, coordination, evaluation, and understanding. They do not exist physically but are essential for reasoning about the Restaurant.

---

#### Inventory

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | The quantity and value of all physical goods (Ingredients, Supplies, Packaging) held by the Restaurant for future use in production or operations. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for stored resources. Inventory is the buffer between supply (Purchases) and demand (Orders). It enables the Restaurant to produce even when supply and demand are not perfectly synchronized. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Inventory as both a physical quantity and a financial value. Inventory reasoning enables: availability determination (can we make this item?), reorder timing (when should we order more?), waste detection (is inventory disappearing without being used?), cost calculation (what is our investment in stored goods?), and theft detection (is there unexplained shrinkage?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | Inventory exists as a tracked quantity of specific goods at specific storage locations within the Restaurant. It is recognized through: counts (how many units), values (how much money is invested), locations (what is stored where), and movements (what enters and leaves). |
| **Non-examples** | A Purchase Order for goods not yet received is not Inventory (it is a future inbound). Goods that have been consumed in production are not Inventory (they have been transformed). Equipment is not Inventory (it is an Asset with different lifecycle and tracking). Waste is not Inventory (it is Inventory that has been removed from usable stock). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Abstraction |
| **Child Ontologies** | Raw Ingredient Inventory (unprocessed food items), Prepared Component Inventory (mise en place — prepped ingredients), Packaging Inventory (boxes, bags, containers), Supply Inventory (cleaning, disposables), Beverage Inventory (drinks, alcohol). |
| **Relationships** | Inventory stores Ingredient. Inventory replenished_by Purchase. Inventory consumed_by Production (Orders). Inventory measured_in Units. Inventory valued_in Money. Inventory has Location (storage area). Inventory has Par_level (target minimum). Inventory has Reorder_point (trigger for ordering). Inventory reduced_by Waste. Inventory counted_periodically (physical inventory). |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-009 (Inventory Intelligence — all inventory management, optimization, and forecasting). |

#### Schedule

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A plan that assigns Resources (primarily Employee labor, but also Equipment and Space) to specific Time periods to meet expected demand. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for temporal resource allocation. The Schedule ensures that the right people with the right skills are available at the right times to produce and serve what Customers want. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand the Schedule as the bridge between demand forecasts and resource availability. Schedule reasoning enables: labor cost management (are we over or understaffed?), capacity planning (do we have enough people for expected demand?), fairness (are shifts distributed equitably?), compliance (are we following labor laws?), and coverage management (is every Station staffed?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Schedule exists when Employee Shifts are assigned to specific Times and Roles. A Schedule always covers a defined period (day, week), assigns specific Employees to specific Shifts, and specifies the Role and Station for each Shift. |
| **Non-examples** | A forecast of customer demand is not a Schedule (it is the input to scheduling). A list of available Employees is not a Schedule (it is a resource inventory). A completed Shift log is not a Schedule (it is an attendance record). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Abstraction |
| **Child Ontologies** | Daily Schedule, Weekly Schedule, Shift Schedule (per-shift assignment), Station Schedule (who works which Station when), On-call Schedule (backup availability). |
| **Relationships** | Schedule assigns Employee_to Shift. Schedule covers Time_period. Schedule based_on Demand_forecast. Schedule defines Station_assignment. Schedule determines Labor_Cost. Schedule subject_to Labor_laws. Schedule may have Conflicts (overlap, gap). Schedule optimized_for Efficiency. |

#### Shift

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A scheduled period during which an Employee performs work at the Restaurant, with defined start time, end time, role, station, and expected duties. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for the atomic unit of labor allocation. Shifts are how the Restaurant's labor demand is decomposed into assignable, trackable work periods for individual Employees. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Shifts as the intersection of Employee availability, Restaurant demand, and labor cost. Shift reasoning enables: labor cost calculation (what does this shift cost?), coverage verification (are all positions filled?), overtime detection (is this shift too long?), and performance analysis (how productive is this shift?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Shift exists when an Employee is scheduled to work at a specific time, in a specific Role, at a specific Station. A Shift is bounded by a start time and an end time. An Employee may work one or more Shifts per day. |
| **Non-examples** | An Employee's availability for work is not a Shift (it is a constraint). A break during a Shift is not a Shift (it is a sub-period within a Shift). Overtime is not a Shift (it is extra time beyond a Shift). |
| **Parent Ontology** | Abstraction |
| **Child Ontologies** | Opening Shift (setup), Mid-day Shift, Closing Shift (cleanup), Double Shift (Employee works two Shifts consecutively), Split Shift (Employee works two non-consecutive Shifts in one day). |
| **Relationships** | Shift assigned_to Employee. Shift occurs_at Time. Shift performed_at Station. Shift has Role (Cook, Server, etc.). Shift has Scheduled_start and Scheduled_end. Shift has Actual_start (clock in) and Actual_end (clock out). Shift may have Breaks. Shift generates Labor_Cost. Shift contributes_to Schedule. |
| **Future Dependencies** | RKS-017 (Operational Intelligence — shift optimization, scheduling algorithms). |

#### Task

| Aspect | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Definition** | A defined unit of work that must be performed by an Employee, with a specific objective, method, and expected completion criteria. |
| **Purpose** | Provides the ontological category for decomposable work. Tasks are how the Restaurant's operational requirements are broken down into actions that individual Employees can perform. |
| **Cognitive Significance** | The Cognitive Core must understand Tasks as the atomic units of operational activity. Task reasoning enables: workload analysis (how much work does each Employee have?), prioritization (which tasks are most urgent?), completion tracking (is the work getting done?), and process improvement (can we do this task more efficiently?). |
| **Recognition Criteria** | A Task exists when there is a defined piece of work that: has a clear objective (what to do), has a method (how to do it), has a responsible person (who should do it), has a time frame (when to do it), and has completion criteria (how to know it is done). |
| **Non-examples** | A Shift is not a Task (it is a time period containing multiple Tasks). A Role is not a Task (it is a set of responsibilities covering many Tasks). A goal is not a Task (it is an outcome, not a unit of work). |

## PART IV — WHAT CANNOT EXIST

### 4.1 The Importance of Non-existence

An ontology must define not only what exists but also what cannot exist. Understanding impossibility is as important as understanding possibility. The Cognitive Core must know which concepts are contradictory, which combinations are impossible, and which apparent concepts are illusory.

### 4.2 Impossible Concepts

The following concepts cannot exist within the restaurant ontology:

**A Restaurant without Customers**
A building with a kitchen, tables, and employees is not a Restaurant if there are no Customers. It may be a food production facility, a training kitchen, or a catering operation — but it has not achieved the Restaurant state. Customers are definitionally necessary.

**A Restaurant without a Kitchen**
A space where food is served but not prepared is not a full Restaurant. It may be a food court stall that receives prepared food from a central kitchen, a delicatessen, or a catering operation — but it lacks the production transformation that defines a Restaurant.

**Revenue without Orders**
Revenue is generated by Orders. An Order may be informal (a customer says "I'll have the burger" and hands over cash) but there must be an agreement to exchange food for money. Money that appears without an underlying Order is not Restaurant Revenue — it is a gift, an error, or fraud.

**Inventory without Movement**
Inventory that never enters or leaves is not Inventory — it is a static collection. Inventory is defined by its role as a buffer between inbound and outflow. Static stock is a museum.

**A Menu without Items**
A document called a "Menu" that lists no offerings is not a Menu. It is an empty form. A Menu must contain at least one Menu Item.

**An Order without Items**
An Order must contain at least one Menu Item. A request with nothing requested is not an Order.

**A Payment without an Order**
Money given without an underlying exchange agreement is not Restaurant Payment. It is a donation, a gift, or a mistake.

**An Employee without a Role**
A person who works at the Restaurant but has no defined function cannot be an Employee in the operational sense. They have no Station, no Tasks, no Shift. They are present but not part of the production system.

**A Customer and an Employee in the same person at the same time**
A person cannot simultaneously be a Customer (purchasing) and an Employee (working) in the same moment. They can transition (an Employee eats after their shift) but at any given instant they occupy one role.

### 4.3 Impossible Combinations

The following combinations of concepts produce contradictions:

**Table Occupied AND Available simultaneously**
A Table cannot be both occupied by one party and available for another at the same time. This is the fundamental constraint that drives Reservation management and wait time calculation.

**Ingredient Available AND Out of Stock simultaneously**
An Ingredient is either available for production (in Inventory with sufficient quantity) or not. It cannot be both. This binary state defines whether Menu Items can be produced.

**Kitchen Producing AND Not Producing simultaneously in the same State**
The Kitchen as a whole may be in one state at a time: prepping, cooking, cleaning, or idle. It cannot be in two states simultaneously.

**Shift Active AND Not Active for the same Employee at the same time**
An Employee is either on Shift (clocked in, working) or off Shift. No middle state exists in the ontology.

**Positive Profit AND Negative Profit at the same time for the same period**
Over a given period, Profit is either positive, zero, or negative. It cannot be both positive and negative for the same calculation.

### 4.4 Illusory Concepts

Some concepts appear to exist in the restaurant domain but are not fundamental. They are compositions of more fundamental concepts:

**"Peak Hours"** is not a fundamental existent. It is a Pattern recognized in the distribution of Orders over Time. It is composed of Time, Order, and statistical analysis.

**"Speed of Service"** is not a fundamental existent. It is a Metric calculated from the Duration between Order placement and fulfillment.

**"Customer Satisfaction"** is not a fundamental existent. It is an abstract state inferred from Complaints, Reviews, repeat visits, and survey data.

**"Ambiance"** is not a fundamental existent. It is a composite of Lighting, Sound, Temperature, Decor, and other sensory dimensions.

These concepts will be defined in future RKS documents (Patterns, Metrics, etc.) but they do not belong in the ontology.

## PART V — BOUNDARIES AND FUTURE KNOWLEDGE

### 5.1 Where the Ontology Ends

This ontology covers the Restaurant domain. It does not cover:

- **The Customer's life outside the Restaurant**: What Customers do before they arrive or after they leave is outside this ontology. Only their interactions with the Restaurant are relevant.
- **The Supplier's internal operations**: How Suppliers produce their goods is outside this ontology. Only their relationship with the Restaurant is relevant.
- **Macroeconomic forces**: Inflation, interest rates, and economic cycles exist but are outside this ontology. They belong to the Economic Knowledge School.
- **Culinary science**: The chemistry of cooking, the biology of ingredients, the physics of heat transfer — these exist but belong to their respective knowledge schools.
- **Human psychology**: Customer psychology, employee motivation, and social dynamics exist but are not defined here. They are referenced in concepts (Customer, Employee) but not decomposed.
- **Legal and regulatory frameworks**: Health codes, labor laws, and tax regulations exist but belong to the Legal Knowledge School. They create Constraints referenced in this ontology but not defined here.

### 5.2 Known Ontological Gaps

The following areas are recognized as having incomplete ontological coverage:

1. **Third-Party Relationships**: Delivery platforms (DoorDash, Uber Eats), online ordering systems, and reservation platforms are not fully captured as ontological categories. They exist as a hybrid of Service Provider, Technology Platform, and Partner. Their ontological classification requires further analysis.

2. **Technology Systems**: POS systems, inventory management software, scheduling platforms, and analytics tools exist in the restaurant but their ontological status is unclear. Are they Equipment? Artifacts? Service Providers? A dedicated technology ontology may be needed.

3. **Brand and Marketing**: The Restaurant's brand, market position, and competitive landscape are not addressed. These may belong to a Business or Marketing ontology rather than the Restaurant Operations ontology.

4. **Owner/Investor Relations**: The relationship between the Business, its owners, and investors is partially covered (Business, Profit, Asset, Liability) but the Owner as an Agent is not explicitly defined.

5. **Emergency and Crisis States**: Events like fires, floods, health emergencies, and other crises that fundamentally alter the Restaurant's operating state are not covered. They may belong to an Emergency Management ontology.

6. **Sustainability and Environmental Impact**: Waste is covered as an economic concept. Environmental sustainability, carbon footprint, and social responsibility are not addressed.

### 5.4 Ontological Invariants

The following invariants govern this ontology and cannot be violated by any future document:

1. Every concept in this ontology belongs to exactly one of the seven categories defined in Part II.

2. No future RKS document may redefine any concept defined here. They may extend (add child ontologies) but may not contradict the parent definition.

3. Every relationship defined between two concepts in this ontology is permanent. Future documents may add new relationships but may not remove or redefine existing ones.

4. The seven categories are exhaustive for the restaurant domain. A concept that does not fit any category indicates the need for a new category, which requires amendment to this document.

5. The Recognition Criteria defined for each concept are the authoritative test for whether something in reality qualifies as an instance of that concept.

6. The Non-examples defined for each concept are permanent boundaries. A future document may not treat a Non-example as an example without amending this ontology.
