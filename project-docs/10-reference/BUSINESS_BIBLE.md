## 1.2 Founding Story

Marco Bellini cooked for 14 years before opening his own restaurant. He started as a dishwasher at a red-sauce joint in Boston at age 16. By 22 he was a line cook. By 28 he was a sous chef at a respected Italian restaurant in Portland's Pearl District. At 32 he was ready.

He spent three years saving, planning, and looking for the right space. He found it on Alberta Street in 2021 — a former bakery with high ceilings, exposed brick, and a wood-fired oven already installed. The neighborhood was evolving: more families, more foot traffic, more disposable income. Marco believed Portland wanted Italian food that was honest — not precious, not Americanized, just well-executed regional Italian cooking.

He opened in January 2022 with $180,000 in personal savings, a $250,000 SBA loan, and $70,000 from friends and family. He had 28 seats, a four-person kitchen, and no idea if anyone would come.

They came.

## 1.5 Concept

Modern Italian trattoria. The menu changes seasonally (four menus per year: Winter, Spring, Summer, Fall). Core dishes remain year-round with ingredient substitutions. The kitchen emphasizes handmade pasta (three varieties made in-house daily), wood-fired proteins (600-degree oven, 90-second pizza, 6-minute fish), and vegetable-forward contorni (side dishes that often outshine the mains).

The dining room is energetic but not loud. Service is knowledgeable but not formal. Wine is approachable — 75% Italian, all under $120 on the list, 12 by the glass.

Via Trattoria is not fine dining. It is generous dining.

## 1.6 Neighborhood

Alberta Street runs through the heart of Northeast Portland. Historically a working-class corridor, Alberta has gentrified significantly since 2015. The street is known for its independent retail, street art, monthly art walks, and restaurant density.

**Neighborhood demographics:**
- Median household income: $78,000 (Portland average: $71,000)
- Median age: 34
- Household composition: 42% families, 35% singles, 23% roommates
- Walk Score: 92

**Foot traffic patterns:**
- Weekday foot traffic peaks 11:30 AM–1:30 PM (lunch) and 5:30–8:00 PM (dinner)
- Weekend foot traffic extends 10:00 AM–10:00 PM
- Art Walk (first Thursday of every month) drives 40% above-normal evening traffic
- Alberta Street Fair (August) drives 300% above-normal traffic for one weekend

**The construction:** In February 2026, the city began a 14-month streetscape improvement project on Alberta Street between 27th and 30th Avenues. Sidewalk access is intermittently restricted. Traffic patterns have shifted. Foot traffic to all Alberta businesses is down an estimated 15–25% depending on proximity to construction.

**Competitive analysis:**
Luca's opened 18 months after Via Trattoria, three blocks away, with a nearly identical concept. Luca's owner (David Chen) has deeper pockets and a PR background. Luca's has been aggressive on social media and has siphoned approximately 12% of Via Trattoria's potential new-customer traffic. However, Luca's consistency issues (inconsistent pasta texture, uneven service) have prevented them from capturing Via Trattoria's regulars.

## 1.8 Positioning

Via Trattoria occupies the sweet spot between everyday and special occasion. You can come for Tuesday night pasta ($18) or Saturday anniversary ($150 with wine). The restaurant is accessible enough for regulars, polished enough for dates. This dual positioning is intentional — it insulates the business from economic downturns (regulars carry the slow months) while capturing premium occasions (higher spend on weekends).

## 3.1 Menu Philosophy

Via Trattoria operates on a four-season menu cycle. Each season introduces approximately 40% new items while retaining 60% of the core. The goal: regulars always find something familiar, but there is always something new.

Menu pricing is reviewed quarterly. Prices have increased approximately 18% cumulatively since opening, driven primarily by supplier cost increases.

## 10.2 How Costs Connect

```
Beef Price → Bolognese Cost → Food Cost %
  $6.50/lb → $1.63/portion → Bolognese CM = $16.50
  $7.00/lb (potential) → $1.75/portion → CM drops to $16.38
  Impact: $0.12 per bowl × 340 bowls/month = $41/month per $0.50/lb increase
```

# SECTION 11 — HIDDEN TRUTHS

*These are problems Marco does not understand. Restaurant OS will discover them.*

## Hidden Truth #1 — The Lunch Decline Is Construction, Not Menu

**What Marco thinks:** Lunch is down because the menu is wrong.

**Reality:** Lunch traffic began declining in February 2026 — the same week Alberta Street construction started. The decline correlates 0.87 with construction phase proximity. The menu is fine. The access is not.

**Why Marco does not see this:** He does not overlay external data with internal sales. He compares lunch sales to last year, not to external events.

**Restaurant OS action:** Cross-reference sales data with construction timeline. Recommendation: express lunch + timing expectation (construction ends Aug 2027).

## Hidden Truth #2 — Food Cost Creep Is Not a Single Problem

**What Marco thinks:** Food cost is high because everything is more expensive.

**Reality:** 60% is supplier price increases. 25% is waste (construction forecasting errors + cooler spoilage). 15% is portion inconsistency (line cooks are not scaling properly since losing Chris Williams).

**Why Marco does not see this:** He tracks food cost as a single number. He does not decompose it into components.

**Restaurant OS action:** Break food cost into: supplier pass-through, waste decomposition, portion variance. Three different cures.

## Hidden Truth #3 — Labor Cost Is Driven by Turnover, Not Covers

**What Marco thinks:** Labor is 34% because sales are soft (fixed costs).

**Reality:** Labor cost is 34% because FOH turnover cost $18,000 last year. Kitchen turnover cost $12,000. The cost of recruiting, training, and below-peak performance is 1.2% of revenue — almost entirely hidden.

**Why Marco does not see this:** Training costs are buried in payroll. He does not track "time to proficiency" or "cost per hire."

**Restaurant OS action:** Surface total cost of turnover. Show that retaining 2 more servers per year would reduce labor cost to 32.5%.

## Hidden Truth #4 — The Walk-In Cooler Is Costing More to Ignore Than to Fix

**What Marco thinks:** $3,200 is too expensive for a non-urgent repair.

**Reality:** The cooler is causing $300/month in spoilage. It has been deferred for 8 months. That is $2,400 in spoilage so far — with no end in sight. Payback period on repair: 11 months.

**Why Marco does not see this:** Spoilage is a line item. The cooler repair is a line item. He does not connect them.

**Restaurant OS action:** Show total cost of deferred maintenance. Quantify the spoilage delta.

## Hidden Truth #5 — Delivery Is a Customer-Quality Problem, Not a Profit Problem

**What Marco thinks:** Delivery is unprofitable because of commission fees.

**Reality:** Delivery is unprofitable AND it is converting one-time customers at 70% rate (vs. 62% for dine-in). The delivery channel is producing low-lifetime-value customers who also generate negative margin transactions. Worst of both worlds.

**Why Marco does not see this:** He looks at delivery revenue (growing) and delivery cost (negative). He does not look at delivery customer lifetime value.

**Restaurant OS action:** Show LTV by channel. Compare delivery vs. dine-in vs. takeout customer value over 12 months. Recommend: reduce delivery marketing, increase takeout.

## Hidden Truth #6 — The Menu Price Increase Was Applied Wrong

**What Marco thinks:** A 3% across-the-board price increase was fair and simple.

**Reality:** Across-the-board pricing ignores item elasticity. Bolognese (18% of entrees, highest volume) could absorb 8% without traffic impact. Grilled Octopus (low volume, high price sensitivity) should not have been increased at all. The optimal increase was 4–5% on high-volume, low-elasticity items, and 0% on low-volume, elastic items.

**Why Marco does not see this:** He does not model price elasticity per item.

**Restaurant OS action:** Show item-level price elasticity. Recommend targeted increases. Result: achieve same margin improvement with half the revenue risk.

## Hidden Truth #7 — Marco Is Burning Out

**What Marco thinks:** This is just what running a restaurant feels like. 65 hours/week. No vacations. Checking 6 systems before brushing his teeth.

**Reality:** Marco's decision quality is measurably declining. He implemented a failed lunch pilot (Jan–Apr 2025). He applied a blunt across-the-board price increase (Mar 2026). He is deferring maintenance (cooler, POS, ice machine). He has not reviewed his P&L in 3 months. The signs of operator fatigue are present.

**Why Marco does not see this:** No one is telling him. He is the owner.

**Restaurant OS action:** Restaurant OS does not tell Marco he is burning out. It reduces his cognitive load. It makes decisions for him. It gives him time back. The product serves him by being the thing he does not need to worry about.
