## CONSTITUTIONAL STATEMENT

Evidence is the justification layer of Restaurant OS.

Observation captures reality. Pattern discovers structure. **Evidence justifies.**

Every claim the system makes — every Pattern, every piece of Knowledge, every Recommendation, every Narrative, every Decision — must be justified by Evidence. Without Evidence, a claim is a belief. With Evidence, a claim is a supported conclusion.

Evidence is not data. Evidence is not an Observation. Evidence is not a Pattern. Evidence is the relationship between Observations and the claims they support.

Evidence answers: "Why do we believe this?"

Nothing in Restaurant OS is believed without Evidence. Nothing is recommended without Evidence. Nothing is communicated without Evidence.

## Part 1 — Philosophy of Evidence

### 1.1 Why Evidence Exists

Evidence exists because the system must justify its beliefs. Without Evidence, the system would have no basis for distinguishing between a well-founded conclusion and a guess. Every Pattern would be equally plausible. Every Recommendation would be equally trustworthy. Every Narrative would be equally credible.

Evidence is the mechanism by which the system earns the right to its beliefs. It transforms "I think" into "I know because."

Evidence exists to implement Constitution Article 4 (Every Recommendation Must Be Explainable), Article 5 (Knowledge Is Earned), and Article 14 (Restaurant OS Admits Mistakes). Without Evidence, these articles cannot be satisfied.

### 1.2 Evidence as Justification

Evidence is a relationship between two cognitive artifacts:

```
[Supporting Artifact] ──Evidence──> [Claim]
```

Where:
- The **Supporting Artifact** is an Observation, Pattern, or existing Knowledge
- The **Claim** is a Pattern, Knowledge statement, Recommendation, Narrative, or Decision
- **Evidence** is the justification that connects them

Evidence is not the Observation itself. Evidence is the act of citing the Observation in support of a claim. The same Observation may serve as Evidence for multiple claims. The same claim may be supported by multiple pieces of Evidence.

Evidence answers: "What makes you say that?"

### 1.3 Evidence vs. Observation

| Dimension | Observation | Evidence |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Definition** | A verified fact about the business | A justification connecting a fact to a claim |
| **Independence** | Standalone (exists regardless of use) | Relational (exists only in connection to a claim) |
| **Lifecycle** | Permanent once captured | Created when Observation is cited; evolves as support accumulates |
| **Purpose** | Capture reality | Justify beliefs |
| **Example** | "Revenue: $4,287 on Tuesday" | "Tuesday revenue of $4,287 supports the Pattern 'Tuesday is slower than Friday'" |

An Observation is a fact. Evidence is a fact in service of a claim. Without being cited as support, an Observation is not Evidence — it is just an Observation.

### 1.4 Evidence vs. Pattern

| Dimension | Evidence | Pattern |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Definition** | Justification for a claim | A recurring structure in Observations |
| **Role** | Supports or refutes | Describes what repeats |
| **Dependency** | Depends on both the support and the claim | Depends only on Observations |
| **Lifecycle** | Created when cited; evolves through weighting | Discovered; evolves through validation |
| **Example** | "Observations A, B, C support Pattern X" | "Tuesday is consistently slower than Friday" |

Patterns require Evidence. A Pattern without Evidence is an unsupported claim. Evidence without a Pattern is a fact looking for a structure.

### 1.5 Evidence vs. Knowledge

| Dimension | Evidence | Knowledge |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Justification for a claim | Validated understanding |
| **Role** | Supports beliefs | Is the belief |
| **Permanence** | May be temporary (if support is challenged) | Stable once validated |
| **Confidence** | Carries weight that contributes to Knowledge confidence | Has confidence derived from supporting Evidence |

Knowledge is the destination. Evidence is the path. Knowledge without Evidence is dogma. Evidence without Knowledge is potential.

### 1.6 Evidence vs. Confidence

| Dimension | Evidence | Confidence |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Definition** | Justification for a claim | Assessment of certainty |
| **Nature** | Structural (connects support to claim) | Quantitative (expresses certainty) |
| **Relationship** | Evidence determines confidence | Confidence reflects Evidence quality |
| **Dependency** | Evidence exists independently | Confidence depends on Evidence |

Confidence is derived from Evidence. The system does not assign confidence directly to a claim — it evaluates the Evidence supporting the claim and derives confidence from that evaluation. This implements Constitution Article 3 (Confidence Before Speed).

### 1.7 Evidence vs. Proof

| Dimension | Evidence | Proof |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Definition** | Justification for a claim | Irrefutable demonstration of truth |
| **Certainty** | Always carries uncertainty | Implies complete certainty |
| **Attainability** | Achievable for any claim | Rarely achievable in business |
| **Example** | "Three sources confirm this Pattern" | "The restaurant opened at 11:00 AM — proven by the POS system's first transaction" |

Restaurant OS deals in Evidence, not proof. Proof implies certainty that the system cannot claim (Constitution Article 3). The system says "we have strong Evidence" not "this is proven."

### 1.8 Evidence vs. Signal

| Dimension | Evidence | Signal |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Definition** | Justification for a claim | An Observation carrying predictive information |
| **Role** | Supports reasoning | Flags potential significance |
| **Temporality** | Evaluated when cited | Detected at observation time |
| **Relationship** | A Signal may become Evidence | Not all Signals become Evidence |

A Signal is an Observation that might matter. Evidence is an Observation that does matter — it has been cited in support of a claim.

### 1.9 Evidence vs. Fact

| Dimension | Evidence | Fact |
|-----------|----------|------|
| **Definition** | Justification for a claim | A statement that is true |
| **Certainty** | Probabilistic | Absolute |
| **Scope** | Claim-specific | Universal within context |
| **Example** | "This Observation supports the claim" | "The restaurant served 142 covers on Tuesday" |

Facts are the foundation. Evidence is the use of facts to support claims. A fact is not Evidence until it is cited.

### 1.10 Evidence vs. Belief

| Dimension | Evidence | Belief |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Definition** | Justification for a claim | An accepted conclusion |
| **Validation** | Must be traceable, verifiable | May be held without justification |
| **Role** | Supports or refutes | Is the content of understanding |
| **Dependency** | Evidence precedes belief | Belief depends on Evidence |

The system does not hold beliefs. It holds conclusions supported by Evidence. If Evidence is withdrawn, the conclusion is withdrawn. The system never maintains a belief after its supporting Evidence has been invalidated.

### 1.11 Evidence vs. Explanation

| Dimension | Evidence | Explanation |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Justification for a claim | The reasoning that connects Evidence to conclusion |
| **Role** | Provides the "what" of support | Provides the "how" and "why" |
| **Relationship** | Evidence is input to Explanation | Explanation is the narrative built on Evidence |

Explanation uses Evidence. Evidence is the raw material. Explanation is the structured story that makes the Evidence meaningful to the Owner. This implements Product Principle 7 (Explain Before Recommend).

### 1.12 Why Restaurant OS Never Assumes Evidence

Evidence is never assumed. It must be:
1. **Collected** — The system must actively gather it
2. **Verified** — The system must confirm it is genuine
3. **Qualified** — The system must assess its quality
4. **Weighted** — The system must determine its significance
5. **Cited** — The system must explicitly connect it to a claim

Assumed Evidence is a contradiction. If it is assumed, it is not Evidence — it is a belief masquerading as justification.

The system does not say "there must be Evidence for this." It finds the Evidence or does not make the claim.

## Part 2 — Evidence Lifecycle

### 2.1 Lifecycle Model

Evidence begins as a potential connection between an artifact and a claim and evolves through stages of collection, verification, qualification, weighting, and eventual retirement.

```
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  POTENTIAL            │
                  │  EVIDENCE             │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Identified as potentially
                             │ supporting a claim
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  COLLECTED            │
                  │  EVIDENCE             │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Authenticity and
                             │ relevance verified
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  VERIFIED             │
                  │  EVIDENCE             │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Quality assessed
                             │ across all dimensions
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  QUALIFIED            │
                  │  EVIDENCE             │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Weight assigned
                             │ based on quality
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  WEIGHTED             │
                  │  EVIDENCE             │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Actively cited in
                             │ support of a claim
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  SUPPORTING           │
                  │  EVIDENCE             │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Claim promoted
                             │ to Knowledge
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  KNOWLEDGE            │
                  │  EVIDENCE             │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Evidence no longer
                             │ actively supporting
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  RETIRED              │
                  │  EVIDENCE             │
                  └──────────┬───────────┘
                             │ Archived with
                             │ full lifecycle
                             ▼
                  ┌──────────────────────┐
                  │  HISTORICAL           │
                  │  EVIDENCE             │
                  └──────────────────────┘
```

### 2.2 Stage Definitions

#### Stage 1 — POTENTIAL EVIDENCE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | A relationship between a supporting artifact (Observation, Pattern, or Knowledge) and a claim has been identified as potentially supportive or contradictory. |
| **Entry** | The system identifies that an artifact may be relevant to a claim under evaluation. |
| **Exit** | Artifact is positively relevant → Collected. Artifact is irrelevant → dismissed. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Collected (relevance confirmed); Dismissed (irrelevant, recorded as dismissed) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Verified (cannot skip collection); → Supporting (cannot support without verification) |
| **Failure Modes** | Relevance blindness (genuinely relevant Evidence not identified); false relevance (irrelevant artifact identified as potentially relevant); Evidence flooding (too many Potential Evidence items, system overwhelmed) |

Potential Evidence is not Evidence. It is a hypothesis that an artifact might support or refute a claim. It exists in Working Memory and is actively evaluated.

#### Stage 2 — COLLECTED EVIDENCE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The supporting artifact has been gathered and its relationship to the claim has been established. |
| **Entry** | The artifact is confirmed to be relevant to the claim. |
| **Exit** | Authenticity and provenance verified → Verified. Artifact found to be inauthentic or provenance broken → discarded. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Verified (authenticity confirmed); Discarded (inauthentic or untraceable, recorded with reason) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Supporting (cannot support without verification); → Knowledge Evidence |
| **Failure Modes** | Collection failure (artifact cannot be retrieved); incomplete collection (partial artifact collected); collection error (wrong artifact collected) |

Collected Evidence is the raw material for justification. The system has the artifact and has confirmed its relevance, but has not yet verified its quality.

#### Stage 3 — VERIFIED EVIDENCE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Evidence has passed verification: the supporting artifact is authentic, its provenance is complete, and its relationship to the claim is genuine. |
| **Entry** | Authenticity and provenance checks pass. |
| **Exit** | Quality assessment complete → Qualified. Verification fails → discarded or returned to Collected. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Qualified (quality assessment complete) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Supporting (cannot support without qualification); → Knowledge Evidence |
| **Failure Modes** | Verification false positive (inauthentic Evidence passes verification); verification false negative (authentic Evidence fails); verification bias (Evidence from trusted sources verified less rigorously) |

Verified Evidence is authenticated. The system can trust that the Evidence is what it claims to be and that it genuinely relates to the claim.

#### Stage 4 — QUALIFIED EVIDENCE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Evidence has been assessed across all quality dimensions: accuracy, reliability, independence, completeness, freshness, consistency, traceability, corroboration, robustness, explainability. |
| **Entry** | Verification complete. Quality assessment begins. |
| **Exit** | Quality profile complete → Weighted. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Weighted (quality assessment complete) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Supporting (cannot support without weighting); → Verified (cannot de-qualify) |
| **Failure Modes** | Quality assessment bias (systematic over- or under-rating of certain Evidence types); incomplete assessment (some quality dimensions not evaluated); assessment inconsistency (same Evidence rated differently in different contexts) |

Qualified Evidence has a quality profile. The system knows how good (or poor) this Evidence is across all relevant dimensions.

#### Stage 5 — WEIGHTED EVIDENCE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Evidence has been assigned a weight based on its quality profile. Weight determines how much this Evidence contributes to the claim's confidence. |
| **Entry** | Quality profile complete. Weight calculation begins. |
| **Exit** | Weight assigned → ready to be cited as Supporting Evidence. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Supporting (active citation); → Verified (if re-evaluation degrades weight below usability threshold) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Collected (cannot return to unverified state) |
| **Failure Modes** | Weight miscalculation (weight does not reflect genuine quality); weight stagnation (weight not updated as Evidence quality changes); weight anchoring (initial weight persists despite quality changes) |

Weighted Evidence has a quantitative contribution value. This is the stage at which Evidence is ready to be used in reasoning.

#### Stage 6 — SUPPORTING EVIDENCE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Evidence is actively cited in support of a claim. It contributes to the claim's confidence and is part of the claim's justification chain. |
| **Entry** | The claim formally cites this Evidence as support. |
| **Exit** | The claim is resolved (promoted, depublished, or rejected) → remains Supporting or transitions. The Evidence may simultaneously support multiple claims. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Knowledge Evidence (the supported claim is promoted to Knowledge); → Verified (if the Evidence is later challenged and re-verification is needed); → Retired (Evidence retired while claim remains active) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Collected (cannot return to pre-verification state while actively cited) |
| **Failure Modes** | Support exaggeration (Evidence cited as stronger than its weight justifies); support dilution (Evidence spread across too many claims, weakening each); support without verification (Evidence used before verification complete) |

Supporting Evidence is active Evidence. It is doing its cognitive job — justifying a claim.

#### Stage 7 — KNOWLEDGE EVIDENCE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Evidence now supports confirmed Knowledge. The claim it supported has been validated and promoted. |
| **Entry** | The supported claim is promoted to Knowledge through the Learning Layer. |
| **Exit** | Permanent. The Evidence serves as Knowledge Evidence until retired. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Retired (Evidence retired while Knowledge remains); → Verified (if Knowledge is challenged and Evidence needs re-verification) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Any earlier lifecycle stage |
| **Failure Modes** | False promotion (Knowledge promoted on insufficient Evidence, contaminating the Evidence's role); Evidence gap (Knowledge has insufficient Evidence but is promoted anyway); Knowledge-Evidence disconnect (Knowledge and Evidence evolve separately, losing their relationship) |

Knowledge Evidence is the highest cognitive role Evidence can achieve. It has contributed to the system's permanent validated understanding.

#### Stage 8 — RETIRED EVIDENCE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Evidence is no longer actively cited. It may have been superseded, challenged, or simply aged out of active use. |
| **Entry** | The Evidence is withdrawn from active support for all claims. |
| **Exit** | Archived to Historical → Historical. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Historical (archived); → Verified (if new context resurrects its relevance) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Supporting (cannot return to active support without re-verification); → Knowledge Evidence |
| **Failure Modes** | Premature retirement (Evidence retired while it still offers value); delayed retirement (Evidence persists in active support after it should have been retired); retirement without learning (Evidence retired but no knowledge extracted) |

Retirement is not deletion. The Evidence remains accessible but is no longer actively contributing to reasoning.

#### Stage 9 — HISTORICAL EVIDENCE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Evidence has been archived with its full lifecycle record: when collected, verified, qualified, weighted, cited, retired, and why. |
| **Entry** | Retired Evidence is archived. |
| **Exit** | Permanent. May be re-activated if new context makes it relevant again. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Potential (rare — new context suggests the Evidence may be relevant to a new claim) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Any active lifecycle stage without a new evaluation cycle |
| **Failure Modes** | Historical loss (Evidence archived without sufficient indexing, unreachable); re-emergence blindness (Evidence becomes relevant again but system does not recognize it) |

Historical Evidence is the system's memory of what it used to rely on. It enables retrospective analysis and re-evaluation.

### 2.3 Lifecycle Principles

1. **Forward motion is the default.** Evidence progresses toward active support.
2. **Stages are not skipped.** Evidence cannot go from Collected to Supporting without passing through Verified, Qualified, and Weighted.
3. **Retirement preserves the record.** Every retired Evidence item retains its full lifecycle history.
4. **Re-activation is possible.** Historical Evidence may be re-evaluated.
5. **Evidence can support multiple claims simultaneously.** Lifecycle stage is per-Evidence, not per-claim.

## Part 3 — Evidence Anatomy

### 3.1 Conceptual Fields

Every piece of Evidence has the following conceptual fields.

#### Identity

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The unique identity of this Evidence item. Distinguishes it from all other Evidence. |
| **Why it exists** | Without identity, Evidence cannot be referenced, traced, related, or weighted. |
| **Cognitive role** | Provides a fixed reference for all relationships and reasoning involving this Evidence. |

#### Description

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | A natural-language statement of what this Evidence is and what claim it supports. |
| **Why it exists** | The description makes the Evidence meaningful to cognition. It answers "what supports what?" |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables explanation generation, narrative composition, and human-readable tracing. |

#### Origin

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The cognitive process that generated this Evidence: Observation capture, Pattern detection, Knowledge validation, Owner feedback, or external source. |
| **Why it exists** | Origin determines how the Evidence should be evaluated. Evidence from different origins has different quality baselines. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables origin-appropriate evaluation, trust calibration, and provenance tracing. |

#### Evidence Type

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The primary category of the Evidence (see Part 4 — Evidence Classification). |
| **Why it exists** | Type determines how the Evidence is processed, weighted, and related. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables type-specific processing, appropriate weighting strategy, and relationship inference. |

#### Supporting Observation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Reference to the Observation that serves as the Evidence's foundation (when the Evidence is Observation-based). |
| **Why it exists** | Observation-based Evidence must trace to its originating Observation. This provides the evidentiary chain. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables Observation→Evidence→Claim tracing, quality inheritance, and confidence cascading. |

#### Supporting Pattern

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Reference to the Pattern that serves as the Evidence's foundation (when the Evidence is Pattern-based). |
| **Why it exists** | Pattern-based Evidence must trace to its originating Pattern. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables Pattern→Evidence→Claim tracing and multi-level justification chains. |

#### Supporting Knowledge

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Reference to the Knowledge that serves as the Evidence's foundation (when the Evidence is Knowledge-based). |
| **Why it exists** | Knowledge-based Evidence is the highest order of justification. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables highest-confidence justification chains and Knowledge-level reasoning. |

#### Business Context

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The Business and specific Restaurant to which this Evidence belongs. |
| **Why it exists** | Evidence is business-specific. Evidence from one business does not automatically apply to another. |
| **Cognitive role** | Scopes all Evidence-based reasoning to the relevant business entity. |

#### Temporal Scope

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The time range over which the Evidence is considered valid. |
| **Why it exists** | Evidence has temporal validity. Old Evidence may no longer support current claims. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables freshness assessment, temporal weighting, and staleness detection. |

#### Operational Scope

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The operational conditions under which the Evidence is valid. |
| **Why it exists** | Evidence may only be valid under specific operational conditions (e.g., during normal operations, not during promotions). |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables context-constrained Evidence evaluation and appropriate weighting. |

#### Environmental Scope

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The environmental conditions under which the Evidence is valid. |
| **Why it exists** | External conditions affect Evidence validity. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables environment-aware Evidence evaluation. |

#### Behavior Scope

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The behavioral dimension the Evidence captures. |
| **Why it exists** | Behavioral Evidence has different properties than operational or financial Evidence. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables behavior-appropriate weighting and evaluation. |

#### Source Reliability

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The assessed reliability of the Evidence's source. |
| **Why it exists** | Evidence from more reliable sources carries more weight. Source reliability is earned over time. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables source-weighted Evidence evaluation and trust calibration. |

#### Evidence Strength

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | A measure of how strongly the Evidence supports the claim. Direct Evidence is stronger than indirect. Multiple independent sources are stronger than one. |
| **Why it exists** | Strength determines the Evidence's contribution to the claim's confidence. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables confidence calculation, comparative claim evaluation, and recommendation certainty. |

#### Evidence Weight

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The composite weight assigned to this Evidence based on its quality profile, strength, and source reliability. |
| **Why it exists** | Weight is the operational value of the Evidence — what it actually contributes to reasoning. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables weighted evidence accumulation, confidence derivation, and multi-Evidence synthesis. |

#### Confidence Contribution

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The specific contribution this Evidence makes to the confidence of the claim it supports. |
| **Why it exists** | Each Evidence item contributes incrementally to claim confidence. Multiple Evidence items combine. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables confidence accumulation, marginal Evidence evaluation, and confidence gap identification. |

#### Quality Profile

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The Evidence's assessment across all quality dimensions (see Part 5 — Evidence Quality). |
| **Why it exists** | Quality determines weight. The quality profile is the Evidence's complete quality picture. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables quality-aware weighting, quality-based retention decisions, and quality trend tracking. |

#### Freshness

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | How recently the Evidence was collected or last verified. |
| **Why it exists** | Stale Evidence contributes less to reasoning than fresh Evidence. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables time-weighted Evidence evaluation, staleness detection, and re-verification triggering. |

#### Persistence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | How long the Evidence has been maintained. Evidence that has persisted through multiple evaluation cycles is more reliable. |
| **Why it exists** | Persistence is a trust signal. Consistently re-verified Evidence earns higher trust. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables persistence-based confidence bonuses and stability assessment. |

#### Importance

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The significance of this Evidence to business outcomes. |
| **Why it exists** | Not all Evidence is equally important. Importance determines attention and retention priority. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables attention prioritization, narrative selection, and resource allocation. |

#### Novelty

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether this Evidence introduces new information or confirms existing understanding. |
| **Why it exists** | Novel Evidence deserves more attention. Confirming Evidence strengthens confidence. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables novelty-based attention, learning opportunity identification, and confidence reinforcement. |

#### Contradiction Count

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The number of active contradictions involving this Evidence. |
| **Why it exists** | Evidence facing active contradictions has reduced confidence. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables contradiction-aware Evidence evaluation, conflict tracking, and resolution triggering. |

#### Supporting Chain

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The complete chain of support: Observation → Evidence → Pattern → Evidence → Knowledge. |
| **Why it exists** | The supporting chain enables full provenance tracing from initial fact to highest-level claim. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables complete provenance, trust auditing, and justification depth assessment. |

#### Relationships

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Links from this Evidence to Observations, Patterns, Knowledge, Recommendations, Narratives, Decisions, and other Evidence. |
| **Why it exists** | Evidence derives its cognitive value from its position in the relationship network. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables relational reasoning, influence tracing, and knowledge graph construction. |

#### Learning Relevance

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether this Evidence has contributed to Learning, and how. |
| **Why it exists** | Evidence that generates learning is more valuable than Evidence that merely confirms. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables learning tracing and meta-cognitive assessment. |

#### Decision Relevance

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether this Evidence has informed a Recommendation or Decision, and how directly. |
| **Why it exists** | Evidence that influences decisions is operationally significant. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables decision tracing and Evidence impact assessment. |

#### Narrative Relevance

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether this Evidence has contributed to a Narrative, and how central it was. |
| **Why it exists** | Evidence that informs Narratives communicates understanding to the Owner. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables narrative tracing and communication impact assessment. |

#### Evolution History

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The complete lifecycle record: when created, each stage transition, weight changes, quality changes, contradictions, re-verifications. |
| **Why it exists** | Evolution history enables the system to understand how its justification has changed. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables learning audit, confidence trajectory analysis, and meta-cognition. |

### 3.2 Field Principles

1. **Every field serves justification.** No field exists for technical convenience.
2. **Fields are not implementation columns.** They are conceptual dimensions of Evidence understanding.
3. **Core fields are present at creation** (Identity, Description, Origin, Evidence Type, Supporting Observation/Pattern/Knowledge, Business Context).
4. **Quality and weight fields fill as the Evidence evolves.** A newly collected Evidence item has minimal quality fields.
5. **Fields are frozen at retirement.** The final field state at retirement is the permanent record.

## Part 4 — Evidence Classification

### 4.1 Classification Model

Every piece of Evidence belongs to exactly one primary category and may belong to zero or more secondary categories. Classification determines how the Evidence is processed, weighted, and related.

### 4.2 Categories

#### Direct Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence that directly supports or refutes a claim without intermediate inference. The supporting artifact speaks directly to the claim. |
| **Examples** | "Tuesday revenue was $4,287" directly supports the claim "Tuesday revenue was below $4,500." |
| **Purpose** | Highest-weight Evidence. Used when available. |
| **Processing priority** | Highest. Direct Evidence is the strongest form of justification. |

#### Indirect Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence that supports a claim through intermediate inference. The connection between support and claim is not immediate. |
| **Examples** | "Weather was rainy Tuesday" indirectly supports "Tuesday revenue was low" (through the known relationship between weather and traffic). |
| **Purpose** | Used when Direct Evidence is unavailable. Lower weight than Direct. |
| **Processing priority** | Medium. Indirect Evidence requires explicit inference chain documentation. |

#### Behavioral Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence derived from observed behavior: what customers, employees, or the Owner actually did. |
| **Examples** | "62% of dessert orders include coffee" supports the claim "coffee and dessert are complementary." |
| **Purpose** | Highly predictive. Behavior reveals intent better than stated preference. |
| **Processing priority** | High. Behavioral Evidence is reliable. |

#### Operational Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence derived from business operations: sales, covers, service times, order accuracy, throughput. |
| **Examples** | "Average lunch service time: 24 minutes" supports the claim "lunch service meets the 25-minute target." |
| **Purpose** | Foundation for operational understanding. Continuously generated. |
| **Processing priority** | Continuous. Operational Evidence is always flowing. |

#### Financial Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence derived from financial data: revenue, costs, margins, profits, percentages. |
| **Examples** | "Food cost percentage: 31.2%" supports the claim "food cost is within target range." |
| **Purpose** | Enables financial health assessment and profitability analysis. |
| **Processing priority** | Continuous. Financial Evidence is generated every period. |

#### Customer Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence derived from customer behavior, feedback, and characteristics. |
| **Examples** | "Customer satisfaction score: 4.2/5" supports the claim "customer experience is positive." |
| **Purpose** | Enables customer understanding, retention analysis, and experience optimization. |
| **Processing priority** | Per-interaction. Customer Evidence arrives with every visit and review. |

#### Environmental Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence from external conditions: weather, events, holidays, competition, economy. |
| **Examples** | "Temperature: 35°C" supports the claim "hot weather conditions apply for demand forecasting." |
| **Purpose** | Provides external context for business performance interpretation. |
| **Processing priority** | Medium. Environmental Evidence is contextual, not primary. |

#### Temporal Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence about timing, duration, frequency, and sequence. |
| **Examples** | "The decline has persisted for 6 consecutive weeks" supports the claim "this is a trend, not a fluctuation." |
| **Purpose** | Enables temporal reasoning, trend detection, and pattern confirmation. |
| **Processing priority** | Medium. Temporal Evidence strengthens or weakens other Evidence types. |

#### Historical Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence drawn from the business's past: historical patterns, prior outcomes, archived observations. |
| **Examples** | "The same supplier price increase occurred in 2024 and resolved within 8 weeks" supports "this price increase is likely temporary." |
| **Purpose** | Enables experience-based reasoning and analogical inference. |
| **Processing priority** | Low (aged). Historical Evidence has lower weight than current Evidence. |

#### Experimental Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence produced through structured experimentation: hypothesis testing, A/B comparisons, controlled trials. |
| **Examples** | "Dessert prices increased $2 → dessert orders dropped 12%, average check rose 3%" supports "dessert demand is elastic above the $14 threshold." |
| **Purpose** | Highest-quality Evidence for causal claims. Produced through deliberate experimentation. |
| **Processing priority** | High (when available). Experimental Evidence is actively produced, not passively collected. |

#### Negative Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence that a claim is NOT supported. Absence of expected confirming Evidence. |
| **Examples** | "No increase in customer complaints during the service slowdown" supports "the slowdown did not negatively impact customer experience." |
| **Purpose** | Enables refutation, confidence reduction, and alternative hypothesis support. |
| **Processing priority** | High (when relevant). Negative Evidence is as important as positive. |

#### Contradictory Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence that directly contradicts a claim or another piece of Evidence. |
| **Examples** | "Customer satisfaction survey shows 4.5/5" contradicts "customer satisfaction is declining (based on comment cards)." |
| **Purpose** | Enables contradiction detection, resolution, and belief updating. |
| **Processing priority** | High. Contradictory Evidence must be addressed before reasoning proceeds. |

#### Corroborating Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence from an independent source that confirms existing Evidence. |
| **Examples** | "POS data shows $4,287" and "Owner's daily report shows $4,300" corroborate each other. |
| **Purpose** | Increases confidence through independent confirmation. |
| **Processing priority** | Medium. Corroboration strengthens existing Evidence. |

#### Predictive Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence that supports a prediction about a future state. |
| **Examples** | "Current trajectory suggests inventory will reach critical level in 3 days" supports "order supplies by Thursday." |
| **Purpose** | Enables forward-looking reasoning and preventative action. |
| **Processing priority** | Medium-High. Predictive Evidence enables proactive decisions. |

#### Diagnostic Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence that helps determine the cause of an observed effect. |
| **Examples** | "Construction started on Main Street the same week traffic declined" supports "construction caused the traffic decline." |
| **Purpose** | Enables root cause analysis and causal attribution. |
| **Processing priority** | High (during investigation). Diagnostic Evidence is critical for understanding anomalies. |

#### Strategic Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence that informs long-term strategic decisions: market trends, competitive dynamics, demographic shifts. |
| **Examples** | "Three-year trend shows 15% annual growth in delivery channel" supports "invest in delivery infrastructure." |
| **Purpose** | Enables strategic planning and long-term investment decisions. |
| **Processing priority** | Low (not time-sensitive). Strategic Evidence influences periodic planning cycles. |

#### Meta Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence about the Evidence system itself: Evidence quality trends, Evidence coverage gaps, Evidence lifecycle statistics. |
| **Examples** | "Evidence verification success rate: 99.2%" supports "the Evidence system is operating reliably." |
| **Purpose** | Enables system self-assessment and meta-cognitive improvement. |
| **Processing priority** | Periodic. Meta Evidence is evaluated during cognitive health reviews. |

#### Composite Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Definition** | Evidence synthesized from multiple lower-level Evidence items. A unified justification built from contributing parts. |
| **Examples** | Combined Evidence from "POS data," "Owner report," and "customer feedback" supporting "Tuesday revenue was below normal." |
| **Purpose** | Enables higher-order justification from diverse sources. |
| **Processing priority** | By component priority (highest-priority component determines composite priority). |

### 4.3 Classification Principles

1. **Every Evidence item has exactly one primary category.** This determines its primary processing path.
2. **An Evidence item may have multiple secondary categories.** Behavioral + Financial Evidence is valid.
3. **Category determines weighting strategy.** Direct Evidence weights differently from Indirect.
4. **Category may evolve.** Evidence collected as Direct may become Indirect if its provenance chain is partially broken.

## Part 5 — Evidence Quality

### 5.1 Quality Dimensions

Every piece of Evidence is assessed across ten quality dimensions. Quality determines weight.

#### Accuracy

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Evidence correctly represents reality. Does the supporting artifact's value match what actually occurred? |
| **Assessment** | Cross-validation with independent sources. Consistency check with related Evidence. Domain invariant enforcement. |
| **Failure detection** | Contradiction with higher-quality Evidence. Implausible values. Owner correction. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Flag for accuracy review. (2) Reduce weight. (3) If confirmed inaccurate, retire with full documentation. (4) Create corrected Evidence. (5) Audit downstream claims that depended on the inaccurate Evidence. |

#### Reliability

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Evidence comes from a source that consistently produces accurate information. |
| **Assessment** | Source trust tracking over time. Historical accuracy rate of the source. |
| **Failure detection** | Source produces increasingly inaccurate Evidence. Source's accuracy rate drops below threshold. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Reduce source reliability rating. (2) Reduce weight of all Evidence from this source. (3) Increase cross-validation requirements for this source. (4) If source becomes unreliable, retire its Evidence and seek alternatives. |

#### Independence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Evidence is independent of other Evidence supporting the same claim. Independent Evidence is more valuable than dependent. |
| **Assessment** | Source correlation analysis. Provenance chain comparison. Shared origin detection. |
| **Failure detection** | Two Evidence items trace to the same original Observation. Two Evidence items share a compromised source. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Flag dependent Evidence as non-independent. (2) Reduce weight of dependent items (they do not add as much confidence as independent ones). (3) Adjust confidence accumulation to avoid double-counting. |

#### Completeness

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Evidence carries sufficient context and provenance to be fully evaluated. |
| **Assessment** | Presence of all required fields. Completeness of provenance chain. |
| **Failure detection** | Evidence used in reasoning that requires context the Evidence does not have. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Flag missing fields. (2) Attempt to infer from related Evidence. (3) If inference succeeds, mark as "inferred." (4) If inference fails, reduce weight proportional to completeness gap. |

#### Freshness

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How recently the Evidence was collected or last verified. |
| **Assessment** | Time since collection or last re-verification. Domain-specific freshness requirements. |
| **Failure detection** | Evidence age exceeds domain freshness threshold. Claim depending on stale Evidence is about to be made. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Mark as stale. (2) Reduce weight. (3) Trigger re-collection or re-verification. (4) If source unavailable, note the gap for the Owner. |

#### Consistency

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Evidence is internally consistent and consistent with established Knowledge. |
| **Assessment** | Internal logic check. Cross-reference with related Evidence. Comparison with established Patterns and Knowledge. |
| **Failure detection** | Evidence contradicts itself. Evidence contradicts well-established Knowledge without justification. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Create contradiction record. (2) Attempt reconciliation. (3) If reconciled, document resolution. (4) If not reconciled, reduce weight and flag for investigation. |

#### Traceability

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Evidence can be traced to its originating Observation with complete provenance. |
| **Assessment** | Presence and completeness of Supporting Chain. Ability to follow from Evidence → Observation → Source. |
| **Failure detection** | Evidence with missing, incomplete, or broken provenance chain. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Flag untraceable Evidence. (2) Reduce weight. (3) Attempt to recover provenance. (4) If unrecoverable, mark as "traceability-limited" and exclude from high-confidence reasoning. |

#### Corroboration

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Evidence is supported by independent corroborating Evidence. |
| **Assessment** | Number of independent sources that confirm the same conclusion. |
| **Failure detection** | Evidence stands alone with no corroboration. Evidence is contradicted by other Evidence. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess corroboration level. (2) Adjust weight up (with corroboration) or down (without). (3) Seek additional sources if weight is insufficient for the claim. |

#### Robustness

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How resilient the Evidence is to challenges, noise, and context changes. |
| **Assessment** | Sensitivity analysis: does the Evidence hold under different conditions? |
| **Failure detection** | Evidence collapses under mild challenge. Evidence is valid only under narrow conditions. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess robustness boundaries. (2) Document fragility conditions. (3) Reduce weight when operating near fragility boundaries. (4) If too fragile to be useful, retire. |

#### Explainability

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Evidence can be explained in business terms. Can the system articulate why this Evidence supports the claim? |
| **Assessment** | The system can produce a natural-language explanation of the Evidence-claim relationship. |
| **Failure detection** | Evidence passes quality checks but the system cannot explain why it supports the claim. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Attempt to articulate the connection. (2) If articulation succeeds, document. (3) If articulation fails, reduce weight (unexplained Evidence is less valuable for communication). |

### 5.2 Quality Principles

1. **Every Evidence item has a quality profile.** Quality is assessed at creation and continuously updated.
2. **Quality determines weight.** Higher-quality Evidence contributes more to reasoning.
3. **Quality can change.** Evidence may improve (re-verified, corroborated) or degrade (stale, challenged).
4. **Quality failures are not deletions.** Low-quality Evidence is preserved with its quality annotation.
5. **Quality is re-evaluated on use.** An Evidence item's quality at creation may differ from its quality at time of use.

## Part 6 — Evidence Relationships

### 6.1 Evidence → Observation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Evidence may be directly supported by an Observation. The Observation is the foundational fact. |
| **Direction** | Observation → Evidence (Observation is cited as Evidence). |
| **Cardinality** | An Observation may support many Evidence items. An Evidence item may trace to one primary Observation. |
| **Cognitive significance** | This is the most fundamental Evidence relationship. Observation-based Evidence is the system's ground truth. |

### 6.2 Evidence → Pattern

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Evidence may support a Pattern. The Evidence is the justification that the Pattern exists. |
| **Direction** | Evidence → Pattern (Evidence supports Pattern) AND Pattern → Evidence (Pattern's supporting Observations are Evidence for the Pattern). |
| **Cardinality** | An Evidence item may support multiple Patterns. A Pattern is supported by multiple Evidence items. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Patterns without Evidence are speculation. This relationship grounds every Pattern in justification. |

### 6.3 Evidence → Knowledge

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Evidence may support Knowledge. Knowledge is the highest-level claim that Evidence can justify. |
| **Direction** | Evidence → Knowledge (Evidence supports Knowledge via Pattern). |
| **Cardinality** | An Evidence item may support multiple Knowledge statements. Knowledge is supported by multiple Evidence items (through Patterns). |
| **Cognitive significance** | This relationship completes the chain: Observation → Evidence → Pattern → Evidence → Knowledge. |

### 6.4 Evidence → Recommendation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Evidence may be cited in a Recommendation. The Recommendation's reasoning depends on the Evidence. |
| **Direction** | Evidence → Recommendation (Evidence supports the Recommendation's claim). |
| **Cardinality** | An Evidence item may support multiple Recommendations. A Recommendation requires at least one Evidence item. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Every Recommendation must trace to Evidence (Domain Model Invariant 13). This relationship implements that invariant. |

### 6.5 Evidence → Decision

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Evidence may be traced to a Decision made by the Owner. The Decision was informed by (or at least aware of) this Evidence. |
| **Direction** | Evidence → Decision (Evidence informed the Decision). |
| **Cardinality** | An Evidence item may inform many Decisions. A Decision may reference many Evidence items. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Decision-Evidence tracing enables the system to evaluate whether decisions were well-founded. |

### 6.6 Evidence → Narrative

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Evidence may be referenced within a Narrative to support the story being told. |
| **Direction** | Evidence → Narrative (Evidence provides justification for the Narrative's claims). |
| **Cardinality** | An Evidence item may appear in multiple Narratives. A Narrative typically references multiple Evidence items. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Narratives without Evidence are stories. This relationship transforms stories into justified accounts. |

### 6.7 Evidence → Learning

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Evidence may trigger or contribute to Learning when it provides outcome data, contradicts existing understanding, or reveals a gap. |
| **Direction** | Evidence → Learning (Evidence enables Learning). |
| **Cardinality** | An Evidence item may contribute to multiple Learning events. A Learning event typically involves multiple Evidence items. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Learning is the extraction of knowledge from Evidence. This relationship closes the cognitive loop. |

### 6.8 Evidence → Memory

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Evidence is stored in multiple memory systems: Working Memory (during evaluation), Pattern Memory (cited evidence for Patterns), Decision Memory (cited evidence for Decisions), Learning Memory (evidence that generated learning). |
| **Direction** | Evidence → Memory (Evidence is stored in Memory). |
| **Cardinality** | Each Evidence item exists in at least Historical Memory and may simultaneously exist in other memory systems. |
| **Cognitive significance** | Memory is where Evidence lives. The Evidence-Memory relationship determines how quickly Evidence can be retrieved for reasoning. |

### 6.9 Evidence → Confidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Evidence determines Confidence. The quality, weight, and quantity of Evidence for a claim determine the claim's confidence. |
| **Direction** | Evidence → Confidence (Evidence drives Confidence). |
| **Cardinality** | Multiple Evidence items collectively determine a claim's confidence. An Evidence item may contribute to the confidence of multiple claims. |
| **Cognitive significance** | This is the most consequential Evidence relationship. Everything the system believes is ultimately a function of the Evidence supporting it. |

### 6.10 Evidence → Business Pulse

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Relationship** | Evidence collectively informs Business Pulse. The health of the Evidence ecosystem — collection rate, verification rate, contradiction rate, quality trends — reflects the system's cognitive health. |
| **Direction** | Evidence → Business Pulse (aggregate). |
| **Cardinality** | All active Evidence contributes to Pulse. |
| **Cognitive significance** | A system with high-quality, consistent Evidence has high Pulse. A system facing many contradictions or quality problems has degrading Pulse. |

## Part 7 — Evidence Invariants

### 7.1 Identity Invariants (1–5)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | Every Evidence item has a unique identity. | Identity collision. The system confuses two different justifications. |
| 2 | Identity is assigned at creation and never changes. | Identity mutation. Evidence becomes untraceable across its lifecycle. |
| 3 | Every Evidence item has exactly one identity. | Identity multiplicity. The same justification appears as multiple distinct Evidence items. |
| 4 | Identity is independent of the claim it supports. | Identity-claim coupling. The same Evidence cited for a different claim appears as a new Evidence item. |
| 5 | Identity survives retirement. Even retired Evidence retains its identity. | Identity reclamation. A retired Evidence item's identity could be reused. |

### 7.2 Evidence Creation Invariants (6–12)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 6 | Evidence is never created without a claim. Evidence is always cited in support of or against a specific claim. | Claimless Evidence. A justification exists for nothing. |
| 7 | Evidence is never created without a supporting artifact. Evidence always traces to an Observation, Pattern, or Knowledge. | Artifactless Evidence. A justification exists without a foundation. |
| 8 | Evidence is never assumed. It must be actively collected and verified. | Assumed Evidence. The system treats belief as justification. |
| 9 | Evidence is never invented. Fabricated Evidence is prohibited. | Fabricated Evidence. The system creates false justification. |
| 10 | Evidence creation is recorded. The time, origin, and context of creation are always documented. | Creation amnesia. The system cannot audit when or why Evidence was created. |
| 11 | Evidence cannot be created after the claim is resolved. Evidence must exist before or during claim evaluation. | Retroactive Evidence. Evidence appears to support a claim after the claim was already decided. |
| 12 | Evidence may be created for or against a claim. Evidence supports or refutes; it does not predetermine the conclusion. | Confirmation-only Evidence. The system only creates Evidence that confirms its expectations. |

### 7.3 Evidence Provenance Invariants (13–18)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 13 | Every Evidence item has complete provenance. The chain from Evidence to its originating artifact is fully traceable. | Provenance gap. The system cannot verify the Evidence's foundation. |
| 14 | Provenance is immutable. The provenance chain never changes after the Evidence is created. | Provenance revision. The system alters the justification chain. |
| 15 | Provenance depth is recorded. The system knows whether Evidence is direct (Observation-level) or indirect (Pattern or Knowledge-level). | Provenance ambiguity. The system does not know how grounded the Evidence is. |
| 16 | A provenance break reduces Evidence weight. If the chain is broken at any point, weight is reduced proportionally. | Provenance-independent weight. Evidence weight is unaffected by provenance integrity. |
| 17 | Multi-hop provenance is valid but carries lower weight than direct. Evidence through Pattern → Knowledge is valid but weaker than direct Observation Evidence. | Equal provenance. All Evidence is treated equally regardless of provenance depth. |
| 18 | Provenance is auditable. Any Evidence item can be traced to its originating Observation. | Provenance isolation. Evidence exists without auditable foundation. |

### 7.4 Evidence Independence Invariants (19–23)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 19 | Evidence independence is mandatory for confidence accumulation. Dependent Evidence does not add full confidence. | False independence. The system double-counts dependent Evidence. |
| 20 | Two Evidence items that trace to the same original Observation are not independent. | Independence illusion. Corroboration is claimed where none exists. |
| 21 | Evidence from the same source is not fully independent. Source correlation reduces independence. | Source independence error. Multiple items from the same source are treated as independent. |
| 22 | Independence is assessed at use time, not creation time. Independence may change as the Evidence ecosystem evolves. | Static independence. Independence assessed once and never re-evaluated. |
| 23 | Evidence independence is documented. The system records which Evidence items are dependent on which others. | Independence amnesia. The system cannot reconstruct its independence assessments. |

### 7.5 Evidence Quality Invariants (24–30)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 24 | Every Evidence item has a quality profile. Quality is assessed across all ten dimensions. | Unqualified Evidence. The system does not know how good the Evidence is. |
| 25 | Quality is assessed at creation and continuously updated. | Static quality. Evidence quality is never updated after creation. |
| 26 | Quality assessment is independent of the claim. Evidence is not rated higher because it supports a preferred conclusion. | Quality bias. Evidence that confirms expectations is rated higher than it deserves. |
| 27 | A quality failure in one dimension is not compensated by excellence in another. Each dimension contributes independently. | Quality compensation. High accuracy compensates for poor freshness. |
| 28 | Quality trends are tracked. Improving or degrading quality is documented. | Quality amnesia. The system cannot detect quality trajectories. |
| 29 | Quality determines weight. Lower quality → lower weight. | Quality-weight disconnect. Evidence weight does not reflect quality. |
| 30 | Quality below minimum threshold triggers review or retirement. | Quality floor violation. Extremely low-quality Evidence remains active. |

### 7.6 Evidence Lifecycle Invariants (31–38)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 31 | Evidence lifecycle moves forward only. No stage regression beyond one level. | Lifecycle reversal. Evidence returns to an earlier stage, invalidating all reasoning that depended on it. |
| 32 | Every Evidence item must pass through Verified before reaching Supporting. | Stage skip. Evidence supports a claim without verification. |
| 33 | Every Evidence item must pass through Qualified before reaching Weighted. | Weighting without quality. Evidence is weighted before its quality is known. |
| 34 | A Supporting Evidence item cannot be retired while the claim it supports is active. | Premature retirement. A claim loses justification while still active. |
| 35 | Retirement is not deletion. Retired Evidence is preserved with full lifecycle record. | Evidence deletion. The system loses its justification history. |
| 36 | Retirement must include a reason. | Reasonless retirement. The system cannot learn from why Evidence was retired. |
| 37 | Historical Evidence may be re-activated. Re-activation starts a new lifecycle. | Permanent archival. Historical Evidence is invisible to future cognition. |
| 38 | An Evidence item's lifecycle stage is always known. | Lifecycle ambiguity. The system does not know what stage Evidence is in. |

### 7.7 Confidence Invariants (39–45)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 39 | Claim confidence is derived from supporting Evidence. No Evidence → minimal confidence. | Confidence without Evidence. A claim has confidence without justification. |
| 40 | Confidence is proportional to Evidence weight. Higher-weight Evidence produces higher confidence. | Confidence-weight disconnect. Confidence does not reflect Evidence quality. |
| 41 | Multiple independent Evidence items produce higher confidence than one. | Single-Evidence confidence ceiling. One Evidence item cannot produce the highest confidence. |
| 42 | Contradictory Evidence reduces confidence. The presence of contradictory Evidence is factored into confidence. | Contradiction-blind confidence. Confidence ignores Evidence that contradicts the claim. |
| 43 | Confidence cannot exceed the weakest quality link in the Evidence chain. | Quality-blind confidence. One high-quality Evidence item compensates for several low-quality ones. |
| 44 | Confidence is updated when Evidence changes. New Evidence, degraded Evidence, or retired Evidence all trigger confidence recalculation. | Static confidence. Claim confidence does not change when its Evidence changes. |
| 45 | Confidence is never absolute. Even the strongest Evidence leaves room for uncertainty. | False certainty. The system treats strong Evidence as infallible. |

### 7.8 Relationship Invariants (46–52)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 46 | Every claim must be supported by at least one Evidence item. Claims without Evidence are beliefs. | Claim without Evidence. The system holds an unjustified belief. |
| 47 | Every Recommendation must trace to at least one Evidence item. | Baseless Recommendation. A Recommendation exists without justification. |
| 48 | Every Narrative must trace to at least one Evidence item. | Baseless Narrative. A story is told without justification. |
| 49 | Every Pattern must trace to at least one Evidence item. | Baseless Pattern. A claimed structure exists without justification. |
| 50 | Evidence-claim relationships are recorded. The system always knows which Evidence supports which claims. | Relationship amnesia. The system cannot trace justification. |
| 51 | Evidence-claim relationships are additive. New relationships do not replace existing ones. | Relationship replacement. Citing Evidence for a new claim removes it from a previous claim. |
| 52 | Evidence may support multiple claims simultaneously without degradation. | Single-claim limitation. Evidence can only support one claim at a time. |

### 7.9 Integrity Invariants (53–60)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 53 | The system never fabricates Evidence. Every Evidence item traces to a genuine artifact. | Fabrication. The system creates false justification. |
| 54 | The system never suppresses Contradictory Evidence. Contradictory Evidence is recorded alongside supporting Evidence. | Suppression. The system hides Evidence that contradicts its conclusions. |
| 55 | The system never ignores negative Evidence. Absence of expected supporting Evidence is noted. | Negative Evidence blindness. The system does not notice when expected Evidence is missing. |
| 56 | The system does not privilege Evidence based on source status. A low-trust source's Evidence is evaluated on its own merits. | Source privilege. Evidence from high-trust sources receives less scrutiny. |
| 57 | The system does not privilege Evidence based on claim alignment. Evidence that contradicts the system's preferred conclusion receives equal evaluation. | Confirmation bias. Evidence that challenges the system's beliefs is evaluated less favorably. |
| 58 | Evidence is re-evaluated when its supporting artifact changes. If the underlying Observation or Pattern changes, the Evidence is re-verified. | Evidence-artifact disconnect. The Evidence does not reflect changes in its supporting artifact. |
| 59 | Evidence is re-evaluated when new Contradictory Evidence emerges. | Stale evaluation. Evidence continues to be cited despite new contradictory evidence. |
| 60 | The Evidence system is self-auditing. The system can evaluate its own Evidence health. | System blindness. The system cannot assess its own justification quality. |

### 7.10 Learning Invariants (61–65)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 61 | Every Evidence lifecycle event is a learning opportunity. Creation, verification, weighting, contradiction, retirement all produce learning. | Learning waste. Evidence lifecycle events pass without knowledge extraction. |
| 62 | Retired Evidence must produce a Learning Observation documenting why it was retired and what was learned. | Retirement without learning. The system loses the value of Evidence that ceased to be useful. |
| 63 | Evidence that consistently supports accurate predictions reinforces its own weight. | Prediction-learning disconnect. Accurate predictions do not strengthen the Evidence that produced them. |
| 64 | Evidence that consistently supports failed predictions triggers investigation. | Prediction failure blindness. The system continues to use Evidence that consistently produces incorrect predictions. |
| 65 | Learning from Evidence is exported to the Learning Layer. Evidence is a primary source of system evolution. | Learning isolation. Evidence-level learning is not propagated to the broader cognitive system. |

### 7.11 Conflict Resolution Invariants (66–70)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 66 | Conflicting Evidence is not an error. It signals incomplete understanding. | Conflict suppression. Conflicting Evidence is resolved by ignoring one side. |
| 67 | Conflict resolution must be documented. The system records how conflicts were resolved and why. | Conflict amnesia. The system cannot reconstruct how conflicts were resolved. |
| 68 | Conflict resolution follows the hierarchy: context → source trust → completeness → temporal recency → Owner escalation. | Arbitrary resolution. Conflicts are resolved without principled ordering. |
| 69 | Unresolvable conflicts are escalated to the Owner. The system does not silently pick a side. | Silent resolution. The system resolves conflicts without Owner awareness. |
| 70 | A resolved conflict may be re-opened if new Evidence emerges. | Permanent resolution. A resolved conflict can never be re-examined. |

## Part 8 — Evidence Failure Modes

### 8.1 False Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Evidence is created based on an artifact that does not exist, is incorrect, or is misinterpreted. The justification is not genuine. |
| **Detection** | Supporting artifact cannot be found. Artifact contradicts all other Evidence. Provenance chain is broken. |
| **Mitigation** | Mandatory artifact verification before Evidence creation. Cross-validation with independent sources. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify as false. (2) Retire with full documentation. (3) Immediately flag all claims that depend on this Evidence. (4) Audit downstream reasoning. (5) Communicate any affected Owner communications. (6) Investigate root cause: how did false Evidence pass verification? |

### 8.2 Weak Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Evidence exists but its weight is insufficient to meaningfully support its claim. The justification is too weak to be useful. |
| **Detection** | Evidence weight is below minimum threshold for its claim type. Evidence contributes negligibly to claim confidence. |
| **Mitigation** | Minimum weight thresholds for active Evidence. Weight-claim matching requirements. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify as weak. (2) Assess whether additional Evidence can strengthen it. (3) If yes, seek strengthening and re-weight. (4) If no, retire and seek alternative Evidence for the claim. |

### 8.3 Conflicting Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Two or more Evidence items support incompatible conclusions. The system faces genuine uncertainty about which justification is correct. |
| **Detection** | Evidence items pointing to contradictory conclusions are both active. Claim confidence is suppressed due to conflict. |
| **Mitigation** | Conflict detection at Evidence creation. Mandatory conflict recording. Principled resolution hierarchy. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Record the conflict. (2) Apply resolution hierarchy: context → source trust → completeness → temporal recency → Owner. (3) If resolved, document resolution and adjust weights. (4) If unresolvable, escalate to Owner. (5) Extract learning from the conflict. |

### 8.4 Circular Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Evidence A supports claim B, and claim B (or its derived Knowledge) is used as Evidence for claim A. The justification chain is circular. |
| **Detection** | Evidence provenance graph contains a cycle. A traces to B and B traces to A. |
| **Mitigation** | Acyclic provenance requirement. Provenance graph analysis at Evidence creation. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify the circular chain. (2) Break the cycle: identify which Evidence item can stand independently. (3) Remove the circular reference. (4) If neither Evidence can stand independently, retire both. (5) Adjust discovery parameters to prevent circularity. |

### 8.5 Missing Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | A claim is made without sufficient supporting Evidence. The justification is incomplete. |
| **Detection** | Claim confidence is below threshold. Evidence count is below minimum for the claim type. Expected Evidence is absent. |
| **Mitigation** | Minimum Evidence requirements per claim type. Evidence gap tracking. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify the Evidence gap. (2) Document what Evidence is missing and why. (3) Seek additional Evidence. (4) If Evidence cannot be obtained, reduce claim confidence and communicate gap. |

### 8.6 Contaminated Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Evidence is based on an artifact that has been compromised. The supporting Observation, Pattern, or Knowledge has a quality failure that propagates to the Evidence. |
| **Detection** | Supporting artifact quality drops below threshold. Artifact is deprecated or retired. |
| **Mitigation** | Evidence re-evaluation when supporting artifact changes. Automatic weight reduction on artifact degradation. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify contaminated Evidence. (2) Re-verify the supporting artifact. (3) If artifact is recoverable, adjust Evidence weight. (4) If artifact is not recoverable, retire Evidence. (5) Investigate how contamination occurred. |

### 8.7 Stale Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Evidence has aged beyond its useful lifetime. The business has changed, but the Evidence has not been updated. |
| **Detection** | Evidence freshness score below threshold. Time since last verification exceeds domain requirement. |
| **Mitigation** | Freshness-based weight decay. Automatic re-verification scheduling. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Mark as stale. (2) Reduce weight. (3) Attempt re-collection or re-verification. (4) If successful, restore weight with new timestamp. (5) If unsuccessful, retire with note. |

### 8.8 Fabricated Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | The system creates Evidence without a genuine supporting artifact. The justification is entirely invented. (Distinct from False Evidence — Fabricated is intentional artifact creation; False is unintentional artifact error.) |
| **Detection** | Supporting artifact never existed. Artifact creation time does not match actual observation time. Artifact violates domain invariants. |
| **Mitigation** | Mandatory artifact verification at creation. Source trust requirements. Domain invariant enforcement. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Immediate identification. (2) Retire with emergency documentation. (3) Full audit of all downstream claims. (4) Root cause investigation. (5) System adaptation to prevent recurrence. (6) Full transparency with the Owner. |

### 8.9 Evidence Pollution

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Low-quality, false, or irrelevant Evidence accumulates in the Evidence ecosystem, degrading the quality of Evidence-based reasoning. |
| **Detection** | Increasing noise in Evidence evaluation. Decreasing claim confidence accuracy. Growing number of low-weight Evidence items. |
| **Mitigation** | Quality thresholds for active Evidence. Regular Evidence audits. Importance-weighted Evidence retention. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify pollution sources. (2) Audit low-quality Evidence. (3) Retire Evidence below quality thresholds. (4) Clean downstream reasoning. (5) Address root cause: is a particular source, category, or process producing low-quality Evidence? |

### 8.10 Evidence Saturation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Too many Evidence items support the same claim, creating diminishing returns. Additional Evidence contributes negligibly to confidence. |
| **Detection** | Evidence count is high but confidence is not increasing proportionally. Marginal value of new Evidence approaches zero. |
| **Mitigation** | Diminishing returns model for Evidence accumulation. Evidence diversity preference over quantity. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify saturation. (2) Prioritize Evidence by independence and quality, not quantity. (3) Retire redundant Evidence. (4) Focus Evidence collection on gaps, not saturation. |

## Part 9 — Future Evolution

### 9.1 Collective Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Anonymous Evidence pattern sharing across businesses. "This Evidence structure that supports the Pattern in Restaurant A also appears in Restaurants B and C." |
| **Architecture Fit** | Cross-instance Evidence matching. Anonymous Evidence structure comparison. |
| **Constraints** | Constitution Article 13 (private data never leaves the customer). Only Evidence structure is shared, never Evidence content. |

### 9.2 Cross-Business Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Evidence from one business that is relevant to another (same Owner, same concept, similar market). |
| **Architecture Fit** | Cross-business Evidence relationship. Explicit Owner consent boundary. |
| **Constraints** | Only applies to businesses under the same Owner. Never between unrelated businesses. Owner must explicitly consent. |

### 9.3 Simulation-Derived Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Evidence produced through simulation rather than direct Observation. The system runs "what if" scenarios and uses the outputs as Evidence. |
| **Architecture Fit** | New Evidence origin (Simulation). Simulation-derived Evidence carries lower base weight. |
| **Constraints** | Simulation-derived Evidence is labeled as such. It must be validated against real-world Observations before contributing to high-confidence claims. |

### 9.4 Autonomous Validation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | The system autonomously validates its own Evidence by seeking corroborating or contradictory sources without human intervention. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Evolution Layer (self-directed validation). Autonomous Evidence quality improvement. |
| **Constraints** | Autonomous validation operates within constitutional bounds. The system does not fabricate sources to validate Evidence. |

### 9.5 Evidence Markets (Far Future)

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Multiple cognitive agents within the system "trade" Evidence — one agent produces Evidence that another agent consumes for different reasoning. Evidence has supply and demand. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Multi-agent Reasoning Layer. Evidence marketplace with producer-consumer dynamics. |
| **Constraints** | All Evidence must still meet quality standards. Evidence provenance is never obscured by trading. |

### 9.6 Probabilistic Evidence Networks

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Evidence organized into probabilistic networks where Evidence items have conditional relationships. The weight of Evidence A depends on the state of Evidence B. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Enhanced Evidence→Evidence relationships. Conditional probability modeling. |
| **Constraints** | Conditional relationships must be documented and explainable. Complexity is bounded by cognitive resource limits. |

### 9.7 Causal Evidence Graphs

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Evidence organized into causal graphs where the direction of justification mirrors the direction of causation. Evidence A is justification for claim B because A causes B. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Typed Evidence relationships (causal). Causal direction tracking. |
| **Constraints** | Causal Evidence requires stronger validation than correlational. Causal claims are always qualified with confidence. |

### 9.8 Self-Healing Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Evidence that autonomously detects quality degradation and initiates its own recovery — seeking re-verification, finding corroborating sources, or gracefully retiring. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Evolution Layer integration. Self-monitoring Evidence lifecycle. |
| **Constraints** | Self-healing operates within constitutional bounds. The Evidence does not autonomously change its provenance. |

### 9.9 Meta-Evidence Intelligence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it enables** | Evidence about the Evidence system itself: Evidence quality trends, Evidence coverage gaps, Evidence lifecycle dynamics, Evidence system health. The system understands how it justifies. |
| **Architecture Fit** | Meta Evidence category extended. Evolution Layer integration. |
| **Constraints** | Meta-Evidence serves the Evolution Layer. Infinite regress is bounded by architectural constraints. |
