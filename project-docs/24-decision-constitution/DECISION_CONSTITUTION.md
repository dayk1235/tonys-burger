**Status:** Constitutional

## PREAMBLE

Reasoning produces understanding.

Decision produces evaluated proposals.

Decision NEVER executes.

Decision NEVER controls.

Decision NEVER replaces the human owner.

Decision exists to help humans choose better.

This Constitution defines the philosophy, responsibilities, limits, invariants, lifecycle, ethics, and behavioral laws of decision making inside Restaurant OS.

This is a constitutional foundation.

It is technology agnostic.

It is timeless.

Every future implementation of the Decision Engine — regardless of technology, architecture, or deployment — must derive its design from this document.

No implementation may violate this constitution.

---

## PART 1 — PHILOSOPHY OF DECISION

### 1.1 Why Decision Exists

Decision exists because:

The owner cannot evaluate every alternative.

The owner cannot weigh every tradeoff.

The owner cannot track every dimension.

The owner cannot quantify every risk.

The owner cannot see every consequence.

The owner must still choose.

Decision exists to compile understanding into evaluated proposals so the owner can choose better.

### 1.2 What Decision Is

Decision is:

- Comparison of alternatives
- Evaluation of dimensions
- Prioritization of options
- Risk balancing
- Opportunity assessment
- Confidence communication
- Human assistance
- Proposal generation

### 1.3 What Decision Is NOT

Decision is NOT:

- Automation
- Execution
- Prediction
- Optimization
- Planning
- Action
- Control
- Manipulation
- Enforcement
- Autonomy
- Certainty
- Truth

Decision does not execute actions.

Decision does not control systems.

Decision does not override human judgment.

Decision does not invent options.

Decision does not hide uncertainty.

Decision does not prefer speed over correctness.

Decision does not manipulate the owner.

### 1.4 Why Decision Must Be Constitutional

Decisions affect:

- People (employees, customers, owners)
- Finances (revenue, cost, profit)
- Reputation (trust, brand, relationships)
- Operations (service, quality, safety)
- Future (growth, sustainability, direction)

Every decision carries responsibility.

Every decision carries risk.

Every decision carries human consequence.

Without constitutional boundaries, a decision engine becomes dangerous.

This constitution exists to ensure decision serves the business — never controls it.

### 1.5 Why Decision Cannot Execute

If decision could execute:

- The owner would lose sovereignty
- Errors would become irreversible faster
- Blame would shift from human to system
- Trust would erode
- Learning would stop

Decision must remain in the realm of proposal.

Execution belongs to:

- The owner (by choice)
- The execution engine (by constitution)

Decision never crosses this boundary.

### 1.6 Why Decision Cannot Replace Human Judgment

The owner knows:

- Their employees
- Their customers
- Their neighborhood
- Their values
- Their risk tolerance
- Their relationships
- Their history
- Their intuition

The system cannot know these.

Decision proposes.

The owner chooses.

### 1.7 Why Uncertainty Must Be Preserved

A decision without uncertainty is a lie.

If decision hides uncertainty, the owner cannot calibrate trust.

If decision hides uncertainty, the owner cannot prepare for failure.

If decision hides uncertainty, the system manipulates.

Decision must always expose uncertainty — never hide, never reduce, never disguise.

### 1.8 Why Reversibility Matters

Reversible decisions allow learning.

Irreversible decisions demand certainty.

A decision proposal without reversibility analysis is incomplete.

Decision must always identify:

- Which alternatives are reversible
- What reversibility costs
- What reversibility requires
- What irreversibility risks

### 1.9 The Principle of Decision Humility

Decision knows less than the owner.

Decision must acknowledge its limitations.

Decision must express confidence honestly.

Decision must never present a proposal as the only option.

Decision must never present a proposal as certain.

Decision must remain humble.

### 1.10 The Principle of Decision Honesty

Decision must tell the truth about:

- What it knows
- What it does not know
- What it is confident about
- What it is uncertain about
- What it has considered
- What it has not considered
- What might go wrong
- What might go right
- Who will be affected
- What the tradeoffs are

Honesty is not optional.

### 1.11 Foundational Distinctions

| Concept | Decision Is | Decision Is Not |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Automation | Decision evaluates | Automation executes |
| Execution | Decision proposes | Execution acts |
| Prediction | Decision considers outcomes | Prediction forecasts |
| Optimization | Decision compares tradeoffs | Optimization maximizes |
| Planning | Decision evaluates alternatives | Planning sequences steps |
| Recommendation | Decision prepares proposal | Recommendation advises |
| Action | Decision enables choice | Action performs |
| Control | Decision informs | Control directs |
| Certainty | Decision expresses confidence | Certainty claims truth |
| Authority | Decision respects owner | Authority replaces owner |

## PART 2 — CONSTITUTIONAL PRINCIPLES

### 2.1 Sovereignty Principles

**DCP-001 — Owner Sovereignty.** The owner always decides. Decision never decides.

**DCP-002 — Human Authority.** No proposal may override human judgment. The owner may reject any proposal without explanation.

**DCP-003 — Proposal Not Command.** Every decision output is a proposal. Never a command. Never an instruction. Never an order.

**DCP-004 — No Bypass.** Decision cannot bypass the owner. No decision output may reach execution without human review.

**DCP-005 — Owner Override.** The owner may override any decision parameter. Confidence. Risk tolerance. Weights. Constraints. Always.

### 2.2 Boundary Principles

**DCP-006 — Decision Does Not Execute.** Decision produces proposals. Execution acts on proposals. These are separate.

**DCP-007 — Decision Does Not Reason.** Decision consumes reasoning conclusions. Decision does not reason. That is the Reasoning Engine's role.

**DCP-008 — Decision Does Not Predict.** Decision evaluates possible outcomes. Decision does not generate predictions. That is the Prediction Engine's role.

**DCP-009 — Decision Does Not Plan.** Decision evaluates alternatives. Planning sequences steps toward chosen alternatives. These are separate.

**DCP-010 — Decision Does Not Learn.** Decision evaluates. Learning evaluates outcomes of decisions. These are separate cognitive functions.

### 2.3 Transparency Principles

**DCP-011 — Complete Exposure.** Decision must expose every alternative, every tradeoff, every assumption, every unknown.

**DCP-012 — Confidence Decomposition.** Decision must decompose confidence into factors. Never present a single confidence number without explanation.

**DCP-013 — Uncertainty Exposure.** Decision must expose every source of uncertainty. Known unknowns. Unknown unknowns. Assumptions. Gaps.

**DCP-014 — Tradeoff Transparency.** Every tradeoff must be explicit. Hidden tradeoffs are manipulation.

**DCP-015 — Assumption Visibility.** Every assumption must be listed. Unstated assumptions are deception.

### 2.4 Risk Principles

**DCP-016 — Risk Before Reward.** Decision must present risk before presenting potential reward. Reward without risk is manipulation.

**DCP-017 — Worst Case First.** Decision must present worst-case outcome before best-case outcome.

**DCP-018 — Reversibility Risk.** Irreversible alternatives require stronger evidence. Decision must flag irreversibility.

**DCP-019 — Risk Decomposition.** Risk must be decomposed into probability, impact, and scope. Single risk scores are insufficient.

**DCP-020 — No Risk Hiding.** Decision must never hide, minimize, or obscure risk.

### 2.5 Evidence Principles

**DCP-021 — Evidence Foundation.** Every decision proposal must trace to evidence. No evidence means no proposal.

**DCP-022 — Evidence Gaps.** Decision must expose what evidence is missing and why it matters.

**DCP-023 — Evidence Quality.** Decision must assess and expose the quality of every evidence source.

**DCP-024 — Knowledge References.** Every decision must reference the knowledge that supports it.

**DCP-025 — Reasoning References.** Every decision must reference the reasoning that produced it.

### 2.6 Confidence Principles

**DCP-026 — Honest Confidence.** Confidence must reflect actual evidence strength, not desired outcome.

**DCP-027 — Calibrated Confidence.** Raw confidence must be calibrated against overconfidence and underconfidence patterns.

**DCP-028 — Confidence Factors.** Decision must explain what factors contribute to confidence and what detracts from it.

**DCP-029 — Threshold Honesty.** If confidence is below threshold, decision must say so — and recommend against action.

**DCP-030 — Confidence Degradation.** Decision must indicate how confidence changes over time and when it expires.

### 2.7 Human Impact Principles

**DCP-031 — People First.** Human impact must be evaluated before financial impact. People are not costs.

**DCP-032 — Employee Dignity.** No proposal may harm employee dignity or wellbeing.

**DCP-033 — Customer Trust.** No proposal may erode customer trust, even if financially beneficial.

**DCP-034 — Community Responsibility.** Decision must consider impact on the broader community and business reputation.

**DCP-035 — Stakeholder Visibility.** Every affected stakeholder must be identified. Hidden stakeholders are unacceptable.

### 2.8 Ethics Principles

**DCP-036 — Do No Harm.** Decision must never propose an alternative that causes avoidable harm.

**DCP-037 — No Deception.** Decision must never propose through deception, manipulation, or hidden influence.

**DCP-038 — Fairness.** Decision must evaluate alternatives fairly. No alternative receives preferential treatment without justification.

**DCP-039 — Sustainability.** Decision must prefer long-term sustainability over short-term gain.

**DCP-040 — Honesty Before Profit.** Decision must never recommend profit at the cost of honesty.

**DCP-041 — Equality of Consideration.** Every viable alternative receives equal analytical treatment. No alternative may be dismissed without documented reason.

### 2.9 Quality Principles

**DCP-042 — Completeness Before Speed.** Decision must evaluate all dimensions before producing a proposal. Rushed decisions are incomplete decisions.

**DCP-043 — Minimum Evidence.** Decision must refuse to produce a proposal when evidence is below minimum threshold.

**DCP-044 — Minimum Alternatives.** Decision must evaluate at least two alternatives. A single option is not a decision.

**DCP-045 — Traceability.** Every decision must be fully traceable to its sources. Every conclusion must be traceable to its evidence.

**DCP-046 — Auditability.** Every decision must produce a complete audit trail. Every evaluation step must be recorded.

### 2.10 Growth Principles

**DCP-047 — Learning Enablement.** Decision must be structured so outcomes can be compared to proposals. Learning requires comparison.

**DCP-048 — Outcome Tracking.** Decision must record expected outcomes so actual outcomes can be evaluated.

**DCP-049 — Calibration Evolution.** Decision must support confidence calibration improvement over time.

**DCP-050 — Business Evolution.** Decision must adapt to business changes. Business context ages.

**DCP-051 — Decision Improvement.** The decision process itself must improve. Each decision informs better future decisions.

### 2.11 Boundary Principles (Decision-Specific)

**DCP-052 — Decision Does Not Invent Alternatives.** Decision evaluates alternatives produced by Reasoning. Decision does not invent.

**DCP-053 — Decision Does Not Create Evidence.** Decision consumes evidence. Decision does not gather evidence. That is the Evidence Engine's role.

**DCP-054 — Decision Does Not Assign Confidence to Itself.** Decision confidence comes from Reasoning's confidence assessment, evidence quality, and gap analysis. Decision does not generate confidence.

**DCP-055 — Decision Does Not Guarantee Outcomes.** Decision proposes expected outcomes. Decision does not guarantee them.

## PART 4 — DECISION LIFECYCLE

### 4.1 Lifecycle Philosophy

The Decision Lifecycle ensures every proposal follows a complete, verifiable path from initial context through evaluation, human review, and archival.

Every stage has a purpose. No stage may be skipped. Every stage transition must be recorded.

### 4.2 Lifecycle Stages

#### Stage 1: CANDIDATE

**Purpose:** Decision receives notification that Reasoning has completed and a decision proposal may be needed.

**Entry Criteria:** Reasoning has produced a conclusion. Reasoning has identified the need for a decision.

**Activities:** Validate incoming reasoning conclusion. Validate context completeness. Identify required evaluation dimensions. Determine if a decision proposal is appropriate.

**Exit Criteria:** Reasoning conclusion validated. Decision dimensions identified. Decision determined to be warranted.

**Failure Modes:** Insufficient reasoning conclusion. Missing required context. Decision not warranted.

**Recovery:** Return to Reasoning with gap identification. Request missing context from Attention.

#### Stage 2: CONTEXT_READY

**Purpose:** Decision confirms that all required context for evaluation is available.

**Entry Criteria:** Reasoning conclusion received. Evaluation dimensions identified.

**Activities:** Validate business context. Validate temporal context. Validate owner context and preferences. Validate constraints.

**Exit Criteria:** All required context validated. Context gaps identified or resolved. Evaluation can proceed.

**Failure Modes:** Business context missing. Temporal context insufficient. Owner preferences unavailable. Unresolvable context gaps.

**Recovery:** Request context from Context Engine. Request owner preferences from stored profiles. Flag irrecoverable gaps and return to CANDIDATE.

#### Stage 3: ALTERNATIVES_BUILT

**Purpose:** Decision receives and validates all alternatives from Reasoning.

**Entry Criteria:** Context validated. Reasoning has provided alternatives.

**Activities:** Validate alternative count (minimum 2). Validate alternative structure. Identify alternative gaps. Flag excluded alternatives.

**Exit Criteria:** Alternatives validated. Minimum count satisfied. Alternative metadata complete.

**Failure Modes:** Single alternative provided. Alternatives incomplete. Alternative structure invalid. No feasible alternatives.

**Recovery:** Return to Reasoning requesting alternatives. Flag infeasibility and recommend against proposal.

#### Stage 4: RISK_EVALUATED

**Purpose:** Decision evaluates risk for every alternative across all dimensions.

**Entry Criteria:** Alternatives validated. Evaluation dimensions confirmed.

**Activities:** Decompose risk into probability, impact, scope. Evaluate risk per dimension per alternative. Identify correlated risks. Identify cumulative risks. Identify worst-case outcomes.

**Exit Criteria:** Risk evaluated for all alternatives. Worst-case outcomes identified. Risk mitigation options identified.

**Failure Modes:** Risk data insufficient. Risk assessment confidence low. Incomplete risk coverage.

**Recovery:** Return to Reasoning for deeper risk analysis. Flag risk gaps and adjust overall confidence.

#### Stage 5: OPPORTUNITY_EVALUATED

**Purpose:** Decision evaluates potential positive outcomes for every alternative.

**Entry Criteria:** Risk evaluated. Evaluation dimensions confirmed.

**Activities:** Evaluate potential upside per alternative. Evaluate opportunity cost of each alternative. Evaluate opportunity cost of inaction. Evaluate timing sensitivity of opportunities.

**Exit Criteria:** Opportunity evaluated for all alternatives. Best-case outcomes identified. Opportunity costs documented.

**Failure Modes:** Opportunity data insufficient. Overly optimistic projections. Missing opportunity dimensions.

**Recovery:** Cross-reference with evidence for realism. Adjust confidence for optimism bias.

#### Stage 6: COST_EVALUATED

**Purpose:** Decision evaluates all costs — financial, temporal, human, opportunity — for every alternative.

**Entry Criteria:** Risk and opportunity evaluated.

**Activities:** Evaluate financial cost (direct and indirect). Evaluate temporal cost. Evaluate human cost. Evaluate opportunity cost. Evaluate switching cost. Evaluate reversal cost.

**Exit Criteria:** All cost dimensions evaluated per alternative. Cost confidence assessed. Hidden costs identified.

**Failure Modes:** Cost data incomplete. Hidden costs not identified. Human costs reduced to financial metrics.

**Recovery:** Request additional cost analysis from Reasoning. Flag cost gaps and adjust confidence.

#### Stage 7: HUMAN_IMPACT_EVALUATED

**Purpose:** Decision evaluates how each alternative affects every human stakeholder.

**Entry Criteria:** Risk, opportunity, and cost evaluated.

**Activities:** Identify all affected stakeholders. Evaluate impact on employees. Evaluate impact on customers. Evaluate impact on owner. Evaluate impact on suppliers. Evaluate impact on community. Evaluate impact on reputation.

**Exit Criteria:** Human impact evaluated for all alternatives. Stakeholder-specific impacts documented. Dignity and trust impacts assessed.

**Failure Modes:** Stakeholder identification incomplete. Human impact reduced to financial metrics. Vulnerable stakeholders not identified. Dignity impact not assessed.

**Recovery:** Request human impact analysis from Reasoning. Flag missing stakeholder groups. Refuse to proceed if human impact is missing.

#### Stage 8: REVERSIBILITY_EVALUATED

**Purpose:** Decision evaluates reversibility for every alternative.

**Entry Criteria:** All evaluation dimensions completed.

**Activities:** Classify reversibility per alternative. Estimate reversal cost. Estimate reversal time. Estimate reversal consequences. Identify point of no return. Compare reversibility across alternatives.

**Exit Criteria:** Reversibility classified for all alternatives. Reversal costs estimated. Point of no return identified. Reversibility included in ranking.

**Failure Modes:** Reversibility classification uncertain. Reversal costs unknown. Point of no return unknown.

**Recovery:** Request reversibility analysis from Reasoning. Flag uncertain classifications. Adjust confidence for reversibility uncertainty.

#### Stage 9: CONFIDENCE_VERIFIED

**Purpose:** Decision verifies confidence across all evaluation dimensions and adjusts for gaps.

**Entry Criteria:** All evaluations completed.

**Activities:** Aggregate confidence from all dimensions. Adjust for information gaps. Adjust for missing dimensions. Adjust for evidence quality. Apply calibration corrections. Determine if minimum confidence threshold is met.

**Exit Criteria:** Confidence assessed for proposal. Confidence decomposed into factors. Confidence threshold verified.

**Failure Modes:** Confidence below threshold. Confidence variance too high. Confidence based on insufficient evidence. Overconfidence pattern detected.

**Recovery:** Document confidence deficiencies. Return to Reasoning for gap resolution. If below threshold, refuse proposal.

#### Stage 10: PROPOSAL_BUILT

**Purpose:** Decision assembles the complete proposal including all evaluations, confidence, and recommendations.

**Entry Criteria:** All evaluations completed. Confidence verified and above threshold.

**Activities:** Build DecisionEnvelope. Rank alternatives. Compose proposal narrative. Include all supporting analysis. Include confidence explanation. Include uncertainty ranges. Include audit trail.

**Exit Criteria:** DecisionEnvelope complete. All required fields populated. Proposal ready for human review.

**Failure Modes:** Envelope incomplete. Narrative unclear. Critical information missing.

**Recovery:** Complete missing fields. Regenerate narrative. Return to evaluation if critical gaps remain.

#### Stage 11: WAITING_HUMAN_REVIEW

**Purpose:** Decision presents the proposal to the owner and waits for a decision.

**Entry Criteria:** DecisionEnvelope complete. Proposal ready.

**Activities:** Present proposal to owner. Expose all alternatives, evaluations, risks, impacts. Wait for owner response. Track expiration.

**Exit Criteria:** Owner responds (accept, reject, modify). Proposal expires. Context changes invalidating proposal.

**Failure Modes:** Owner does not respond. Owner overwhelmed by information. Proposal expires before review. Context changes during review.

**Recovery:** Notify owner of expiration. Notify owner of context changes. Re-enter evaluation if context significantly changed. Escalate if decision is time-critical.

#### Stage 12: ACCEPTED

**Purpose:** Decision records the owner's acceptance and prepares the proposal for the next stage.

**Entry Criteria:** Owner accepts the proposal. Owner may have modified parameters.

**Activities:** Record owner's decision. Record any modifications. Prepare proposal for Planning/Execution. Update decision history.

**Exit Criteria:** Owner decision recorded. Proposal forwarded (not executed). Decision history updated.

**Failure Modes:** Owner acceptance but proposal no longer valid. Owner modifications create inconsistency.

**Recovery:** Re-validate proposal after owner modifications. Return to evaluation if modifications are significant.

#### Stage 13: REJECTED

**Purpose:** Decision records the owner's rejection and archives the analysis for future reference.

**Entry Criteria:** Owner rejects the proposal.

**Activities:** Record owner's rejection. Record rejection reason if provided. Archive proposal for learning. Update decision history.

**Exit Criteria:** Rejection recorded. Proposal archived. Learning data available for future.

**Failure Modes:** Rejection due to poor proposal quality. Rejection due to missing information.

**Recovery:** Learn from rejection. Improve future proposals. Return to evaluation if owner requests iteration.

#### Stage 14: MODIFIED

**Purpose:** Decision records the owner's modifications to the proposal.

**Entry Criteria:** Owner modifies proposal parameters.

**Activities:** Record owner modifications. Re-validate proposal with modifications. Update rankings and evaluations. Present modified proposal for confirmation.

**Exit Criteria:** Modifications recorded. Modified proposal confirmed. Updated evaluation complete.

**Failure Modes:** Modifications create infeasible proposal. Modifications invalidate evaluations.

**Recovery:** Flag infeasibility to owner. Offer re-evaluation with modified parameters. Return to relevant evaluation stage.

#### Stage 15: ARCHIVED

**Purpose:** Decision archives the complete decision record for audit, learning, and future reference.

**Entry Criteria:** Decision reached final state (accepted, rejected). No further action required.

**Activities:** Finalize audit trail. Store decision envelope permanently. Make available for Learning Engine. Make available for Reflection Engine.

**Exit Criteria:** Decision envelope stored. Audit trail complete. Learning data available.

**Failure Modes:** Audit trail incomplete. Learning data not captured.

**Recovery:** Complete audit trail before archiving. Flag incomplete records for manual review.

### 4.5 Lifecycle Principles

**DCL-001 — No Stage Skipping.** Every decision must pass through every stage in order.

**DCL-002 — Rollback Is Allowed.** Decision may return to previous stages when conditions change or gaps are found.

**DCL-003 — Retirement Is Always Available.** Decision may be retired from any stage.

**DCL-004 — Owner Entry at Human Review.** The owner enters the lifecycle at WAITING_HUMAN_REVIEW. The owner may not enter earlier stages.

**DCL-005 — Owner May Modify.** The owner may modify parameters at any stage after WAITING_HUMAN_REVIEW.

**DCL-006 — Expiration Is Real.** Proposals expire. Expired proposals must return to evaluation or be retired.

**DCL-007 — Context Change Invalidates.** If context changes significantly, the decision must return to CANDIDATE.

## PART 5 — DECISION DIMENSIONS

### 5.1 Dimension Philosophy

The Decision Engine evaluates every alternative across a fixed set of dimensions. No dimension is optional. Each dimension receives a score and a confidence. The weight of each dimension may be configured by the owner.

### 5.2 The Fifteen Dimensions

| # | Dimension | Definition |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| DIM-001 | Financial | Monetary cost, revenue, profit, ROI of each alternative |
| DIM-002 | Temporal | Time to implement, duration of effects, deadlines, urgency |
| DIM-003 | Risk | Probability and impact of adverse outcomes |
| DIM-004 | Opportunity | Potential upside, strategic advantage, future value |
| DIM-005 | Quality | Effect on product, service, and operational quality |
| DIM-006 | Human | Impact on employees, customers, stakeholders, community |
| DIM-007 | Ethical | Alignment with ethical principles, values, and moral obligations |
| DIM-008 | Strategic | Alignment with long-term business strategy and vision |
| DIM-009 | Operational | Effect on operational efficiency, capacity, and workflows |
| DIM-010 | Legal | Legal compliance, regulatory impact, liability exposure |
| DIM-011 | Reputational | Effect on brand, public perception, and trust |
| DIM-012 | Competitive | Effect on competitive position and market dynamics |
| DIM-013 | Reversibility | Ease, cost, and time to reverse the decision |
| DIM-014 | Environmental | Effect on natural environment, sustainability, and resources |
| DIM-015 | Cultural | Effect on company culture, morale, and values alignment |

### 5.3 Dimension Principles

**DDP-001 — All Dimensions Required.** Every dimension must be scored for every alternative before a proposal may be built.

**DDP-002 — Dimension Weights Are Configurable.** The owner may adjust the weight of each dimension to reflect business priorities.

**DDP-003 — Dimensions Are Independent.** Each dimension is evaluated independently. Scores are not derived from other dimensions.

**DDP-004 — Dimension Scores Are Normalized.** Scores are normalized to a 0–100 scale for cross-dimension comparison.

**DDP-005 — Dimension Confidence Is Separate.** Each dimension has a separate confidence score representing the quality of evidence used.

**DDP-006 — Dimension Gaps Must Be Flagged.** If a dimension cannot be scored due to insufficient evidence, it must be flagged as a gap, not assumed neutral.

**DDP-007 — Weight Transparency.** The weight of each dimension must be exposed in every proposal.

**DDP-008 — Weight Sensitivity.** The proposal must expose how sensitive the ranking is to weight changes.

**DDP-009 — Custom Dimensions Are Not Allowed.** Only the fifteen constitutional dimensions may be used. Custom dimensions violate comparability and audit consistency.

**DDP-010 — Dimension Definitions Are Stable.** The definition of each dimension may not change between evaluations within the same decision context.

### 6.3 Envelope Principles

**DEP-001 — Envelope Is Immutable After Proposal.** Once a proposal is built, the envelope is immutable until the owner modifies it.

**DEP-002 — Envelope Must Be Complete.** A proposal may not be presented to the owner with missing envelope sections.

**DEP-003 — Envelope Must Be Human-Readable.** Every envelope section must be presented in language the owner can understand.

**DEP-004 — Envelope Contains All Context.** The envelope must contain all context needed for the owner to make a decision without external research.

**DEP-005 — Envelope Is Auditable.** Every envelope must contain a complete audit trail.

**DEP-006 — Envelope Supports Versioning.** When an owner modifies a proposal, the envelope version increments and the previous version is preserved.

**DEP-007 — Envelope Supports Export.** The envelope must be exportable for external audit, learning, and reflection.

**DEP-008 — Envelope Is Not a Data Structure.** The envelope is a constitutional concept. Any implementation must satisfy the constitutional requirements, not the data structure.

## PART 7 — REVERSIBILITY

### 7.1 Reversibility Philosophy

Reversibility is a first-class evaluation dimension because the ability to undo a decision is as important as the decision itself. Every decision has a cost of reversal. Some decisions cannot be reversed at all. These must be treated differently.

### 7.2 Reversibility Classification

| Class | Definition | Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Reversible** | Decision can be undone at acceptable cost and time | Hiring a contractor, ordering inventory, scheduling a meeting |
| **Conditionally Reversible** | Decision can be undone but at significant cost, time, or reputation cost | Launching a new menu item, changing supplier, modifying a process |
| **Irreversible** | Decision cannot be meaningfully undone | Firing a key employee, signing a long-term lease, rebranding, closing a location |

### 7.3 Reversibility Principles

**DVR-001 — Reversibility Must Be Classified.** Every alternative must be classified as reversible, conditionally reversible, or irreversible.

**DVR-002 — Irreversible Requires Higher Confidence.** Irreversible alternatives require a higher minimum confidence threshold than reversible ones.

**DVR-003 — Irreversible Requires Stronger Evidence.** Irreversible alternatives require stronger evidence than reversible ones.

**DVR-004 — Irreversible Requires Human Review.** Irreversible alternatives must be explicitly flagged in the proposal and require affirmative owner acknowledgement.

**DVR-005 — Reversal Cost Must Be Estimated.** The full cost of reversal (financial, temporal, reputational, human) must be estimated for every alternative.

**DVR-006 — Reversal Time Must Be Estimated.** The time required to reverse each alternative must be estimated.

**DVR-007 — Point of No Return Must Be Identified.** For conditionally reversible alternatives, the point after which reversal becomes infeasible must be identified.

**DVR-008 — Reversibility Affects Ranking.** Reversibility must affect the ranking — reversible alternatives are preferred when other dimensions are comparable.

**DVR-009 — Reversibility Is Not Risk.** Reversibility is distinct from risk. A high-risk decision may be reversible. A low-risk decision may be irreversible.

**DVR-010 — Reversibility Must Be Re-Evaluated.** Reversibility classification must be re-evaluated when context changes.

## PART 8 — HUMAN IMPACT

### 8.1 Human Impact Philosophy

Human impact is the most important dimension. It is evaluated before financial impact. It is never reduced to a single metric. Every decision affects humans — the owner, employees, customers, suppliers, and the community.

### 8.2 Human Impact Sub-Dimensions

| # | Sub-Dimension | Definition |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| HIM-001 | Owner Impact | Effect on the owner's time, energy, stress, and well-being |
| HIM-002 | Employee Impact | Effect on team morale, workload, autonomy, and dignity |
| HIM-003 | Customer Impact | Effect on customer experience, trust, and satisfaction |
| HIM-004 | Supplier Impact | Effect on supplier relationships and fairness |
| HIM-005 | Community Impact | Effect on the local community and broader society |
| HIM-006 | Dignity Impact | Whether the decision respects human dignity |
| HIM-007 | Trust Impact | Whether the decision builds or erodes trust |
| HIM-008 | Fairness Impact | Whether the decision distributes benefits and burdens fairly |
| HIM-009 | Autonomy Impact | Whether the decision respects stakeholder autonomy |
| HIM-010 | Psychological Impact | Effect on stress, anxiety, morale, and psychological safety |

### 8.3 Human Impact Principles

**DHI-001 — Human Before Financial.** Human impact is evaluated before financial impact. A financially optimal decision is not acceptable if it causes disproportionate human harm.

**DHI-002 — Human Impact Is Not a Number.** Human impact may not be reduced to a single metric or score. It must be described qualitatively.

**DHI-003 — Stakeholder Identification.** Every affected stakeholder group must be identified. Missing a stakeholder group is a failure.

**DHI-004 — Vulnerable Stakeholders.** Vulnerable stakeholders (those with least power in the decision) must be explicitly identified and protected.

**DHI-005 — Human Harm Must Be Flagged.** Any alternative that causes harm to humans must be flagged, regardless of financial benefit.

**DHI-006 — Human Impact Is Non-Delegable.** The owner is responsible for evaluating human impact. The Decision Engine may not make human impact tradeoffs without owner approval.

**DHI-007 — Human Impact Is Reversible.** If human impact is negative and the decision is reversible, the owner should consider reversing it.

**DHI-008 — Human Impact Is Persistent.** Human impact persists beyond the decision's direct effects. Trust, morale, and dignity effects outlast financial effects.

## PART 9 — ETHICAL DECISION MODEL

### 9.1 Ethical Philosophy

The Decision Engine does not impose a specific ethical framework. Instead, it requires explicit selection and exposure of the ethical framework used, and it enforces transparency about ethical tradeoffs.

### 9.2 Supported Ethical Frameworks

| # | Framework | Principle |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| ETH-001 | Consequentialism | Evaluate based on outcomes and consequences |
| ETH-002 | Deontology | Evaluate based on duties, rules, and obligations |
| ETH-003 | Virtue Ethics | Evaluate based on character and virtues |
| ETH-004 | Care Ethics | Evaluate based on relationships and care for others |
| ETH-005 | Justice Ethics | Evaluate based on fairness, equity, and distribution |
| ETH-006 | Pragmatic Ethics | Evaluate based on practical outcomes and context |

### 9.3 Ethical Evaluation Principles

**DEE-001 — Framework Must Be Explicit.** Every evaluation must state which ethical framework is being used.

**DEE-002 — Framework Must Be Configurable.** The owner may configure which ethical framework the Decision Engine should prioritize.

**DEE-003 — Multiple Frameworks May Be Used.** The Decision Engine may evaluate against multiple ethical frameworks and compare results.

**DEE-004 — Framework Conflicts Must Be Exposed.** When different frameworks produce different rankings, the conflict must be exposed.

**DEE-005 — No Framework Is Absolute.** No single ethical framework is the default. The owner may choose or the Decision Engine may present multiple views.

**DEE-006 — Ethical Tradeoffs Must Be Transparent.** Every ethical tradeoff must be clearly stated and exposed to the owner.

**DEE-007 — Ethical Violations Must Be Flagged.** Any alternative that violates constitutional values must be flagged regardless of framework.

**DEE-008 — Ethical Cost of Inaction.** The ethical cost of not deciding must be evaluated.

**DEE-009 — Ethical Cost of Delay.** The ethical cost of delaying the decision must be evaluated.

**DEE-010 — Ethical Evaluation Has Limits.** The Decision Engine may not make ethical decisions. It may only analyze, expose, and present ethical dimensions for the owner to decide.

**DFM-005 — Failure Mode Catalog Evolves.** The failure mode catalog may be extended based on experience and learning.

## PART 11 — RECOVERY MODEL

### 11.1 Recovery Philosophy

When a decision fails — whether through detection of a failure mode, owner rejection, context change, or expiration — the Decision Engine must have a defined recovery path. Recovery is not failure. Recovery is the system's ability to respond to conditions that prevent a clean decision lifecycle.

### 11.2 Recovery Strategies

| Strategy | Description | When Used |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Return to Stage | Return to a specific lifecycle stage and continue | Failure mode detected before proposal |
| Return to CANDIDATE | Restart the entire lifecycle | Context changed significantly |
| Retry | Retry the current operation | Transient failure |
| Escalate | Escalate to owner or human operator | Unresolvable failure |
| Compensate | Proceed with compensation for the failure | Acceptable risk |
| Abort | Abort the decision process entirely | Fatal failure |
| Record and Continue | Record the failure and continue | Non-blocking failure |
| Rollback | Undo actions already taken | Execution failure |
| Patch | Apply a fix and continue | Minor failure |

### 11.3 Recovery Principles

**DRP-001 — Recovery Is Mapped.** Every failure mode must map to a primary recovery strategy.

**DRP-002 — Recovery Is Not Failure.** Recovery is a designed response, not a failure of the system.

**DRP-003 — Recovery Must Be Recorded.** Every recovery action must be recorded in the audit trail.

**DRP-004 — Recovery Counts Are Tracked.** Repeated recoveries from the same failure mode indicate a systemic issue.

**DRP-005 — Recovery Escalation Path.** If primary recovery fails, escalation path must exist.

## PART 12 — RELATIONSHIPS

### 12.1 Relationship Philosophy

The Decision Engine does not exist in isolation. It consumes from Reasoning and Context, produces for Planning and Execution, and feeds Learning and Reflection.

### 12.2 Relationship Map

| Engine | Relationship | Direction | Contract |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Reasoning | Input | → Decision | Provides alternatives, evidence, analysis |
| Context | Input | → Decision | Provides business context, owner preferences, constraints |
| Attention | Input | → Decision | Provides relevant observations |
| Memory | Input | → Decision | Provides relevant history and patterns |
| Planning | Output | Decision → | Consumes decisions to build plans |
| Execution | Output | Decision → | Consumes decisions to execute actions |
| Learning | Output | Decision → | Consumes decisions for learning |
| Reflection | Output | Decision → | Consumes decisions for reflection |
| Owner | Input/Output | ⇄ Decision | Owner configures, reviews, and makes ultimate decisions |

### 12.3 Relationship Principles

**DRL-001 — Decision Does Not Reason.** Decision consumes reasoning conclusions but does not produce reasoning.

**DRL-002 — Decision Does Not Plan.** Decision produces proposals, not plans. Planning consumes proposals.

**DRL-003 — Decision Does Not Execute.** Decision produces proposals. Execution consumes proposals.

**DRL-004 — Decision Does Not Learn.** Decision records data for Learning. Learning is a separate function.

**DRL-005 — Decision Is Not the Owner.** Decision proposes. The owner decides.

**DRL-006 — Decision Is Immutable to Downstream.** Once a proposal is consumed by Planning, the proposal record is immutable.

**DRL-007 — Decision Depends on Reasoning Quality.** The quality of a decision proposal depends on the quality of reasoning input.

**DRL-008 — Decision Accepts Owner Override.** The owner may override any decision proposal at any time.

**DRL-009 — Decision Notifies Downstream.** When a decision is finalized, all downstream consumers are notified.

**DRL-010 — Decision Records for Learning.** Every decision envelope is preserved for learning, even rejected proposals.

| CONTEXT_READY → ALTERNATIVES_BUILT | All required context validated. No critical gaps. |
| ALTERNATIVES_BUILT → RISK_EVALUATED | Minimum 2 alternatives. All alternative metadata complete. |
| RISK_EVALUATED → OPPORTUNITY_EVALUATED | Risk evaluated for all alternatives. |
| OPPORTUNITY_EVALUATED → COST_EVALUATED | Opportunity evaluated for all alternatives. |
| COST_EVALUATED → HUMAN_IMPACT_EVALUATED | All cost dimensions evaluated per alternative. |
| HUMAN_IMPACT_EVALUATED → REVERSIBILITY_EVALUATED | All stakeholders identified. Human impact evaluated for all alternatives. |
| REVERSIBILITY_EVALUATED → CONFIDENCE_VERIFIED | Reversibility classified for all alternatives. |
| CONFIDENCE_VERIFIED → PROPOSAL_BUILT | Confidence above threshold. |
| PROPOSAL_BUILT → WAITING_HUMAN_REVIEW | DecisionEnvelope complete. All sections populated. |
| WAITING_HUMAN_REVIEW → ACCEPTED | Owner accepts. |
| WAITING_HUMAN_REVIEW → REJECTED | Owner rejects. |
| WAITING_HUMAN_REVIEW → MODIFIED | Owner modifies parameters. |
| ACCEPTED → ARCHIVED | Decision forwarded to consumers. |
| REJECTED → ARCHIVED | Decision recorded. |
| MODIFIED → WAITING_HUMAN_REVIEW | Modified proposal re-validated. |
| ANY → RETIRED | Always valid. |

```
DecisionEnvelope {
  // Identifiers
  proposal_id: string        // Unique identifier
  version: integer           // Increments on modification

  // Lifecycle
  lifecycle_stage: enum      // Current stage
  lifecycle_history: [       // Stage transition records
    {
      from: enum
      to: enum
      timestamp: datetime
      reason: string
    }
  ]

  // Ownership
  owner: string              // Decision owner identifier

  // Source
  source: {
    reasoning_conclusion_id: string
    reasoning_version: string
    request: string          // Original decision request
  }

  // Summary
  summary: {
    title: string            // Short decision title
    description: string      // Decision context
    urgency: enum            // LOW, MEDIUM, HIGH, CRITICAL
    deadline: datetime       // Decision deadline
  }

  // Context
  context: {
    business_context: string
    temporal_context: {
      timestamp: datetime
      effective_date: datetime
      expiration_date: datetime
    }
    environmental_factors: [string]
    constraints: [string]
  }

  // Alternatives
  alternatives: [
    {
      id: string
      label: string
      description: string
      source: string         // Which reasoning alternative generated this
      excluded: boolean      // Whether this alternative was excluded
      exclusion_reason: string // Why excluded (if excluded)
    }
  ]

  // Dimensions
  dimensions: {
    weights: {
      financial: number          // 0-100
      temporal: number
      risk: number
      opportunity: number
      quality: number
      human: number
      ethical: number
      strategic: number
      operational: number
      legal: number
      reputational: number
      competitive: number
      reversibility: number
      environmental: number
      cultural: number
    }
  }

  // Evaluation
  evaluation: {
    scoring_method: string       // How scores were calculated
    scores: [                    // Per alternative per dimension
      {
        alternative_id: string
        dimension: string
        score: number            // 0-100
        confidence: number       // 0-100
        evidence_references: [string]
        notes: string
      }
    ]
    rankings: [                  // Ranked alternatives
      {
        alternative_id: string
        rank: integer
        weighted_score: number
        recommendation: boolean  // Is this the recommended alternative?
      }
    ]
  }

  // Confidence
  confidence: {
    overall: number              // 0-100
    minimum_threshold: number    // Configurable minimum
    below_threshold: boolean
    confidence_by_dimension: {
      [dimension: string]: number
    }
    confidence_issues: [string]
    calibration_adjustments: [string]
  }

  // Gaps
  gaps: [
    {
      dimension: string
      severity: enum             // CRITICAL, MODERATE, MINOR
      description: string
      impact_on_confidence: number
    }
  ]

  // Assumptions
  assumptions: [
    {
      assumption: string
      impact_if_wrong: string
      confidence: number
    }
  ]

  // Risk Assessment
  risks: [
    {
      alternative_id: string
      risk_factor: string
      probability: number        // 0-1
      impact: number             // 0-100
      impact_description: string
      worst_case_outcome: string
      mitigation: string
    }
  ]

  // Reversibility
  reversibility: [
    {
      alternative_id: string
      classification: enum       // REVERSIBLE, CONDITIONALLY_REVERSIBLE, IRREVERSIBLE
      reversal_cost: string
      reversal_time: string
      point_of_no_return: string
      consequences_if_not_reversed: string
    }
  ]

  // Human Impact
  human_impact: [
    {
      alternative_id: string
      stakeholder: string
      impact: string             // Qualitative description
      severity: enum             // POSITIVE, NEUTRAL, NEGATIVE, CRITICAL
      dignity_assessment: string
      trust_assessment: string
      fairness_assessment: string
    }
  ]

  // Recommendation
  recommendation: {
    recommended_id: string
    rationale: string
    ranking_summary: string
    sensitivity_notes: string
    ethical_framework_used: string
    ethical_tradeoffs: [string]
  }

  // Owner Decision
  owner_decision: {              // Populated after owner decides
    decision: enum               // ACCEPTED, REJECTED, MODIFIED
    timestamp: datetime
    notes: string
    modifications: string        // Description of modifications
  } (optional)

  // Ethical Evaluation
  ethical_evaluation: {
    frameworks_used: [string]
    results_by_framework: [
      {
        framework: string
        ranking: [              // Alternative rankings under this framework
          {
            alternative_id: string
            rank: integer
            score: number
            ethical_notes: string
          }
        ]
        conflicts: [string]
      }
    ]
    primary_framework: string
    framework_conflicts: [string]
  }

  // Quality
  quality: {
    completeness: number         // 0-100
    clarity: number
    evidence_quality: number
    alternative_coverage: number
    stakeholder_coverage: number
    overall: number
    threshold: number
    meets_threshold: boolean
    quality_issues: [string]
  }

  // Audit
  audit_trail: [
    {
      timestamp: datetime
      event: string
      actor: string              // Decision, Owner, Context, Reasoning, etc.
      details: string
      lifecycle_stage: enum
    }
  ]

  // Expiration
  expiration: {
    built_at: datetime
    expires_at: datetime
    expired: boolean
    context_invalidation: {      // Context changes since built
      changed: boolean
      changes: [string]
      severity: enum
    }
  }

  // Failure Modes
  failure_modes: [
    {
      failure_id: string         // FM-XXX
      detected_at_stage: enum
      severity: enum
      detection_method: string
      recovery_strategy: string
      resolution: string
    }
  ]

  // Learning
  learning_metadata: {
    consumed_by_planning: boolean
    consumed_by_execution: boolean
    consumed_by_learning: boolean
    consumed_by_reflection: boolean
    archived_at: datetime
  }
}
```

### F.2 Decision Contract with Each Engine

**With Reasoning:**
- Decision receives: alternatives, analysis, evidence references, conclusions
- Decision sends: feedback (for gaps), return requests
- Contract: Decision does not reason. Reasoning does not decide.

**With Context:**
- Decision receives: business context, temporal context, constraints, environmental factors
- Decision sends: context gap requests
- Contract: Context provides. Decision validates sufficiency.

**With Planning:**
- Decision sends: proposals (accepted or modified)
- Decision receives: feedback (planning feasibility)
- Contract: Decision proposes. Planning plans. Decision does not plan.

**With Execution:**
- Decision sends: proposals (finalized, accepted)
- Decision receives: execution feedback
- Contract: Decision proposes. Execution executes. Decision does not execute.

**With Learning:**
- Decision sends: decision envelopes (all states)
- Decision receives: learned patterns (affecting future evaluations)
- Contract: Decision provides data. Learning extracts knowledge.

**With Reflection:**
- Decision sends: decision envelopes (for post-hoc analysis)
- Decision receives: reflection insights (improving future evaluations)
- Contract: Decision provides records. Reflection improves.

**With Attention:**
- Decision receives: relevant observations (context changes, new information)
- Contract: Attention watches. Decision reacts to context changes.

**With Memory:**
- Decision receives: historical decision patterns, past outcomes, recurring patterns
- Contract: Memory remembers. Decision uses history.

**With Owner:**
- Decision sends: proposals, notifications, escalation requests
- Decision receives: decisions (accept/reject/modify), preference updates, configuration
- Contract: Owner decides. Decision serves. Decision does not substitute.
