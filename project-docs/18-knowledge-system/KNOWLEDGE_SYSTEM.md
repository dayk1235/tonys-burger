## CONSTITUTIONAL STATEMENT

Knowledge is the system's validated understanding — the cognitive artifact that has earned the right to influence reasoning.

Knowledge is not data. Data is unprocessed. Knowledge is validated. Knowledge is not information. Information is structured data. Knowledge is applied understanding. Knowledge is not belief. Belief is unvalidated. Knowledge is earned.

Knowledge implements Constitution Article 5 (Knowledge Is Earned), Article 28 (Knowledge Ages), and Article 13 (Collective Knowledge — private data never leaves the customer, anonymous knowledge improves everyone).

Without Knowledge, every reasoning cycle starts from opinion. Every Recommendation is a guess. Every Decision is uninformed. Knowledge is what makes the system trustworthy.

## Part 1 — Philosophy of Knowledge

### 1.1 Why Knowledge Exists

Knowledge exists because the system must distinguish between what it has validated and what it has merely encountered.

An Observation is a fact. A Pattern is a recurring structure. Evidence is a justification. Memory preserves all of these. But none of them alone constitutes **understanding**.

Knowledge is the output of the Learning stage of the Intelligence Loop — Observation that has been processed, understood, validated, and committed to permanent memory. Knowledge is what makes each subsequent cognitive cycle smarter than the last.

Without Knowledge, every intelligence cycle starts from zero. The system would have experience but no understanding. It would remember everything but know nothing.

Knowledge exists to implement Constitution Article 3 (Never Pretends to Know) — the system must clearly delineate between what is known, what is projected, and what is unknown.

### 1.2 Knowledge vs. Observation

| Dimension | Observation | Knowledge |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Definition** | A verified fact captured at a point in time | Validated understanding earned over time |
| **Validation** | Verified at capture (authenticity, provenance) | Validated through evidence accumulation and contradiction resolution |
| **Immutability** | Immutable (once recorded, never changed) | Evolvable (confidence changes, may be deprecated) |
| **Temporality** | Instantaneous | Cumulative |
| **Example** | "Revenue on Tuesday was $4,287" | "Tuesdays are consistently 18% slower than Fridays" |

An Observation is what happened. Knowledge is what it means. Observations are the raw material; Knowledge is the refined output. Without Observations, Knowledge has no foundation. Without Knowledge, Observations have no interpretation.

### 1.3 Knowledge vs. Pattern

| Dimension | Knowledge | Pattern |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Definition** | Validated understanding | A recurring structure detected in Observations |
| **Validation** | Fully validated (Learning Layer gates) | May be tentative (low confidence, unvalidated) |
| **Permanence** | Long-term (until deprecated) | Variable (retained while valid) |
| **Role** | Informs reasoning | Describes what repeats |
| **Dependency** | May incorporate multiple Patterns | Knowledge may be derived from Patterns |

A Pattern becomes Knowledge when it passes through the Learning Layer's validation gates. Not all Patterns become Knowledge. A Pattern that has been detected but not validated remains in Pattern Memory. A Pattern that has been validated, cross-referenced against Evidence, and confirmed by outcomes becomes Knowledge in Knowledge Memory.

### 1.4 Knowledge vs. Evidence

| Dimension | Knowledge | Evidence |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Definition** | Validated understanding | Justification connecting an artifact to a claim |
| **Role** | What the system knows | Why the system knows it |
| **Temporality** | Long-term | May be temporary (if supporting artifact changes) |
| **Relationship** | Knowledge is supported by Evidence | Evidence supports Knowledge |
| **Confidence** | Knowledge has confidence derived from Evidence | Evidence has weight that determines Knowledge confidence |

Evidence is the path. Knowledge is the destination. Every piece of Knowledge must trace to Evidence. Knowledge without Evidence is dogma.

### 1.5 Knowledge vs. Memory

| Dimension | Knowledge | Memory |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Definition** | Validated understanding | Persistent cognition |
| **Scope** | A subset of Memory (validated content) | All preserved experience (validated and unvalidated) |
| **Validation** | Must pass Learning Layer validation gates | No validation requirement |
| **Home** | Knowledge Memory (Long-Term Memory) | All Memory systems (Working, Short-Term, Long-Term, Historical, etc.) |

All Knowledge is in Memory, but not all Memory is Knowledge. A tentative hypothesis in Short-Term Memory is Memory but not Knowledge. Knowledge is Memory that has earned the right to be trusted. This distinction implements Constitution Article 5 (Knowledge Is Earned).

### 1.6 Knowledge vs. Learning

| Dimension | Knowledge | Learning |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Definition** | Validated understanding | The process of acquiring and validating new understanding |
| **Role** | The output | The process |
| **Temporality** | Static (at any given moment) | Continuous |
| **Relationship** | Learning produces Knowledge | Knowledge enables further Learning |

Learning is the engine that produces Knowledge. Knowledge is the fuel that powers reasoning. Without Learning, Knowledge stagnates. Without Knowledge, Learning has no purpose.

### 1.7 Knowledge vs. Information

| Dimension | Knowledge | Information |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Definition** | Validated understanding | Structured data with context |
| **Validation** | Fully validated | May be unvalidated |
| **Actionability** | Directly informs reasoning and decisions | Requires processing before it can inform |
| **Permanence** | Long-term | Transitory |
| **Example** | "Dessert demand is elastic above $14" | "Dessert sales: 42 units, Tuesday, 8 PM" |

Information is structured data. Knowledge is applied understanding. The system transforms information into Knowledge through the Intelligence Loop. Information is transitory. Knowledge is permanent.

### 1.8 Knowledge vs. Belief

| Dimension | Knowledge | Belief |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Definition** | Validated understanding | An accepted proposition without full validation |
| **Certainty** | Supported by Evidence | May be held without Evidence |
| **Update mechanism** | Updated through Evidence and Learning | May persist despite contradictory Evidence |
| **Role** | Foundation for reasoning | Provisional understanding awaiting validation |

The system does not hold beliefs. It holds Knowledge (validated) and hypotheses (under evaluation). The system never acts on a belief as if it were Knowledge. This implements Constitution Article 3 (Never Pretends to Know).

### 1.9 Knowledge vs. Truth

| Dimension | Knowledge | Truth |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Definition** | Validated understanding | A statement that corresponds to reality |
| **Attainability** | Achievable (within confidence bounds) | Rarely fully attainable in business contexts |
| **Temporality** | Evolves with Evidence | Absolute (if truly true) |
| **Relationship** | Knowledge approximates Truth | Truth is the ideal that Knowledge approaches |

Knowledge approximates truth. The system never claims to possess absolute truth (Constitution Article 3). It says "we know this with high confidence" not "this is proven." Knowledge is always qualified by confidence.

### 1.10 Knowledge vs. Confidence

| Dimension | Knowledge | Confidence |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Definition** | Validated understanding | The system's expressed certainty |
| **Role** | What the system knows | How sure the system is |
| **Relationship** | Knowledge has confidence | Confidence is a property of Knowledge |
| **Dependency** | Knowledge exists independently of confidence | Confidence quantifies Knowledge reliability |

Confidence is a property of Knowledge, not the Knowledge itself. Two pieces of Knowledge may have different confidence levels. The same piece of Knowledge may have different confidence at different times. But the Knowledge remains Knowledge as long as it remains validated.

### 1.11 Knowledge vs. Experience

| Dimension | Knowledge | Experience |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Definition** | Validated understanding | The accumulation of Observations and outcomes over time |
| **Role** | What the system knows | What the system has been through |
| **Relationship** | Knowledge is extracted from Experience | Experience is the raw material for Knowledge |
| **Permanence** | Knowledge is permanent (until deprecated) | Experience is preserved in Historical Memory |

Experience is everything the system has encountered. Knowledge is what the system has learned from that experience. Experience without Knowledge extraction is wasted. Knowledge without supporting Experience is ungrounded.

### 1.12 Knowledge vs. Intelligence

| Dimension | Knowledge | Intelligence |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| **Definition** | Validated understanding | The capacity to apply Knowledge to novel situations |
| **Role** | What the system knows | What the system can do with what it knows |
| **Relationship** | Knowledge is the input to Intelligence | Intelligence is the application of Knowledge |
| **Dependency** | Intelligence requires Knowledge | Knowledge without Intelligence is inert |

Knowledge is the what. Intelligence is the how. Knowledge provides the foundation; Intelligence provides the reasoning. The system must have both: Knowledge to know, Intelligence to act.

### 1.13 Why Knowledge Must Be Earned

Knowledge is never invented, never assumed, never guessed.

The system earns Knowledge through a rigorous chain:
1. **Observation.** A fact is captured and verified.
2. **Pattern detection.** A recurring structure is identified across Observations.
3. **Evidence collection.** Justifications are gathered supporting the Pattern.
4. **Validation.** The Pattern passes the Learning Layer's validation gates.
5. **Confirmation.** Outcomes confirm the Pattern's predictive value.
6. **Promotion.** The Pattern is promoted to Knowledge Memory.

Each step is mandatory. No step can be skipped. Knowledge that has not passed through this chain is not Knowledge — it is speculation.

This implements Constitution Article 5 (Knowledge Is Earned). The system builds its knowledge one verified fact at a time.

### 1.14 Why Knowledge Ages

Knowledge ages because the business environment changes.

A restaurant in 2026 does not operate like it did in 2023. Customer preferences evolve. Competitors change. Supply chains shift. Regulations update. Knowledge that was valid last year may be misleading today.

Knowledge aging is governed by:
1. **Confidence decay.** Unreinforced Knowledge loses confidence over time.
2. **Outcome divergence.** When Knowledge-based predictions consistently fail, the Knowledge is flagged for review.
3. **Context change.** When the conditions under which Knowledge was validated change, the Knowledge is re-evaluated.
4. **Active deprecation.** Knowledge that is no longer valid is explicitly deprecated with a record of why.

This implements Constitution Article 28 (Knowledge Ages). The system actively deprecates knowledge that is no longer relevant to the current business environment.

### 1.15 Why Knowledge Is Never Absolute

Knowledge is always qualified by confidence. The system never says "this is true" — it says "we know this with high confidence" or "this is our current understanding."

Knowledge is never absolute because:
1. **Evidence is always incomplete.** There is always more that could be observed.
2. **Context changes.** What is true today may not be true tomorrow.
3. **Contradictions are possible.** New Evidence may challenge established Knowledge.
4. **Certainty is a spectrum.** Complete certainty is unattainable in business.

This implements Constitution Article 3 (Never Pretends to Know). The system clearly delineates between what is known, what is projected, and what is unknown.

## Part 2 — Knowledge Lifecycle

### 2.1 Lifecycle Model

Knowledge begins as a candidate understanding and evolves through stages of validation, promotion, contradiction, deprecation, and archival.

```

                    │  CANDIDATE            │
                    │  KNOWLEDGE            │
                    └──────────┬───────────┘

                    │  HYPOTHESIS           │
                    │  KNOWLEDGE            │

                               ▼
                    ┌──────────────────────┐
                    │  TENTATIVE            │
```

### 2.2 Stage Definitions

#### Stage 1 — CANDIDATE KNOWLEDGE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | A Pattern or understanding has been identified as potentially valid and worth evaluating for Knowledge promotion. |
| **Entry** | Pattern Memory identifies a Pattern with sufficient strength to warrant Knowledge evaluation. Or Learning identifies a candidate understanding from outcome analysis. |
| **Exit** | Sufficient potential → promoted to Hypothesis. Insufficient potential → returned to Pattern Memory. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Hypothesis (evaluation initiated); → Pattern Memory (returned for more evidence) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Validated (cannot skip Hypothesis); → Knowledge Memory |
| **Failure Modes** | Candidate blindness (valid Pattern never identified as Knowledge candidate); false candidate (weak Pattern flagged for Knowledge evaluation); candidate flooding (too many candidates, evaluation capacity overwhelmed) |

Candidate Knowledge is not Knowledge. It is a Pattern or understanding that might be worth promoting. It exists at the boundary between Pattern Memory and Knowledge Memory.

#### Stage 2 — HYPOTHESIS KNOWLEDGE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The candidate has been formalized as a hypothesis. The system states what it believes may be true and begins gathering supporting Evidence. |
| **Entry** | The candidate is formalized with a clear statement, scope, and expected supporting Evidence. |
| **Exit** | Sufficient Evidence gathered → Tentative. Evidence shows the hypothesis is invalid → returned to Pattern Memory or discarded. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Tentative (Evidence gathering complete); → Candidate (returned for reformulation); Discarded (hypothesis invalid, recorded with reason) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Validated (cannot bypass Tentative and Under Validation) |
| **Failure Modes** | Hypothesis confirmation bias (only confirming Evidence gathered); hypothesis blindness (contradictory Evidence ignored); hypothesis stagnation (hypothesis maintained despite insufficient Evidence) |

Hypothesis Knowledge is an explicit proposition being tested. The system states "we think this may be true" and actively seeks Evidence.

#### Stage 3 — TENTATIVE KNOWLEDGE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | Some Evidence supports the hypothesis but not enough for confident validation. The Knowledge is provisionally held. |
| **Entry** | Minimum Evidence threshold met. The hypothesis has supporting Observations and Evidence. |
| **Exit** | Active validation initiated → Under Validation. Evidence proves insufficient → returned to Hypothesis. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Under Validation (formal validation process initiated); → Hypothesis (returned for more Evidence) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Validated (cannot bypass Under Validation); → Knowledge Memory |
| **Failure Modes** | Premature tentativity (hypothesis promoted to Tentative with insufficient Evidence); tentative stagnation (Knowledge remains Tentative indefinitely, never validated or refuted) |

Tentative Knowledge is the system's best current understanding that has not yet been formally validated. It is better than a guess but not yet Knowledge.

#### Stage 4 — UNDER VALIDATION

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Knowledge is actively being validated. The Learning Layer evaluates Evidence, checks for contradictions, assesses quality, and determines whether promotion is warranted. |
| **Entry** | Formal validation process initiated by the Learning Layer. |
| **Exit** | Validation passes → Validated. Validation fails → returned to Tentative or Hypothesis. Contradiction found → Contradicted. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Validated (all validation gates passed); → Tentative (more Evidence needed); → Contradicted (contradictory Evidence found during validation) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Knowledge Memory (validation not complete); → Retired |
| **Failure Modes** | Validation bias (systematic favoritism toward confirming validation); validation fatigue (critical validation steps skipped due to resource constraints); incomplete validation (some dimensions not evaluated) |

Under Validation is the most critical stage in the Knowledge lifecycle. It is where the system determines whether an understanding has earned the right to become Knowledge.

#### Stage 5 — VALIDATED KNOWLEDGE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Knowledge has passed all validation gates. It is promoted to Knowledge Memory and becomes part of the system's trusted understanding. |
| **Entry** | Learning Layer confirms: sufficient Evidence, no unresolved contradictions, quality thresholds met, confidence minimum satisfied. |
| **Exit** | Contradicted by new Evidence → Contradicted. Confidence decays below threshold → Under Validation. Deprecated by Learning Layer → Deprecated. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Contradicted (new Evidence challenges validity); → Under Validation (confidence decay triggers re-validation); → Deprecated (Learning Layer determines Knowledge no longer valid) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Candidate (cannot revert to pre-validation state without going through Contradicted or Deprecated) |
| **Failure Modes** | False validation (Knowledge promoted that should not have been); validation rigidity (Knowledge not re-validated despite changing conditions); confirmation lock (Knowledge immune to contradiction due to high confidence) |

Validated Knowledge is the system's trusted understanding. It is promoted to Knowledge Memory and actively informs reasoning, recommendations, narratives, and decisions.

#### Stage 6 — CONTRADICTED KNOWLEDGE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | New Evidence contradicts the Knowledge. Both the Knowledge and the contradiction are preserved while resolution is determined. |
| **Entry** | New Evidence emerges that contradicts validated Knowledge. |
| **Exit** | Resolution achieved → Resolved. Knowledge confirmed → return to Validated with updated confidence. Knowledge invalidated → Deprecated. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Resolved (contradiction resolved); → Validated (Knowledge confirmed, contradiction explained); → Deprecated (Knowledge invalidated) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Validated without resolution (cannot ignore contradiction) |
| **Failure Modes** | Contradiction paralysis (system cannot resolve contradiction); contradiction escalation (multiple contradictions compound without resolution); contradiction suppression (system ignores valid contradictions to protect established Knowledge) |

Contradicted Knowledge is not an error. It signals that understanding is incomplete (Intelligence Loop Stage 2). The system preserves both the Knowledge and the contradiction while resolution is determined.

#### Stage 7 — RESOLVED KNOWLEDGE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The contradiction has been resolved. The Knowledge either survives with updated confidence, is refined, or is replaced. |
| **Entry** | Contradiction resolution process completes. |
| **Exit** | Knowledge confirmed → Validated (updated). Knowledge refined → new candidate generated. Knowledge invalidated → Deprecated. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Validated (Knowledge confirmed); → Candidate (refined understanding needs re-validation); → Deprecated (Knowledge invalidated) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Contradicted (cannot re-enter unresolved state) |
| **Failure Modes** | Superficial resolution (contradiction resolved without genuine understanding); resolution without learning (contradiction resolved but no Knowledge gained); premature resolution (contradiction closed before fully investigated) |

Resolved Knowledge is the outcome of contradiction handling. The system has learned from the contradiction and updated its understanding accordingly.

#### Stage 8 — DEPRECATED KNOWLEDGE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Knowledge is no longer considered valid. It is removed from active Knowledge Memory and prepared for archival. |
| **Entry** | Learning Layer determines Knowledge no longer valid: outcome divergence, context change, superseded by better Knowledge, or contradiction resolution invalidated it. |
| **Exit** | Archived → Archived. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Archived (preserved with deprecation record) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Validated (cannot be restored without full re-validation); → Knowledge Memory |
| **Failure Modes** | Premature deprecation (Knowledge deprecated while still valuable); deprecation without learning (Knowledge deprecated but no Knowledge extracted about why); deprecation without notification (Knowledge used in active recommendations deprecated without informing the Owner) |

Deprecated Knowledge is no longer trusted. But it is not deleted. It is preserved with a full deprecation record: why it was deprecated, when, by what evidence, and what replaced it (if anything).

#### Stage 9 — ARCHIVED KNOWLEDGE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Knowledge has been archived with its full lifecycle record. Preserved for historical reference and potential re-activation. |
| **Entry** | Deprecated Knowledge is archived. |
| **Exit** | Permanent. May be re-activated if new context makes it relevant again. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | → Candidate (rare — new evidence suggests the Knowledge may be valid under new conditions); → Retired (explicitly decommissioned) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Validated (cannot be restored without full re-validation cycle) |
| **Failure Modes** | Archival loss (Knowledge archived without sufficient indexing, unreachable); re-emergence blindness (Knowledge becomes relevant again but system does not recognize it) |

Archived Knowledge is the system's memory of what it used to know. It enables retrospective analysis, historical comparison, and potential re-activation if conditions change.

#### Stage 10 — RETIRED KNOWLEDGE

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Purpose** | The Knowledge has been explicitly decommissioned. Retained only as a record that it existed. |
| **Entry** | Explicit decommissioning decision. Knowledge is no longer needed even for historical reference. |
| **Exit** | Permanent. Retired Knowledge is never re-activated. |
| **Allowed Transitions** | None (terminal state) |
| **Forbidden Transitions** | → Any Knowledge stage (retirement is irreversible) |
| **Failure Modes** | Premature retirement (Knowledge retired while still historically valuable); retirement without documentation (no record of why Knowledge was retired) |

Retired Knowledge is the system's acknowledgment that some understanding is no longer worth preserving. Retirement is a deliberate act with a documented reason.

### 2.3 Lifecycle Principles

1. **Forward motion requires validation.** Knowledge progresses toward Validated only through explicit validation.
2. **Stages are not skipped.** Knowledge cannot go from Hypothesis to Validated without passing through Tentative and Under Validation.
3. **Contradiction is not failure.** Contradicted Knowledge is a normal and valuable state.
4. **Deprecation is not deletion.** Deprecated Knowledge is preserved with full lifecycle record.
5. **Retirement is deliberate and documented.** Nothing is retired without a reason.
6. **Re-activation is possible but rare.** Archived Knowledge may be re-evaluated under new conditions.
7. **Validation is never complete.** Even Validated Knowledge may be re-opened if new Evidence emerges.

### 2.4 Promotion Rules

| Promotion | Condition |
|-----------|-----------|
| Candidate → Hypothesis | Pattern strength meets minimum threshold; understanding has clear proposition |
| Hypothesis → Tentative | Minimum Evidence threshold met; supporting Observations and Evidence exist |
| Tentative → Under Validation | Learning Layer initiates formal validation process |
| Under Validation → Validated | All validation gates pass: sufficient Evidence, no unresolved contradictions above threshold, quality minimums met, confidence minimum satisfied |
| Validated → Deprecated | Outcome divergence exceeds threshold; context change invalidates Knowledge; superseded by better Knowledge |
| Validated → Contradicted | New Evidence directly contradicts Knowledge |

### 2.5 Deprecation Rules

| Condition | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Outcome divergence | Outcomes consistently diverge from Knowledge-based predictions → mark for review |
| Context change | Conditions under which Knowledge was validated have changed → re-evaluate |
| Superseded | Better Knowledge replaces existing Knowledge → deprecate old, promote new |
| Owner correction | Owner indicates Knowledge is incorrect → highest-priority deprecation |

### 2.6 Validation Rules

1. **Evidence sufficiency.** Sufficient Evidence must exist to support the Knowledge. Minimum thresholds per Knowledge type.
2. **Contradiction check.** No unresolved contradictions above the tolerance threshold.
3. **Quality assessment.** Supporting Evidence must meet minimum quality thresholds.
4. **Confidence minimum.** Knowledge must meet minimum confidence threshold.
5. **Context appropriateness.** Knowledge must be valid in the context in which it will be used.
6. **Independence check.** Supporting Evidence must be sufficiently independent (no double-counting).
7. **Explainability.** The system must be able to explain why the Knowledge is valid.

## Part 3 — Knowledge Anatomy

### 3.1 Conceptual Fields

Every piece of Knowledge has the following conceptual fields.

#### Identity

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The unique identity of this Knowledge item. Distinguishes it from all other Knowledge. |
| **Why it exists** | Without identity, Knowledge cannot be referenced, related, retrieved, or tracked across its lifecycle. |
| **Cognitive role** | Provides a fixed reference for all relationships, reasoning, and lifecycle tracking involving this Knowledge. |

#### Statement

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The Knowledge statement itself — a natural-language expression of what the system knows. |
| **Why it exists** | The statement is the Knowledge. Everything else is metadata. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables explanation generation, narrative composition, and human-readable communication. |

#### Origin

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The cognitive process that generated this Knowledge: Pattern promotion, Learning extraction, Owner teaching, system reflection, or external import. |
| **Why it exists** | Origin determines how the Knowledge should be evaluated and weighted. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables origin-appropriate validation, trust calibration, and provenance tracing. |

#### Knowledge Type

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The primary category of the Knowledge (see Part 4 — Knowledge Classification). |
| **Why it exists** | Type determines how Knowledge is validated, retained, retrieved, and applied. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables type-specific processing and appropriate reasoning influence. |

#### Supporting Observations

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | References to the Observations that provide the evidentiary foundation for this Knowledge. |
| **Why it exists** | Observations are the ground truth. Knowledge must trace to the Observations that support it. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables full provenance tracing from Knowledge back to raw facts. |

#### Supporting Patterns

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | References to the Patterns that were promoted to create this Knowledge. |
| **Why it exists** | Most Knowledge is derived from Pattern promotion. The originating Patterns must be traceable. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables Pattern→Knowledge tracing and multi-level justification chains. |

#### Supporting Evidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | References to the Evidence that justifies this Knowledge. |
| **Why it exists** | Every piece of Knowledge must be justified by Evidence (Domain Model Invariant 13). |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables Evidence-based justification and confidence derivation. |

#### Business Scope

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The Business and specific Restaurant to which this Knowledge applies. |
| **Why it exists** | Knowledge is business-specific. Knowledge from one business does not automatically apply to another. |
| **Cognitive role** | Scopes all Knowledge-based reasoning to the relevant business entity. |

#### Temporal Scope

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The time range over which this Knowledge is considered valid. |
| **Why it exists** | Knowledge has temporal validity. Knowledge from 2023 may not apply in 2026. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables freshness assessment, temporal weighting, and staleness detection. |

#### Confidence

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The system's certainty that this Knowledge accurately represents reality. |
| **Why it exists** | Confidence determines how much influence this Knowledge has on reasoning. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables confidence-weighted reasoning and appropriate uncertainty communication. |

#### Freshness

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | How recently the Knowledge was validated or re-validated. |
| **Why it exists** | Freshness determines temporal relevance. Stale Knowledge has reduced influence. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables freshness-based weighting and re-validation triggering. |

#### Validity

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether the Knowledge is currently considered valid (passes all validation criteria). |
| **Why it exists** | Validity is the Knowledge's fitness for active use in reasoning. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables validity-gated reasoning. Invalid Knowledge is excluded from active reasoning. |

#### Importance

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The significance of this Knowledge to business outcomes and system cognition. |
| **Why it exists** | Not all Knowledge is equally important. Importance determines reasoning priority and retention. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables importance-weighted reasoning and attention allocation. |

#### Relationships

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Links from this Knowledge to Observations, Patterns, Evidence, other Knowledge, Decisions, Recommendations, Narratives, Learning events, Memory. |
| **Why it exists** | Knowledge derives its cognitive value from its position in the relationship network. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables relational reasoning and knowledge graph construction. |

#### Dependencies

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Other Knowledge items that this Knowledge depends on. If the dependency is deprecated, this Knowledge may be affected. |
| **Why it exists** | Knowledge is not independent. Knowledge A may assume Knowledge B is valid. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables dependency-aware reasoning and cascade deprecation. |

#### Contradictions

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | References to Knowledge items that contradict this Knowledge, along with the contradiction record. |
| **Why it exists** | Contradictions are preserved, not suppressed. The system tracks all active contradictions. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables contradiction-aware reasoning and resolution tracking. |

#### Version History

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Every version of this Knowledge that has existed, with timestamps and reasons for each version change. |
| **Why it exists** | Knowledge evolves. Version history enables the system to understand how its understanding has changed. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables evolution tracing and historical comparison. |

#### Promotion History

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Record of every lifecycle transition: when promoted, from what stage to what stage, by what criterion. |
| **Why it exists** | Promotion history is the complete provenance of how the Knowledge evolved. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables lifecycle auditing and learning extraction. |

#### Learning Contribution

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether this Knowledge has contributed to further Learning, and how. |
| **Why it exists** | Knowledge that generates further Learning is more valuable than Knowledge that merely confirms. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables learning tracing and meta-cognitive assessment. |

#### Decision Contribution

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether this Knowledge has informed a Recommendation or Decision, and how directly. |
| **Why it exists** | Knowledge that influences decisions is operationally significant. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables decision tracing and Knowledge impact assessment. |

#### Narrative Contribution

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | Whether this Knowledge has contributed to a Narrative, and how central it was. |
| **Why it exists** | Knowledge that informs Narratives communicates understanding to the Owner. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables narrative tracing and communication impact assessment. |

#### Evolution History

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **What it is** | The complete record of this Knowledge's existence: creation, every lifecycle transition, every confidence change, every contradiction, every validation, every deprecation. |
| **Why it exists** | Evolution history enables the system to understand the full biography of what it knows. |
| **Cognitive role** | Enables full provenance, trust auditing, and meta-cognitive learning. |

### 3.2 Field Principles

1. **Every field serves understanding.** No field exists for technical convenience.
2. **Fields are not implementation columns.** They are conceptual dimensions of Knowledge understanding.
3. **Core fields are present at creation** (Identity, Statement, Origin, Knowledge Type, Business Scope, Supporting Observations).
4. **Validation and confidence fields fill as the Knowledge evolves.** A newly created Candidate Knowledge item has minimal validation fields.
5. **Relational fields fill as the Knowledge connects** to other cognitive artifacts.
6. **Fields are frozen at retirement.** The final field state at retirement is the permanent record.

| Property | Definition |

## Part 5 — Knowledge Quality

### 5.1 Quality Dimensions

Every piece of Knowledge is assessed across twelve quality dimensions. Quality determines confidence, retention priority, and reasoning influence.

#### Accuracy

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Knowledge correctly represents the reality it claims to represent. |
| **Assessment** | Cross-validation with independent Observations. Consistency check with related Knowledge. Outcome prediction tracking. |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge-based predictions consistently fail. Owner correction. Contradiction with direct Observations. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Flag for accuracy review. (2) Reduce confidence. (3) If confirmed inaccurate, create contradiction record. (4) Initiate deprecation or correction. (5) Audit downstream reasoning that depended on the inaccurate Knowledge. |

#### Validity

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Knowledge currently passes all validation criteria. Valid Knowledge is fit for active use in reasoning. |
| **Assessment** | Validation gate status. Freshness check. Contradiction check. Context appropriateness check. |
| **Failure detection** | Validation criteria no longer met. Freshness expired. Active contradiction exists. Context changed. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Mark as invalid. (2) Remove from active reasoning. (3) Determine whether re-validation is possible. (4) If yes, initiate re-validation. (5) If no, deprecate. |

#### Freshness

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How recently the Knowledge was validated or re-validated. |
| **Assessment** | Time since last validation. Domain-specific freshness requirements. Frequency of relevant context change. |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge age exceeds domain freshness threshold. Knowledge used in reasoning despite being stale. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Mark as stale. (2) Reduce confidence. (3) Trigger re-validation. (4) If re-validation succeeds, restore with new timestamp. (5) If re-validation fails, deprecate. |

#### Consistency

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Knowledge is internally consistent and consistent with established Knowledge. |
| **Assessment** | Internal logic check. Cross-reference with related Knowledge. Knowledge network consistency analysis. |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge contradicts itself. Knowledge contradicts established Knowledge without justification. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Create contradiction record. (2) Attempt reconciliation. (3) If reconciled, document resolution. (4) If not reconciled, reduce confidence and flag for investigation. |

#### Robustness

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How resilient the Knowledge is to challenges, noise, and context changes. Robust Knowledge holds under varying conditions. |
| **Assessment** | Sensitivity analysis: does the Knowledge hold under different conditions? How much Evidence variation can it tolerate? |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge collapses under mild challenge. Knowledge is valid only under narrow conditions. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess robustness boundaries. (2) Document fragility conditions. (3) Reduce confidence when operating near fragility boundaries. (4) If too fragile for general use, narrow scope or deprecate. |

#### Completeness

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Knowledge carries sufficient supporting Evidence, context, and provenance to be fully evaluated and justified. |
| **Assessment** | Presence of all required fields. Completeness of supporting Evidence. Provenance chain completeness. |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge used in reasoning that requires Evidence the Knowledge does not have. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Flag missing fields or Evidence. (2) Attempt to gather from related Knowledge. (3) If gathering succeeds, annotate. (4) If gathering fails, reduce confidence proportional to completeness gap. |

#### Traceability

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Knowledge can be traced to its originating Observations with complete provenance. |
| **Assessment** | Presence and completeness of Supporting Observations, Patterns, Evidence. Ability to follow from Knowledge → Observation. |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge with missing, incomplete, or broken provenance chain. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Flag untraceable Knowledge. (2) Reduce confidence. (3) Attempt to recover provenance. (4) If unrecoverable, demote to Historical with traceability limitation. |

#### Stability

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Knowledge has remained consistent over time without frequent changes. Stable Knowledge is more trustworthy. |
| **Assessment** | Version history analysis. Frequency of changes. Magnitude of changes. |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge changes frequently. Knowledge oscillates between states. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess change pattern. (2) If changes reflect genuine evolution, document. (3) If changes reflect instability, investigate root cause. (4) If Knowledge is inherently unstable, reduce confidence and narrow scope. |

#### Generality

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | How broadly the Knowledge applies. General Knowledge is applicable across many contexts; specific Knowledge applies in narrow conditions. |
| **Assessment** | Context range over which the Knowledge holds. Number of conditions under which it has been validated. |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge applied outside its valid context. Knowledge assumed general when it is specific. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify scope boundaries. (2) Document valid context range. (3) Apply context gating during reasoning. (4) If overgeneralization is detected, narrow scope. |

#### Reliability

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Knowledge consistently produces accurate predictions and sound reasoning across multiple use cycles. |
| **Assessment** | Track record of Knowledge-informed reasoning outcomes. Prediction accuracy over time. |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge-informed reasoning increasingly produces incorrect conclusions. Prediction accuracy trends downward. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Flag for reliability review. (2) Investigate root cause (stale, contradicted, overgeneralized). (3) If remediable, update and re-validate. (4) If not remediable, deprecate. (5) Audit downstream reasoning. |

#### Explainability

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Knowledge can be explained in business terms. Can the system articulate why this Knowledge is valid and what it means? |
| **Assessment** | The system can produce a natural-language explanation of the Knowledge, its Evidence, and its reasoning significance. |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge passes quality checks but the system cannot explain why it is valid or what it means. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Attempt to articulate the Knowledge. (2) If articulation succeeds, document. (3) If articulation fails, investigate why. (4) If Knowledge was promoted without genuine understanding, deprecate. |

#### Relevance

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Meaning** | Whether the Knowledge has proven useful when applied in reasoning. High-relevance Knowledge produces better reasoning outcomes. |
| **Assessment** | Outcome of reasoning that used this Knowledge. Did the Knowledge contribute to accurate understanding and effective recommendations? |
| **Failure detection** | Knowledge is retrieved but does not influence reasoning outcome. Knowledge is retrieved but leads to incorrect conclusions. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Assess relevance failure. (2) Improve indexing to match retrieval context better. (3) If Knowledge is persistently irrelevant, reduce retrieval priority. (4) If Knowledge has become misleading, deprecate. |

### 5.2 Quality Principles

1. **Every piece of Knowledge has a quality profile.** Quality is assessed at creation and continuously updated.
2. **Quality determines confidence.** Higher-quality Knowledge has higher confidence.
3. **Quality determines reasoning influence.** Higher-quality Knowledge has more influence on reasoning outcomes.
4. **Quality determines retention priority.** Higher-quality Knowledge is retained more aggressively.
5. **Quality can change.** Knowledge may improve (re-validated, reinforced) or degrade (stale, contradicted).
6. **Quality failures are not deletions.** Low-quality Knowledge is preserved with its quality annotation.
7. **Quality is re-evaluated on use.** A Knowledge item's quality at retrieval may differ from its quality at creation.

## Part 7 — Knowledge Invariants

### 7.1 Identity Invariants (1–5)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | Every Knowledge item has a unique identity. | Identity collision. The system confuses two different pieces of understanding. |
| 2 | Identity is assigned at creation and never changes. | Identity mutation. Knowledge becomes untraceable across its lifecycle. |
| 3 | Every Knowledge item has exactly one identity. | Identity multiplicity. The same understanding appears as multiple distinct Knowledge items. |
| 4 | Identity is independent of the statement it holds. | Identity-statement coupling. Changed understanding creates identity crisis. |
| 5 | Identity survives deprecation and retirement. Even retired Knowledge retains its identity. | Identity reclamation. A retired Knowledge item's identity could be reused. |

### 7.2 Validation Invariants (6–14)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 6 | Knowledge must be validated before promotion to Knowledge Memory. Unvalidated content is not Knowledge. | False Knowledge. Unvalidated understanding enters the system's trusted core. |
| 7 | Validation requires Evidence. Every Knowledge item must trace to supporting Evidence. | Evidence-less Knowledge. Knowledge exists without justification. |
| 8 | Validation requires Observation support. Every Knowledge item must ultimately trace to Observations. | Groundless Knowledge. Knowledge cannot be traced to empirical reality. |
| 9 | Validation checks for contradictions. Knowledge with unresolved contradictions cannot be promoted. | Contradictory Knowledge. Knowledge is promoted despite conflicting Evidence. |
| 10 | Validation is independent of the Knowledge's desirability. The system does not favor Knowledge that confirms its expectations. | Confirmation bias. Knowledge that confirms existing beliefs receives less scrutiny. |
| 11 | Validation is repeatable. The same Knowledge validated under the same conditions produces the same outcome. | Unreliable validation. Validation outcomes are inconsistent. |
| 12 | Validation is recorded. Every validation decision is documented with criteria, Evidence, and outcome. | Validation amnesia. The system cannot audit why Knowledge was validated. |
| 13 | Validation includes confidence assessment. Every validated Knowledge item has an initial confidence value. | Confidence-less validation. Knowledge enters without quantified certainty. |
| 14 | Validation is never final. Even validated Knowledge may be re-opened for re-validation. | Permanent validation. Knowledge cannot be re-examined once validated. |

### 7.3 Evidence Invariants (15–21)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|
| 15 | Every Knowledge item must be supported by at least one Evidence item. | Evidence-less Knowledge. Knowledge exists without justification. |
| 16 | Knowledge confidence is derived from its supporting Evidence quality and quantity. | Confidence-Evidence disconnect. Knowledge confidence does not reflect its support. |
| 17 | Evidence independence is required. Dependent Evidence does not add full confidence to Knowledge. | False corroboration. Dependent Evidence creates illusion of strong support. |
| 18 | Evidence quality decay reduces Knowledge confidence. If supporting Evidence degrades, Knowledge confidence degrades. | Evidence-Knowledge decoupling. Knowledge confidence persists despite Evidence degradation. |
| 19 | New Evidence may increase or decrease Knowledge confidence. Evidence affects Knowledge bidirectionally. | One-way Evidence. Evidence can only increase, never decrease, Knowledge confidence. |
| 20 | Evidence for Knowledge is preserved even if the Knowledge is deprecated. Deprecated Knowledge retains its Evidence trace. | Evidence loss on deprecation. Deprecated Knowledge loses its justification record. |
| 21 | Contradictory Evidence is preserved alongside supporting Evidence. The system does not choose sides. | Evidence suppression. Contrary Evidence is hidden to protect Knowledge confidence. |

### 7.4 Promotion Invariants (22–28)

| # | Invariant | Violation Consequence |
|---|-----------|----------------------|

## Part 8 — Knowledge Failure Modes

### 8.1 False Knowledge

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Knowledge is created based on incorrect Observations, invalid Patterns, or fabricated Evidence. The system believes something that is not true. |
| **Detection** | Knowledge-based predictions consistently fail. Knowledge contradicts direct Observations. Owner corrects the Knowledge. |
| **Mitigation** | Rigorous validation gates. Mandatory Evidence traceability. Cross-validation with independent sources. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify as false. (2) Deprecate immediately. (3) Create contradiction record with full documentation. (4) Audit all downstream reasoning that depended on this Knowledge. (5) Investigate root cause: how did false Knowledge pass validation? (6) Communicate any affected Owner communications. |

### 8.2 Stale Knowledge

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Knowledge was valid but the business environment has changed. The Knowledge has not been re-validated and no longer reflects reality. |
| **Detection** | Knowledge age exceeds freshness threshold. Knowledge-based predictions diverge from outcomes. Context conditions have changed since validation. |
| **Mitigation** | Freshness-based confidence decay. Automatic re-validation scheduling. Context change detection. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Mark as stale. (2) Reduce confidence. (3) Trigger re-validation. (4) If re-validation succeeds, restore with new timestamp. (5) If re-validation fails, deprecate with documentation of what changed. |

### 8.3 Conflicting Knowledge

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Two or more Knowledge items support incompatible conclusions. The system has genuine uncertainty about which understanding is correct. |
| **Detection** | Knowledge items pointing to contradictory conclusions are both validated. Reasoning that uses both produces inconsistent results. |
| **Mitigation** | Contradiction detection at Knowledge creation. Mandatory contradiction recording. Principled resolution hierarchy. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Record the conflict. (2) Apply resolution hierarchy: context → Evidence quality → source trust → completeness → temporal recency → Owner. (3) If resolved, document resolution and adjust confidences. (4) If unresolvable, escalate to Owner. (5) Extract learning from the conflict. |

### 8.4 Knowledge Drift

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Knowledge content gradually changes over time without explicit updating. The Knowledge statement subtly shifts, relationships weaken, or confidence drifts. |
| **Detection** | Knowledge no longer matches the Observations it originally traced to. Version history shows incremental changes without explicit validation. |
| **Mitigation** | Immutable Knowledge statements. Version-controlled updates. Audit-triggered re-validation. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify drifted Knowledge. (2) Assess drift magnitude. (3) If drift is minor, re-validate and update. (4) If drift is significant, deprecate and create corrected Knowledge. (5) Investigate the mechanism that allowed undocumented drift. |

### 8.5 Knowledge Fragmentation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Knowledge exists in isolation without adequate relationships. Understanding is stored without connections, making it unreachable through relational retrieval. |
| **Detection** | Knowledge items with zero or minimal relationships. Retrieval that should return connected Knowledge returns only isolated items. |
| **Mitigation** | Minimum relationship requirements for Knowledge Memory. Periodic relationship audits. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify fragmented Knowledge. (2) Assess relationship gaps. (3) Attempt to establish missing relationships. (4) If relationships cannot be established, evaluate whether the Knowledge should be retained or deprecated. (5) Address root cause: is the learning strategy producing isolated Knowledge? |

### 8.6 Knowledge Pollution

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Low-quality, false, or irrelevant Knowledge accumulates in Knowledge Memory, degrading the quality of all Knowledge-based reasoning. |
| **Detection** | Increasing noise in Knowledge retrieval. Decreasing accuracy of Knowledge-informed reasoning. Growing number of low-confidence Knowledge items. |
| **Mitigation** | Quality thresholds for Knowledge Memory retention. Regular Knowledge audits. Importance-weighted retention. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify pollution sources. (2) Audit low-quality Knowledge. (3) Deprecate Knowledge below quality thresholds. (4) Clean downstream reasoning. (5) Address root cause: is a particular source, category, or validation process producing low-quality Knowledge? |

### 8.7 Unsupported Knowledge

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Knowledge exists in Knowledge Memory without adequate supporting Evidence. The system cannot justify what it knows. |
| **Detection** | Knowledge cannot trace to supporting Observations or Evidence. Knowledge confidence is high despite minimal Evidence. |
| **Mitigation** | Evidence minimum requirements for Knowledge promotion. Proveance verification at promotion. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify unsupported Knowledge. (2) Assess Evidence gap. (3) If Evidence can be gathered, initiate re-validation. (4) If Evidence cannot be gathered, deprecate. (5) Audit downstream reasoning that depended on unsupported Knowledge. |

### 8.8 Overgeneralization

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Knowledge that is valid in a specific context is applied more broadly than justified. The system overestimates the scope of its understanding. |
| **Detection** | Knowledge produces accurate predictions in its validation context but fails outside it. Context conditions not properly documented or enforced. |
| **Mitigation** | Explicit context scope at Knowledge creation. Context gating during Knowledge retrieval. Generality assessment during validation. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify overgeneralization. (2) Determine the valid context boundary. (3) Update Knowledge scope documentation. (4) Apply context gating during retrieval. (5) If the Knowledge cannot be properly scoped, deprecate and replace with more specific items. |

### 8.9 Knowledge Saturation

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | Knowledge Memory has too many items, causing retrieval degradation. Redundant, overlapping, or low-value Knowledge crowds out high-value items. |
| **Detection** | Retrieval latency increases. Retrieval relevance decreases. Less important Knowledge crowds out more important Knowledge. |
| **Mitigation** | Knowledge Memory capacity management. Importance-weighted retention. Automatic deprecation of redundant or low-value Knowledge. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify saturated Knowledge areas. (2) Assess current Knowledge by importance and quality. (3) Deprecate or merge redundant Knowledge. (4) Restore retrieval quality. (5) Adjust validation and retention thresholds to prevent recurrence. |

### 8.10 Knowledge Collapse

| Property | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Cause** | A critical Knowledge item is deprecated or invalidated, and the Knowledge that depended on it collapses. A cascade of deprecations follows. |
| **Detection** | Single deprecation triggers multiple downstream deprecations. Reasoning quality degrades rapidly. |
| **Mitigation** | Dependency tracking. Graceful degradation: dependent Knowledge is flagged, not automatically deprecated. Confidence reduction rather than immediate deprecation. |
| **Recovery** | (1) Identify the collapse epicenter. (2) Assess the dependency cascade. (3) Determine whether the foundational Knowledge can be restored or replaced. (4) If yes, rebuild from the foundation. (5) If no, systematically deprecate dependents with documentation. (6) Notify the Owner if collapse affects active recommendations or narratives. |
