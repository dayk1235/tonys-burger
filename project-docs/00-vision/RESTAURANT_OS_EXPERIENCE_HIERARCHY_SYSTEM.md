# Restaurant OS — Experience Hierarchy System

## The Universal Grammar of Attention

---

## Preamble

Every screen in Restaurant OS is a hierarchy of attention.

Some information must be seen immediately. Some must be discovered. Some must never compete. This document defines the rules for how information earns its place in the owner's perception — not through visual weight, not through animation, not through spacing, but through the fundamental architecture of attention itself.

This system is not about design. It is about perception.

It defines which information deserves to be seen first, which deserves to be seen at all, and which must remain invisible until requested. It governs the competition for attention on every screen, across every surface, in every moment.

The principles below are timeless. They apply regardless of screen size, technology, or visual style. They are the grammar that every screen in Restaurant OS must follow.

---

## SECTION 1 — First Impression

**Principle:** The first piece of information the owner sees must answer: *"Is my restaurant okay?"*

**Purpose:** The owner opens the platform with a single question. That question must be answered before any other information is presented. Every millisecond the owner spends searching for this answer is a failure of the hierarchy.

**Psychological reasoning:** When humans face uncertainty about a critical domain, their cognitive system prioritizes threat assessment above all else. Until the primary question is answered — *"Am I safe?"* — the brain cannot process secondary information. The owner cannot evaluate a recommendation, analyze a metric, or read a summary until they know whether their restaurant is in trouble.

**Good example:** The owner opens the platform and immediately sees Restaurant Health — the number, the state, and a one-line explanation. Within one second, they know the answer.

**Bad example:** The owner opens the platform and sees a dashboard with metrics, charts, activity feeds, and recommendations. No single element answers the primary question. The owner must scan, interpret, and decide where to look.

**Validation question:** Within the first 500 milliseconds of opening the platform, does the owner know whether their restaurant needs their attention?

---

## SECTION 2 — First Three Seconds

**Principle:** Within three seconds, the owner must know: *"What should I do today?"*

**Purpose:** After confirming the restaurant's status, the owner's next question is about action. The hierarchy must guide them from awareness to decision within three seconds.

**Psychological reasoning:** The "cognitive snapshot" — the brain's ability to assess a scene's meaning — completes within approximately three seconds. After this window, the owner shifts from rapid assessment to deliberate analysis. The hierarchy must deliver its primary message within this window.

**Good example:** After seeing health, the owner's eye naturally moves to the Daily Brief's top recommendation. The path from status to action is one glance.

**Bad example:** Health is visible, but the path to the next decision requires scrolling, tapping, or interpreting multiple competing elements.

**Validation question:** After three seconds, can the owner articulate what they should do next?

---

## SECTION 3 — One Primary Focus

**Principle:** Every screen has exactly one primary focus. Everything else is subordinate.

**Purpose:** The hierarchy must be absolute. If two elements compete for primary focus, neither wins. The screen becomes noise.

**Psychological reasoning:** The human visual system can only process one focal point at a time. Saccadic eye movements — the rapid jumps between fixations — take approximately 200 milliseconds each. Every unnecessary fixation taxes the owner's attention and increases cognitive load.

**Good example:** The dashboard has one primary element: Restaurant Health. The health card is the undisputed visual anchor. Every other element is clearly secondary.

**Bad example:** The dashboard has health, metrics, activity, and recommendations all at similar visual weight. The owner's eye bounces between elements, unable to settle.

**Validation question:** If forced to identify the single most important element on this screen, would every observer choose the same element?

---

## SECTION 4 — Information Gravity

**Principle:** Information should feel as though it has mass. More important information has stronger gravity, drawing the owner's attention naturally.

**Purpose:** The hierarchy should feel physical — the owner should not need to "find" important information. It should pull their attention through its inherent gravity.

**Psychological reasoning:** The human visual system is drawn to contrast, size, and position. These are not learned behaviors — they are hardwired. Elements that are larger, higher, or more visually distinct are perceived as more important. Information gravity exploits this innate hierarchy.

**Good example:** Health sits at the top of the screen. It is the largest numerical element. The owner's eye arrives at health before any other element, not because of instruction, but because of gravity.

**Bad example:** Health is the same size as other metrics. It is positioned mid-screen. The owner must search to find it.

**Validation question:** Does the most important information on this screen have the strongest visual gravity?

---

## SECTION 5 — Visual Silence

**Principle:** The elements that do not demand attention are as important as the elements that do.

**Purpose:** The hierarchy is defined as much by what is quiet as by what is prominent. Secondary information must know its place and stay silent until called upon.

**Psychological reasoning:** Attention is a finite resource. Every element that competes for attention — through color, motion, size, or position — consumes a portion of that resource. Visual silence preserves attention for the moments that matter.

**Good example:** Supporting metrics are small, muted, and positioned below the primary content. They exist to be found when needed, not to announce themselves.

**Bad example:** Every metric, card, and section uses similar visual weight. None is willing to be quiet. The owner is overwhelmed by competition.

**Validation question:** Can the owner easily ignore the secondary information on this screen to focus on what matters?

---

## SECTION 6 — Progressive Discovery

**Principle:** Information reveals in layers. The owner controls the depth of discovery.

**Purpose:** The hierarchy must support multiple depths of engagement. The owner who wants a quick check should get it in seconds. The owner who wants deep analysis should find it one tap away.

**Psychological reasoning:** Humans process information most effectively when it is presented in increasing levels of detail. This is called the "inverted pyramid" — start with the conclusion, then provide supporting evidence, then supply context. Each level of depth is a choice the owner makes, not an obligation.

**Good example:** Health shows a score, state, and tagline. Tapping opens a summary. Tapping again opens the detail. The owner controls the depth.

**Bad example:** Health shows the score, all dimension breakdowns, trend charts, comparison data, and historical context all at once. The owner cannot find the signal.

**Validation question:** What is the minimum information the owner needs to make a decision? Is everything else one tap away?

---

## SECTION 7 — Reading Rhythm

**Principle:** Information should be scannable in hierarchy, not in density.

**Purpose:** The owner should be able to scan the screen and understand its structure without reading every word. Hierarchy is communicated through position and visual weight, not through text density.

**Psychological reasoning:** The F-shaped reading pattern — scanning horizontally across the top, then vertically down the left side — is the natural human approach to new information. The hierarchy must align with this pattern. The top-left region receives the most attention. The bottom-right receives the least.

**Good example:** The most important information is at the top. Each section has a clear heading. The owner scans down the left edge and understands the screen's structure.

**Bad example:** Information is scattered across the screen. No clear scanning path exists. The owner must read to understand structure.

**Validation question:** Can the owner understand the screen's structure by scanning headings alone?

---

## SECTION 8 — Editorial Flow

**Principle:** Screens read like a well-edited publication. From most important to least important. From conclusion to evidence.

**Purpose:** Information should flow naturally from the most critical insight to the supporting details. The owner should never need to re-read or re-interpret to understand priority.

**Psychological reasoning:** Editorial flow mirrors how humans naturally tell stories — the punchline comes first, then the explanation. This structure respects the owner's time by delivering maximum value in minimum time.

**Good example:** A recommendation card leads with the conclusion: "Afternoon sales are declining." Then provides evidence: "18% drop this week." Then offers an action: "Try an after-lunch Happy Hour."

**Bad example:** A recommendation card starts with data tables, charts, and historical context. The owner must to conclusion.

**Validation question:** Does the most actionable information appear first in every section?

---

## SECTION 9 — Decision Hierarchy

**Principle:** Information is prioritized by the decisions it enables, not by its data source.

**Purpose:** Not all data is created equal. A metric that drives a decision is more important than a metric that is merely informative. The hierarchy must reflect decision value, not data volume.

**Psychological reasoning:** Humans are decision-making engines. Information that directly enables a decision activates the prefrontal cortex — the part of the brain responsible for executive function. Information that is merely informative activates shorter, shallower neural paths. Decision-enabling information must have hierarchy priority.

**Good example:** Health is first because it enables the highest-level decision: "Do I need to act?" The Daily Brief follows because it enables the next decision: "What should I do?"

**Bad example:** Raw metrics appear before actionable recommendations. The owner must generate their own insight from data.

**Validation question:** Does the hierarchy of information on this screen match the hierarchy of decisions the owner needs to make?

---

## SECTION 10 — Emotional Hierarchy

**Principle:** The emotional impact of information must match its position in the hierarchy.

**Purpose:** Information that creates strong emotions — concern, relief, urgency, satisfaction — must be positioned higher in the hierarchy. Emotionally neutral information belongs lower.

**Psychological reasoning:** Emotional arousal significantly enhances memory encoding and attention allocation. Information that triggers an emotional response is processed more deeply and remembered longer. The hierarchy must ensure that emotionally significant information receives the attention it deserves.

**Good example:** A critical health state occupies maximum visual gravity. The owner feels the importance before they read the details.

**Bad example:** A critical alert is presented with the same visual weight as a routine metric update. The owner cannot distinguish urgency from normalcy.

**Validation question:** Does the emotional weight of each piece of information match its position in the hierarchy?

---

## SECTION 11 — Trust Hierarchy

**Principle:** The owner must trust the most prominent information before they can trust anything else.

**Purpose:** Trust flows downward through the hierarchy. If the primary element is not trusted, everything below it is suspect. The hierarchy must protect trust by ensuring the most visible information is the most reliable.

**Psychological reasoning:** Humans use a "trust anchor" — a single reliable reference point — to calibrate their trust in surrounding information. If the trust anchor is strong, surrounding information is given the benefit of the doubt. If the anchor is weak, everything is questioned.

**Good example:** Health is always accurate, always current, and always sourced. The owner trusts it completely. This trust extends to the recommendations below it.

**Bad example:** Health occasionally displays stale data or unexplained changes. The owner's trust erodes, and they begin questioning every element on the screen.

**Validation question:** Is the most prominent information on this screen the most trustworthy?

---

## SECTION 12 — Business Priority Hierarchy

**Principle:** Information is prioritized by business impact, not by recency.

**Purpose:** A small revenue decline from yesterday is more important than a large revenue from two weeks ago. The hierarchy must reflect what matters to the business, not what happened most recently.

**Psychological reasoning:** Human attention is naturally drawn to novelty — the most recent event captures focus regardless of its importance. This is a cognitive bias that the hierarchy must counteract by actively prioritizing impact over recency.

**Good example:** The Daily Brief surfaces the recommendation with the highest expected business impact as the primary card, even if a more recent event exists.

**Bad example:** The most recent data point is given the highest position, regardless of its significance to the business.

**Validation question:** Is the most prominent information on this screen the most impactful for the business, or just the most recent?

---

## SECTION 13 — Attention Anchors

**Principle:** Every screen must have a visual anchor — a single element that the owner's eye returns to between explorations.

**Purpose:** The owner needs a home base — a reference point that tells them where they are and whether anything has changed. The attention anchor is the constant in a changing information landscape.

**Psychological reasoning:** Humans maintain spatial awareness through landmarks — reference points that remain constant while surroundings change. The attention anchor serves the same purpose: it is the fixed point from which all other information is oriented.

**Good example:** Health always occupies the same position on every screen. The owner knows where to look for it without thinking. After reading a recommendation, they glance back at health to confirm nothing changed.

**Bad example:** Elements shift position based on context. Health moves depending on what else is on screen. The owner must search for the anchor every time.

**Validation question:** Does every screen have a persistent element that serves as the owner's visual home base?

---

## SECTION 14 — Confidence Before Detail

**Principle:** The owner must feel confident in the conclusion before being presented with supporting detail.

**Purpose:** Detail without context creates uncertainty. The hierarchy must deliver the conclusion first, establish confidence, and then provide detail for those who need it.

**Psychological reasoning:** When presented with a conclusion first, the human brain creates a mental model that organizes subsequent detail. The detail confirms or refines the model rather than creating it from scratch. This is significantly more efficient than building understanding from raw data.

**Good example:** "Your restaurant is Healthy." Then, below: "Sales are on track. Customer traffic is normal. No issues detected."

**Bad example:** "Sales: +3%. Traffic: -1%. Labor: 32%. Food cost: 28%. Inventory: 85%." The owner must interpret all data to derive the conclusion.

**Validation question:** Does every section deliver its conclusion before its evidence?

---

## SECTION 15 — Summary Before Evidence

**Principle:** Every section must begin with a one-sentence summary that the owner can read in under two seconds.

**Purpose:** The owner should never have to read an entire section to understand its point. The summary is the section's thesis statement. Everything below it is supporting evidence.

**Psychological reasoning:** Humans decide whether to engage with content within approximately two seconds. A clear summary allows the owner to make an informed decision about whether to invest attention in the details.

**Good example:** "Afternoon sales were slower than usual. — You had 6 orders between 2 and 5 PM — 18% fewer than last week. A Happy Hour promotion could recover this lost revenue."

**Bad example:** A section that begins with a chart, a table, or raw data. The owner must interpret before understanding.

**Validation question:** Can the owner understand this section's message by reading only the first sentence?

---

## SECTION 16 — Context Before Numbers

**Principle:** The meaning of a number must be established before the number is displayed.

**Purpose:** Numbers without context are noise. The hierarchy must ensure that every metric is preceded by the context that makes it meaningful.

**Psychological reasoning:** Numbers are processed in the brain's parietal lobe — the region responsible for quantitative reasoning. But meaning is processed in the prefrontal cortex. The brain must shuttle between these regions to connect a number to its significance. Context reduces this cognitive distance.

**Good example:** "Delivery delays are increasing. Orders are taking 12 minutes longer than two weeks ago."

**Bad example:** "12 minutes" displayed with no context. The owner wonders: is that good or bad? Compared to what?

**Validation question:** Does every number on this screen have a clear contextual frame before it appears?

---

## SECTION 17 — Story Before Data

**Principle:** Information should be structured as a narrative, not a report.

**Purpose:** Humans understand the world through stories. Information hierarchies that follow narrative structure — setup, conflict, resolution — are processed more naturally than hierarchies that present data as disconnected facts.

**Psychological reasoning:** Narratives activate multiple regions of the brain simultaneously — language, sensory, motor, and emotional centers — creating richer encoding and better recall. Data alone activates only the language and quantitative centers.

**Good example:** "This week, we noticed something unusual. Afternoon orders dropped 18%. It happened before last month too. A lunch special might help."

**Bad example:** "Afternoon orders: 6. Change: -18%. Comparable period: same."

**Validation question:** Does this information tell a story, or does it just present data?

---

## SECTION 18 — Information Density

**Principle:** The density of information on any screen must be inversely proportional to the urgency of the primary message.

**Purpose:** When something critical is happening, the screen must be sparse. When everything is normal, the screen can be richer. Urgency demands clarity. Calm permits exploration.

**Psychological reasoning:** Under stress, the human visual field narrows — a phenomenon called "tunnel vision" caused by the sympathetic nervous system. The hierarchy must adapt to this biological reality by reducing information density when urgency is high.

**Good example:** In a critical state, the screen shows only health, the critical alert, and three prioritized action steps. Everything else is hidden.

**Bad example:** A critical alert is surrounded by recommendations, activity feeds, and metric cards. The owner cannot focus on what matters.

**Validation question:** Does the information density of this screen match the urgency of its primary message?

---

## SECTION 19 — Screen Breathing

**Principle:** Every screen must have deliberate moments of emptiness — visual pauses where the owner's eye can rest.

**Purpose:** Information consumption is tiring. The hierarchy must include breathing room — spaces where nothing competes for attention — to prevent cognitive fatigue.

**Psychological reasoning:** The brain requires micro-breaks between periods of focused attention. These breaks typically last 200-500 milliseconds and occur naturally when the visual field contains empty space. Without empty space, the brain cannot rest, and fatigue accumulates rapidly.

**Good example:** Between health and the Daily Brief, there is generous whitespace. The owner's eye rests before moving to the next section.

**Bad example:** Sections are packed tightly together. No visual breathing room exists. The owner feels the screen is dense and demanding.

**Validation question:** Does this screen have intentional moments of emptiness where the owner's eye can rest?

---

## SECTION 20 — Empty Space Philosophy

**Principle:** Empty space is not wasted space. It is attention preserved.

**Purpose:** The hierarchy values what is not shown as much as what is shown. Empty space communicates confidence — the platform does not need to fill every pixel to prove its value.

**Psychological reasoning:** The Gestalt principle of figure-ground perception states that humans perceive objects by distinguishing them from their background. Empty space creates the background against which information becomes figure. Without sufficient background, nothing can be clearly perceived.

**Good example:** A card with generous padding. The content inside is clearly separated from its surroundings. The owner processes it as a discrete unit.

**Bad example:** A card with tight padding. Content feels cramped. The owner struggles to distinguish where one element ends and another begins.

**Validation question:** Does this screen have more empty space than content? If not, what can be removed?

---

## SECTION 21 — Layered Understanding

**Principle:** The hierarchy must support at least three layers of understanding: awareness, comprehension, and mastery.

**Purpose:** Different owners engage at different depths. The hierarchy must serve all three levels without forcing any owner into a depth they do not want.

**Psychological reasoning:** Humans progress through stages of understanding — from "I know something exists" (awareness) to "I understand what it means" (comprehension) to "I can act on this knowledge" (mastery). Each stage requires different information and different hierarchy depth.

**Good example (Awareness):** Health state and score. The owner knows the restaurant is "Healthy."

**Good example (Comprehension):** Health state + tagline + trend. The owner understands what "Healthy" means in context.

**Good example (Mastery):** Full health breakdown with dimensions, comparisons, and action links. The owner can decide what to do.

**Bad example:** Only the awareness layer exists. The owner knows the state but cannot understand or act on it.

**Validation question:** Can the owner at every depth level — glance, read, study — extract the value they need?

---

## SECTION 22 — Supporting Information

**Principle:** Supporting information must be visually quiet but discoverable without effort.

**Purpose:** The hierarchy must make a clear distinction between primary and supporting information. Supporting information is always accessible but never prominent.

**Psychological reasoning:** Knowing that supporting information exists — even without reading it — reduces anxiety. The brain allocates attention more efficiently when it knows that additional context is available if needed.

**Good example:** A small timestamp below health: "Updated 30 seconds ago." It is visible when looked for but never demands attention.

**Bad example:** No supporting information exists, or it is presented with the same prominence as primary information.

**Validation question:** Is every piece of supporting information on this screen clearly secondary in visual weight?

---

## SECTION 23 — Persistent Elements

**Principle:** Elements that provide orienting context — health, navigation, freshness — must persist consistently across screens.

**Purpose:** The owner should never feel lost. Persistent elements create spatial and informational continuity that anchors the owner regardless of where they are in the platform.

**Psychological reasoning:** Spatial navigation in digital environments follows the same neural pathways as physical navigation. The hippocampus — the brain's spatial mapping center — requires consistent landmarks to maintain orientation. Persistent elements serve as digital landmarks.

**Good example:** Health appears in the same position on the dashboard, the insights page, and the experiment page. The owner always knows where to find it.

**Bad example:** Health appears on the dashboard but is absent from other pages. The owner must remember their health status as they navigate.

**Validation question:** Can the owner navigate to any screen and immediately find the persistent elements they rely on?

---

## SECTION 24 — Temporary Elements

**Principle:** Temporary information — alerts, notifications, confirmations — must clearly communicate their impermanence.

**Purpose:** The hierarchy must distinguish between persistent information (which the owner can trust to remain) and temporary information (which will disappear). This distinction prevents the owner from feeling like important information might vanish.

**Psychological reasoning:** The brain allocates different attention strategies to permanent versus transient information. Permanent information is integrated into the owner's mental model. Transient information is held in working memory with an expectation of decay. Clear impermanence cues prevent unnecessary anxiety about losing information.

**Good example:** A confirmation toast appears, persists for three seconds, and fades gently. The owner knows it is temporary and does not try to memorize it.

**Bad example:** A critical alert that looks like a permanent screen element. The owner worries that dismissing it will lose important information.

**Validation question:** Can the owner immediately distinguish temporary elements from persistent ones?

---

## SECTION 25 — Motion Hierarchy

**Principle:** The intensity and duration of motion must match the importance of the information it accompanies.

**Purpose:** Motion is a tool for directing attention. The hierarchy must ensure that the owner's attention is directed to the most important information first, not to the most animated information.

**Psychological reasoning:** The human visual system is exquisitely sensitive to motion — it triggers an orienting response in the superior colliculus within approximately 100 milliseconds. This response is involuntary. Motion can override the owner's intentional focus. It must be used with extreme discipline.

**Good example:** A health state change uses a gentle, smooth transition. The motion communicates the change without startling the owner.

**Bad example:** A minor metric update uses a dramatic animation. The owner's attention is drawn to something unimportant.

**Validation question:** Does every animation on this screen match the hierarchy position of the information it accompanies?

---

## SECTION 26 — Notification Hierarchy

**Principle:** Notifications are classified by urgency, not by source. Every notification must answer: Why now? Why me? What should I do?

**Purpose:** The notification hierarchy must protect the owner from noise. Notifications from any source are filtered through a single urgency classification before reaching the owner.

**Psychological reasoning:** The brain processes interruptions as threat assessments. Every notification triggers a cortisol response — even positive ones. Unnecessary notifications accumulate stress and teach the owner to ignore the platform.

**Good example:** A notification: "Your Happy Hour experiment is ready to review. Afternoon orders increased 14%." The owner knows why it appeared, why it matters, and what to do.

**Bad example:** A notification: "Experiment status updated." The owner must open the platform to understand what changed and what to do.

**Validation question:** Does every notification on this platform answer all three questions?

---

## SECTION 27 — Interruptions Philosophy

**Principle:** The platform should interrupt the owner only when their attention is the only solution. All other communication is ambient.

**Purpose:** Interruption is the highest-cost communication mechanism in the platform. It consumes the owner's focus, context, and time. It must be reserved for the moments when nothing else will suffice.

**Psychological reasoning:** Task-switching carries a cognitive cost of 15-30 minutes per interruption in knowledge work environments. While restaurant operations are different, the principle holds — every interruption forces the owner to stop their current task, assess the new information, and decide whether to act.

**Good example:** A critical health decline triggers an interruption. The owner stops what they are doing and addresses it. This happens once per month.

**Bad example:** A recommendation triggers an interruption. The owner stops serving a customer to check a suggestion that could have waited.

**Validation question:** Would the owner's business suffer materially if this interruption were delayed by one hour?

---

## SECTION 28 — Dashboard Flow

**Principle:** The dashboard must follow a single vertical flow from status to action. No horizontal navigation required.

**Purpose:** The dashboard hierarchy is a reading path — from top to bottom, from awareness to action. The owner should never need to navigate sideways to complete their daily check-in.

**Psychological reasoning:** Linear reading paths minimize cognitive load by eliminating navigational decisions. Each section naturally leads to the next. The owner scans downward without needing to decide where to go.

**Good example:** Dashboard flow: Health → Summary → Daily Brief → Recommended Focus → Activity → Performance. The owner reads from top to bottom. Each section prepares them for the next.

**Bad example:** Dashboard requires switching tabs, scrolling left-right, or opening separate pages to complete the check-in flow.

**Validation question:** Can the owner complete their daily check-in by reading the dashboard in a single vertical pass?

---

## SECTION 29 — Detail View Flow

**Principle:** Every detail view must maintain a visible connection to the context that led the owner there.

**Purpose:** When the owner taps into a detail view, they should not feel like they have left the dashboard. The detail view hierarchy must preserve a breadcrumb of context that anchors the owner.

**Psychological reasoning:** Losing navigational context triggers spatial anxiety — the brain's orientation response activates, consuming attention that should be directed to the content. Maintaining context eliminates this anxiety.

**Good example:** The insight detail page shows a small health indicator in the corner and a back link labeled with the previous screen's primary message. The owner never feels lost.

**Bad example:** The insight detail page is a blank slate with no connection to the dashboard. The owner must re-orient.

**Validation question:** Can the owner always trace their path back to their starting point without losing context?

---

## SECTION 30 — Attention Earning

**Principle:** Nothing on any screen is important simply because it exists. Every element must earn the owner's attention.

**Purpose:** Attention is not a right. It is earned through relevance, timing, and trust. The hierarchy must ensure that every element that demands attention can justify its demand.

**Psychological reasoning:** The brain's reticular activating system (RAS) filters approximately 2 million bits of information per second down to the approximately 100 bits that reach conscious awareness. Every element that penetrates this filter must earn its passage.

**Good example:** A recommendation appears only when it has new information and sufficient confidence. The owner knows that if the platform is showing something, it has been earned.

**Bad example:** The platform shows every possible recommendation, even low-confidence ones. The owner learns to ignore them.

**Validation question:** Does every element on this screen deserve the owner's attention right now?

## SPECIAL CHAPTER A — The Emotional Journey

### First 500 Milliseconds

**Emotion:** Relief or concern — resolved within 500ms.

The owner opens the platform. The first thing they see is Restaurant Health. If it is green, they feel relief. If it is red, they feel focused concern. Either way, the uncertainty that preceded opening the platform is resolved within half a second.

The experience is: *"I know where I stand."*

### First 3 Seconds

**Emotion:** Confidence or determination.

After resolving their status, the owner's eye naturally moves to the next element in the hierarchy — the Daily Brief or the primary recommendation. They now know not only their status but what to do about it.

If health is good and the brief is calm, the owner feels confident. They may close the platform.

If health is compromised or the brief has urgency, the owner feels determined. They know their next action.

The experience is: *"I know what to do."*

### First 30 Seconds

**Emotion:** Comprehensive understanding.

The owner has scanned the full vertical flow. They understand health, read the summary, reviewed the brief, and checked activity. They have a complete picture of their restaurant's current state.

If everything is normal, this takes 15-20 seconds. The owner closes the platform feeling informed.

If action is needed, this takes 30 seconds. The owner starts executing the recommended action.

The experience is: *"I understand everything I need to know."*

### First Session

**Emotion:** Optimism with a touch of curiosity.

The owner completes their first full session — exploring recommendations, checking an insight, or starting an experiment. They discover something they did not know about their business.

The combination of understanding their current state and discovering new opportunities creates optimism. The platform has proven its value.

The experience is: *"This platform sees things I did not see."*

### First Week

**Emotion:** Growing trust.

After daily use for a week, the owner has experienced the platform's rhythm. They know when to check it, what to expect, and how to act. The platform has been reliable — no false alarms, no missed alerts.

The owner starts to trust the platform proactively. They rely on it rather than verifying its conclusions.

The experience is: *"I trust what the platform tells me."*

### First Month

**Emotion:** Partnership.

The owner has acted on recommendations, seen results, and observed the platform learn. They have experienced at least one success — a recommendation that led to a measurable improvement.

The relationship shifts from tool to partner. The owner does not just check the platform — they consult it.

The experience is: *"We are working together."*

### First Year

**Emotion:** Dependence without anxiety.

After a year, the platform is integrated into the owner's decision-making. They do not think about using it — they simply use it. The platform has become invisible.

The owner runs their business with less anxiety than before. They take days off without worrying. They trust that the platform is watching.

The experience is: *"My business is under control. I can focus on what matters."*

## SPECIAL CHAPTER B — How Information Earns Attention

### The Five Gates of Attention

Every piece of information on every Restaurant OS screen must pass through five gates before it earns the owner's attention.

### Gate 1 — Relevance

**The question:** Does this information affect a decision the owner needs to make today?

**The test:** If the information would not change the owner's next decision, it does not pass.

**The rule:** Relevance is the first filter. Information that does not affect a decision cannot earn attention.

### Gate 2 — Timeliness

**The question:** Does this information require attention now, or can it wait?

**The test:** If the information would be equally valuable tomorrow, it should be deferred.

**The rule:** Timeliness is the second filter. Information that can wait should not compete for attention now.

### Gate 3 — Confidence

**The question:** Is the platform confident enough in this information to present it?

**The test:** If the platform's confidence in the information is below the threshold for the information's urgency level, it does not pass.

**The rule:** Confidence is the third filter. Low-confidence information does not earn attention.

### Gate 4 — Novelty

**The question:** Is this information genuinely new, or has the owner already seen it?

**The test:** If the owner has already been presented with this information and did not act on it, it must be re-earned with new context.

**The rule:** Novelty is the fourth filter. Repeated information must present new evidence to earn attention again.

### Gate 5 — Actionability

**The question:** Can the owner act on this information?

**The test:** If there is no clear action the owner can take based on this information, it does not pass.

**The rule:** Actionability is the final filter. Information without an associated action is noise.

### The Earned Attention Protocol

Every element in Restaurant OS must document which gates it passes:

```
Element: Health Score
Relevance: ✅ Affects "do I need to act?" decision
Timeliness: ✅ Always timely — primary status signal
Confidence: ✅ High — calculated from verified data
Novelty: ✅ Changes only when state or score shifts
Actionability: ✅ Determines whether to continue or act
```

```
Element: Secondary Metric
Relevance: ⚠️ Supports understanding but does not drive a decision
Timeliness: ✅ Current data
Confidence: ✅ High
Novelty: ❌ Same value as last session — no change
Actionability: ❌ No direct action associated
→ Does not earn primary attention. Belongs in supporting layer.
```

### What This Means

Most information on any screen does not earn attention.

That is by design.

The platform's value is not in how much information it shows — it is in how much information it successfully hides. Every element that passes through the five gates is information the owner can trust. Every element that fails is noise that the platform has filtered.

The hierarchy is not a ranking of information.

It is a filter.

## Closing Manifesto

Restaurant OS does not show information.

It curates attention.

Every screen is an editorial decision — a choice about which information deserves the owner's focus and which information must wait. The hierarchy exists not to organize data, but to protect the owner's most scarce resource: their attention.
